Building Your Medical Career in the UK

The UK offers one of the most structured medical training pathways in the world. From your first day as a Foundation Year 1 doctor through to Consultant status, every step is defined, regulated, and quality-assured by the General Medical Council (GMC). For international medical graduates (IMGs), the journey begins with proving your clinical and English competence through the PLAB exams. This guide maps every stage of your medical career in the UK.

The Complete Medical Pathway

1

GMC Registration and English Proficiency

Your first step is registering with the General Medical Council (GMC). All doctors must be GMC-registered to practise in the UK. International Medical Graduates (IMGs) typically register via the PLAB route or postgraduate qualifications route.

PLAB Route Requirements

  • Primary medical qualification recognised by the GMC
  • 12 months post-registration clinical experience
  • IELTS: Band 7.5 overall (minimum 7.0 in each) OR OET: Grade B in all sub-tests
  • PLAB Part 1: 180 MCQs, sat internationally
  • PLAB Part 2: 16 OSCE stations, sat in the UK only
  • Health and character checks

Alternative Route: Postgraduate Qualifications

  • MRCP (Membership of Royal College of Physicians)
  • MRCS (Membership of Royal College of Surgeons)
  • Other Royal College membership/fellowship exams
  • These can exempt you from PLAB

Important

The GMC now also accepts certain postgraduate qualifications as alternatives to PLAB. Check the GMC website for the most current list of accepted qualifications.

2

PLAB 1: The Knowledge Test

PLAB 1 is a written exam consisting of 180 multiple-choice questions to be answered in 3 hours. It tests your ability to apply clinical knowledge as expected of a UK FY2 doctor. Questions cover medicine, surgery, obstetrics, gynaecology, paediatrics, psychiatry, and common clinical scenarios.

PLAB 1 Overview

  • 180 single-best-answer MCQs
  • 3 hours duration
  • Pass mark: typically 60-65%
  • Held four times per year internationally
  • Cost: GBP 268 (2026)
  • Pass rate: approximately 70%

Preparation: Most IMGs need 3-6 months of dedicated study. Focus on NICE guidelines, BNF prescribing, and UK-specific protocols. Use Anglotec's PLAB question bank for targeted practice.

3

PLAB 2: The Clinical OSCE

PLAB 2 is a 16-station OSCE examination held only in Manchester, UK. It tests your clinical skills, communication, and professionalism. Each station lasts 8 minutes with 90 seconds reading time. The exam covers history taking, examination, practical procedures, communication, and acute management.

PLAB 2 Station Types

  • History taking (3-4 stations)
  • Physical examination (2-3 stations)
  • Practical procedures (2 stations)
  • Communication (3-4 stations)
  • Acute/emergency management (2 stations)
  • Rest stations (2 stations)

PLAB 2 Costs

Exam fee: GBP 905. You must travel to Manchester (flights, accommodation). Most IMGs spend GBP 2,000-3,000 total on their PLAB 2 attempt. Preparing properly first time saves significantly.

Preparation: Allow 8-12 weeks of full-time preparation. Practise 100+ timed stations using AI simulators like Anglotec, join study groups, and consider a PLAB 2 academy course.

4

Foundation Training (FY1 and FY2)

After GMC registration, you enter the UK Foundation Programme, a 2-year structured training programme. FY1 doctors work under supervision, developing core clinical competencies. FY2 doctors take on more responsibility and rotate through different specialties.

Foundation Programme Structure

  • FY1: 4 rotations of 3 months each (medicine, surgery, GP, and one other)
  • FY2: 4 rotations of 3 months each, including at least one community placement
  • Workplace-based assessments (mini-CEX, DOPS, CBD)
  • ePortfolio completion required for ARCP

For IMGs: Some IMGs enter directly into FY2 or CT1 if they have equivalent experience. Apply through the UKFPO and ORIEL recruitment systems.

5

Core Training (CT1-CT3)

After Foundation Training, you enter Core Training in your chosen broad specialty. This provides the foundational training needed before subspecialty training. Core Training lasts 2-3 years depending on the specialty.

Core Medical Training (CMT / IMT)

3 years of internal medicine training. Leads to MRCP and specialty training in medical subspecialties (cardiology, gastroenterology, rheumatology, etc.).

Core Surgical Training (CST)

2 years of surgical training. Leads to MRCS and specialty training in surgical subspecialties (orthopaedics, general surgery, neurosurgery, etc.).

Other Core Training

Psychiatry, paediatrics, obstetrics and gynaecology, emergency medicine, general practice, and pathology each have their own core training programmes.

6

Membership Exams (MRCP / MRCS / MRCPsych etc.)

During Core Training, you must pass your Royal College membership exams. These are challenging examinations that test your specialist knowledge and clinical skills. Passing is mandatory before entering higher specialty training.

MRCP (Membership of Royal College of Physicians)

  • Part 1: 200 MCQs (clinical sciences)
  • Part 2 Written: 200 MCQs (clinical scenarios)
  • Part 2 PACES: 5-station clinical examination
  • Pass rate: approximately 50-60% per attempt

MRCS (Membership of Royal College of Surgeons)

  • Part A: 300 MCQs (applied basic sciences)
  • Part B: 18-station OSCE (clinical and procedural skills)
  • Required for all surgical specialties
7

Specialty Training (ST1-ST8)

Higher Specialty Training is where you become a specialist. Training length varies from 3 years (General Practice) to 8 years (neurosurgery, cardiothoracic surgery). You work in specialist departments, perform procedures, conduct research, and develop expertise in your chosen field.

Popular Training Pathways and Duration

  • General Practice: 3 years after Foundation
  • Internal Medicine: 4-6 years after IMT
  • General Surgery: 6 years after CST
  • Emergency Medicine: 6 years after Foundation
  • Psychiatry: 6 years after Foundation
  • Anaesthetics: 7 years after Foundation
8

Consultant: The Pinnacle of UK Medical Career

Upon completing Specialty Training and gaining your Certificate of Completion of Training (CCT), you are eligible to apply for Consultant posts. Consultants are the most senior doctors in the NHS, leading clinical teams, shaping service delivery, and earning the highest salaries.

Consultant Responsibilities

  • Clinical leadership of a team
  • Making complex diagnostic and treatment decisions
  • Teaching and mentoring junior doctors
  • Clinical governance and quality improvement
  • Research and publication
  • Management and administrative duties
  • On-call for emergencies in your specialty

Salary: GBP 100,000 - 120,000+ (basic). With private practice, Clinical Excellence Awards, and additional NHS work, total earnings can exceed GBP 200,000.

9

Alternative Pathways: SAS and GP

Not all doctors pursue the Consultant pathway. The UK offers excellent alternatives including Specialty and Associate Specialist (SAS) roles and General Practice.

SAS Doctor Pathway

SAS doctors are experienced, senior doctors who do not pursue Consultant training. They provide expert clinical care, often in the same roles as Consultants. New pathways (SAS Charter) offer development opportunities and career progression.

General Practice Pathway

GPs are the backbone of UK primary care. Training is 3 years after Foundation (shorter than most hospital specialties). GP partners can earn GBP 120,000-160,000+ and enjoy regular hours. GP is the largest medical specialty in the UK.

Your Medical Career Timeline

Year 0-1: GMC registration, PLAB 1 & 2

Year 3-5: Core Training + Membership exams
Year 6-12: Specialty Training

Total time from PLAB to Consultant: typically 10-14 years

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the entire UK medical training pathway take?

From Foundation Year 1 to Consultant typically takes 10-14 years: 2 years Foundation, 2-3 years Core Training, 3-8 years Specialty Training. General Practice is shorter at approximately 5 years total after medical school.

Can I work while preparing for PLAB?

Yes, but not as a doctor. Many IMGs work as healthcare assistants or in non-clinical roles while studying. You cannot practise medicine in the UK until GMC-registered.

What is the PLAB 2 pass rate?

The PLAB 2 pass rate is approximately 70-75% on first attempt. Candidates who prepare with structured OSCE practice, including 100+ timed stations, have significantly higher pass rates.

Can IMGs apply directly to specialty training?

In some cases, yes. If you have equivalent qualifications and experience from your home country, you may be eligible for entry at CT1, ST1, or even higher levels. Each case is assessed individually.

What is the difference between PLAB and MRCP?

PLAB demonstrates you meet the standard of a UK FY2 doctor. MRCP is a higher qualification showing specialist physician competence. MRCP can substitute for PLAB for GMC registration.
