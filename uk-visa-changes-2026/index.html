Overview of UK Student Visa Changes 2026

The UK student visa system has undergone significant changes for 2026 as the government continues to refine its approach to international education. These changes affect all prospective international students planning to study in the UK, from those enrolling in foundation programmes to doctoral researchers. Understanding the latest rules is essential for a successful visa application and a smooth transition to student life in the UK.

The UK remains one of the world's most popular study destinations, with over 600,000 international students enrolled in 2025. The government's International Education Strategy aims to grow this number further, but with increased focus on ensuring students are genuine, financially prepared, and studying at high-quality institutions. The 2026 visa changes reflect this balance between welcoming international students and maintaining immigration control.

Key changes for 2026 include updated financial evidence requirements, changes to the Graduate visa route, restrictions on bringing dependents for certain courses, enhanced English language requirements for some institutions, and a new digital visa system that streamlines the application process. This guide provides a comprehensive analysis of each change and practical advice for prospective students.

New UK Student Visa Rules for 2026

The UK government has introduced several new rules affecting the Student Route (formerly Tier 4) visa category for 2026. These rules apply to all new applications and, in some cases, to students extending their existing visas.

Digital Visa Status (eVisa)

The UK has accelerated its transition to digital visa status (eVisa) for 2026. Most new student visa applicants will no longer receive a physical Biometric Residence Permit (BRP). Instead, their visa status is recorded digitally and can be accessed through the UKVI online service. Students must create a UKVI account to view and share their visa status with employers, landlords, and their university. Physical BRPs are being phased out entirely by the end of 2026.

Points-Based Calculator Update

The student visa points-based system has been updated for 2026. Applicants must score 70 points across three categories: Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies (CAS) from an approved provider (50 points), financial requirements (10 points), and English language proficiency (10 points). The points allocation remains the same, but verification procedures have been enhanced.

Genuineness Assessment Enhancement

The genuineness assessment, which evaluates whether applicants are bona fide students, has been enhanced for 2026. Applicants may now be asked about their post-study career plans, knowledge of their chosen course and institution, and financial circumstances during a credibility interview. Preparation for this interview is essential, particularly for students from countries with higher refusal rates.

Remote Study Restrictions

Following the pandemic-era flexibility, rules on remote study have been tightened for 2026. Student visa holders are generally required to attend in-person teaching and are restricted from enrolling in predominantly online courses. Exceptions exist for certain research-based programmes and for students completing dissertations, but the default expectation is physical attendance in the UK.

Updated Financial Requirements 2026

The financial requirements for UK student visas have been updated for 2026, reflecting increases in living costs across the UK.

Maintenance Funds Requirement

For 2026, students must demonstrate access to maintenance funds of £1,483 per month for courses in London (up from £1,334 in 2025) and £1,136 per month for courses outside London (up from £1,023 in 2025). Students must show funds for the duration of their course, up to a maximum of 9 months. This means the maximum requirement is £13,347 for London and £10,224 for outside London.

Funds Evidence Rules

The rules for financial evidence remain broadly unchanged for 2026. Funds must have been held in the applicant's account (or a parent's account) for at least 28 consecutive days before the application date. The bank statement must be no more than 31 days old at the time of application. Acceptable evidence includes bank statements, building society statements, and official financial sponsorship letters.

Student Loan Evidence

A positive 2026 change is the expanded acceptance of student loan evidence. Students using government-backed student loans from their home country can now submit their loan approval letter as financial evidence, even if funds have not yet been disbursed. This change benefits students from countries with established student loan programmes, such as India, Nigeria, and the USA.

Dependents' Financial Requirements

For students bringing dependents, the financial requirement has increased to £845 per month for a partner in London (£680 outside London) and £315 per month for each child in London (£260 outside London). These increases reflect the higher costs of supporting family members in the UK.

Graduate Visa (PSW) Changes 2026

The Graduate visa, which allows students to work in the UK after completing their studies, has undergone changes for 2026 that affect eligibility and duration.

Duration Reduction

The most significant 2026 change is the reduction of the Graduate visa duration for non-doctoral graduates. Bachelor's and master's degree graduates now receive an 18-month Graduate visa (down from 24 months in 2025). PhD and other doctoral graduates continue to receive a 36-month visa. This change aims to ensure the Graduate visa aligns more closely with the original purpose of providing a short-term transition to skilled work.

Job Level Requirements

While the Graduate visa itself does not require sponsorship or a specific job level, the 2026 changes introduce clearer guidance on transitioning to the Skilled Worker visa. Graduates seeking to switch to a sponsored work visa must secure employment at the appropriate skill level with a licensed sponsor. The minimum salary threshold for the Skilled Worker visa has been updated to £38,700 for most occupations, with lower thresholds for new entrants and shortage occupations.

Application Timing

The Graduate visa must be applied for while in the UK on a Student visa. The 2026 guidance clarifies that students must have successfully completed their course before applying, with the university confirming completion to UKVI. Students should apply within six months of their course end date to maintain continuous residence.

Dependent Rules Update 2026

The rules regarding who can bring dependents to the UK have been significantly tightened for 2026.

Course-Level Restrictions

From 2026, only students enrolled in postgraduate research programmes (PhD, MPhil, and other research-based doctorates) can bring dependents. Students on taught master's programmes, bachelor's degrees, and other undergraduate courses are no longer permitted to bring partners or children on their student visa. This change represents a significant restriction compared to previous years.

Existing Dependent Rights

Students who commenced their studies before 2026 and are extending their visas may continue to have their dependents in the UK under transitional arrangements. However, new applications from continuing students are subject to the new rules. Students should check their specific circumstances with their university's international office.

Dependent Work Rights

Dependents of postgraduate research students continue to have unrestricted work rights in the UK. Partners can work in any role at any skill level, providing valuable financial support during the student's studies. Children have access to state schooling and, in some cases, the NHS.

English Language Requirements 2026

English language proficiency remains a core requirement for UK student visas in 2026. The specific requirements depend on the course level and the sponsoring institution.

Minimum CEFR Level

The minimum English language requirement for degree-level courses remains CEFR Level B2 (equivalent to IELTS 5.5 overall with no component below 5.5) for 2026. Pre-sessional courses require at least B1 (IELTS 4.0). Individual universities may set higher requirements, and competitive courses often require IELTS 6.5-7.0.

Accepted English Tests

In addition to IELTS Academic, the UKVI accepts several other English tests for 2026, including PTE Academic, Trinity College London ISE, and LanguageCert. The SELT (Secure English Language Test) requirement applies to most applicants, though nationals of majority English-speaking countries and those who completed degrees in English may be exempt.

University-Specific Requirements

Many UK universities have raised their English language requirements for 2026, particularly for courses with high contact hours such as nursing, medicine, and law. Prospective students should check their chosen institution's specific requirements, which may be higher than the UKVI minimum.

CAS & Application Process 2026

The Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies (CAS) remains central to the student visa application process. Several updates have been introduced for 2026.

CAS Issuance Timeline

Universities can now issue CAS up to six months before the course start date (increased from three months in 2025). This change allows students more time to prepare and submit their visa applications, reducing last-minute stress. However, students cannot apply for their visa more than three months before their course start date.

CAS Content Requirements

CAS statements have been enhanced for 2026 to include additional information about the course delivery method, expected attendance requirements, and any work placement components. This information helps UKVI assess whether the course meets student visa criteria and whether the applicant understands their obligations.

Application Fee

The student visa application fee remains £490 for applications made outside the UK for 2026. The Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) has increased to £776 per year of the visa. Students should budget approximately £1,500-2,000 for visa-related costs including the application fee, IHS, biometric enrolment, and priority services if used.

Work Rights & Restrictions 2026

Student visa work rights have been clarified for 2026, with no major changes to the underlying rules but enhanced enforcement of existing restrictions.

Term-Time Work Limits

Students on degree-level courses can work up to 20 hours per week during term time and full-time during official vacation periods. Students on below-degree-level courses (including foundation programmes) are restricted to 10 hours per week during term time. These limits include both paid and unpaid work.

Prohibited Work Types

Students are prohibited from being self-employed, engaging in business activity, filling a full-time permanent vacancy, or working as a professional sportsperson or entertainer. The 2026 guidance provides clearer examples of what constitutes self-employment, including gig economy work such as freelance delivery driving.

Work Placement Rules

Work placements integrated into courses (such as sandwich year placements or nursing clinical placements) are permitted and do not count towards term-time work limits. The placement must be an assessed part of the course and approved by the university.

Switching Visa Routes 2026

The rules for switching from a Student visa to other immigration routes have been updated for 2026.

Skilled Worker Switch

Students who secure graduate-level employment with a licensed sponsor can switch to the Skilled Worker visa from within the UK. The 2026 updates include a new entrant salary concession that allows recent graduates to be paid 70% of the standard going rate for their occupation. This concession makes it easier for fresh graduates to qualify for sponsorship.

Start-Up & Innovator Routes

Students with entrepreneurial ambitions can switch to the Start-Up or Innovator visa routes. The 2026 changes streamline the endorsement process for graduates of UK universities, with some institutions able to endorse their own graduates directly.

Health Surcharge & Insurance 2026

The Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) has been updated for 2026, and students should understand their healthcare entitlements.

IHS Rate Increase

The IHS for students and their dependents has increased to £776 per year of visa duration (up from £470 in 2025). This surcharge must be paid in full at the time of visa application. For a three-year degree, the IHS cost is approximately £2,328. The IHS provides access to the NHS on the same basis as UK residents.

Healthcare Entitlements

Students who have paid the IHS are entitled to NHS hospital treatment, GP services, and emergency care without additional charges. However, some services such as dental treatment, eye tests, and prescriptions in England require additional payment. Students in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland may have different entitlements for certain services.

Common Rejection Reasons & How to Avoid Them

Understanding common refusal reasons helps you prepare a stronger application.

Financial

Insufficient Financial Evidence

The most common refusal reason is inadequate financial evidence. Ensure funds have been held for 28 days, evidence is clear and complete, and the amount meets the requirement for your circumstances. Include a cover letter explaining your funding sources.

Academic

Academic Qualification Issues

Ensure your previous qualifications are genuine, verifiable, and meet UK entry requirements for your chosen course. Include certified transcripts and explain any gaps in your academic history.

Genuineness

Genuineness Concerns

Be prepared to explain why you chose your specific course and institution, how the course fits your career plans, and your intentions after graduation. Inconsistencies between your application and interview answers can lead to refusal.

Documentation

Document Errors

Incomplete or incorrect documentation is a frequent cause of delays and refusals. Use the UKVI document checklist, ensure all translations are certified, and double-check every detail before submission.

Country-Specific Updates 2026

UKVI has introduced country-specific policies for 2026 that affect students from certain regions.

Indian Students

India remains the largest source of international students to the UK. For 2026, UKVI has introduced streamlined processing for students from Indian institutions with established recruitment partnerships with UK universities. Students from these partner institutions benefit from reduced document requirements and faster processing times.

Nigerian Students

Nigerian students should be aware of enhanced financial verification procedures for 2026. UKVI may conduct additional checks on bank statements and may request supplementary evidence of fund sources. Students should ensure their financial documentation is comprehensive and transparent.

Chinese Students

Chinese students continue to be welcomed to the UK. The 2026 changes include expanded priority visa services in major Chinese cities and enhanced English language preparation partnerships between UK universities and Chinese institutions.

How to Prepare Your 2026 Visa Application

Follow these steps for a successful UK student visa application in 2026:

  1. Secure your university place: Receive and accept an unconditional offer from a licensed student sponsor.
  2. Obtain your CAS: Your university will issue a CAS once you have met all conditions and paid any required deposits.
  3. Prepare financial evidence: Ensure you have the required funds in an acceptable account for at least 28 days before applying.
  4. Take your English test: Schedule and pass an approved English language test if required.
  5. Complete the online application: Fill in the visa application form accurately and pay the application fee and IHS.
  6. Book biometrics: Attend a biometric appointment at a visa application centre.
  7. Submit documents: Upload or submit all required supporting documents.
  8. Attend interview if required: Prepare for a credibility interview if selected.
  9. Receive decision: Standard processing takes approximately 3 weeks; priority services are available for faster decisions.
