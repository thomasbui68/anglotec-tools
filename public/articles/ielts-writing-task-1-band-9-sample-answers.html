What Makes a Band 9 IELTS Writing Task 1 Response?

[Image: IELTS Writing Task 1 Band descriptors comparison chart showing criteria from Band 5 to Band 9]

Achieving Band 9 in IELTS Writing Task 1 requires meeting the highest standards across four equally weighted criteria used by IELTS examiners: Task Achievement, Coherence and Cohesion, Lexical Resource, and Grammatical Range and Accuracy. Understanding exactly what examiners look for is the first step toward producing consistently excellent responses.

Task Achievement (Band 9)

At Band 9, your response must fully satisfy all requirements of the task. This means presenting a clear, well-developed overview that highlights the main trends, differences, or stages depending on the task type. All key features must be covered with appropriate accuracy and detail, while non-essential information is skillfully omitted. The response must be at least 150 words, though Band 9 answers typically range from 160 to 190 words.

Coherence and Cohesion (Band 9)

The information must be organised with clear, skilful progression throughout the response. Paragraphing is logical and purposeful, with a clear introduction, overview, and body paragraphs that group related information effectively. Cohesive devices are used appropriately and naturally, without drawing attention to themselves. Referencing and substitution are used flexibly and accurately.

Lexical Resource (Band 9)

Band 9 responses demonstrate a wide range of vocabulary used with full flexibility and precision. There is sophisticated control of lexical features, including natural collocation, accurate word choice for describing data, and effective use of less common vocabulary. Errors in spelling and word formation are extremely rare, if present at all.

Grammatical Range and Accuracy (Band 9)

A wide range of structures is used with full flexibility and control. Complex sentences are produced with natural ease, including subordinate clauses, participle clauses, passive constructions, and conditional forms. Punctuation and grammar are consistently accurate, with errors being extremely rare and only minor slips.

Essential IELTS Task 1 Vocabulary by Task Type

One of the most significant differences between Band 7 and Band 9 responses is vocabulary sophistication. Examiners are trained to spot repetitive, generic language and reward candidates who demonstrate precise, varied lexical choices. The following vocabulary lists are essential for anyone targeting Band 8 or 9.

Vocabulary for Describing Trends (Line Graphs)

Rose dramatically - sharp upward movement
Plummeted - fell suddenly and steeply
Remained stable - stayed at the same level
Fluctuated wildly - varied irregularly
Reached a peak of - hit the highest point
Hit a low of - reached the minimum
Gradual upward trend - slow increase over time
Levelled off - stopped rising and became stable
Experienced a downturn - began to decrease
Stood at - was at a specific value

Vocabulary for Comparisons (Bar Charts)

By far the highest - significantly more than others
Considerably lower than - much less than
Ranked second at - was in second place
Narrow margin - small difference
Outnumbered - was greater in number
Accounted for - represented a proportion
A noticeable disparity - a clear difference
At the opposite end - on the other extreme

Vocabulary for Proportions (Pie Charts)

Constituted - made up a portion
Represented a quarter of - was 25%
Comprised the smallest share - was the least
The lion's share - the largest portion
At 35% - specifying exact proportions
Collectively - taken together

Band 9 Sample Answer 1: Line Graph

[Image: Sample IELTS line graph showing monthly rainfall and temperature data for two cities over a 12-month period]

The Task

The line graph compares the average monthly rainfall and temperature in two cities, London and Rome, over the course of a year. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

Band 9 Model Answer (~175 words)

The line graph illustrates how monthly rainfall levels and average temperatures in London and Rome fluctuated over a twelve-month period. Overall, it is evident that Rome experienced considerably warmer conditions throughout the year, whereas London received a greater amount of precipitation in every single month. Both cities saw their temperature patterns follow a broadly similar trajectory, rising in the first half of the year before declining. Looking at temperature first, Rome began the year at 8C, a figure which climbed steadily to reach a peak of 25C in July and August, subsequently falling back to 9C by December. London, by contrast, recorded significantly cooler temperatures, starting at just 4C in January and reaching a maximum of only 18C during the summer months. With regard to rainfall, London consistently outpaced Rome, with precipitation figures ranging from 45mm in April to 80mm in the autumn months. Rome's rainfall was more variable, fluctuating between approximately 20mm in July and 65mm in November, with a particularly sharp decline during the summer period.

Why This Is Band 9:

  • Clear overview in paragraph 2: Identifies the two main trends (Rome warmer, London wetter) without repeating specific data points
  • Logical paragraphing: Introduction → Overview → Temperature detail → Rainfall detail
  • Sophisticated comparisons: "Whereas," "by contrast," "consistently outpaced"
  • Wide vocabulary range: fluctuated, trajectory, outpaced, precipitation, subsequently
  • Complex grammar: "a figure which climbed steadily to reach a peak of," "with a particularly sharp decline"
  • No errors: Zero grammatical or lexical mistakes
  • Appropriate data selection: Key figures only, no listing of every point

Band 9 Sample Answer 2: Bar Chart

[Image: Sample IELTS bar chart comparing internet usage percentages across five age groups in three different countries]

The Task

The bar chart shows the percentage of people in five different age groups who used the Internet in three countries (the UK, the USA, and Australia) in 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

Band 9 Model Answer (~185 words)

The bar chart compares Internet usage rates across five age categories in the UK, the USA, and Australia during the year 2020. Overall, Internet penetration was consistently highest among younger demographics across all three nations, with a clear downward trend observable as age increased. Notably, the USA recorded the greatest overall usage, while Australia generally reported the lowest figures. In the two youngest age groups (18-25 and 26-35), Internet usage was near-universal, with the USA leading at 98% and 95% respectively, followed closely by the UK at 96% and 93%. Australia's figures were marginally lower at 94% and 90%. Among 36-50-year-olds, a similar pattern prevailed, with the USA maintaining its lead at 88%, compared to 85% in the UK and 79% in Australia. The most significant disparities emerged in the older cohorts. Whereas 76% of Americans aged 51-65 used the Internet, the equivalent figures for the UK and Australia stood at 70% and 62% respectively. The gap widened further among those over 65: 60% in the USA, compared to just 52% in the UK and 40% in Australia.

Why This Is Band 9:

  • Strong overview: Two clear overall trends identified without data dumping
  • Excellent grouping: Younger groups combined, older groups separated for contrast
  • Variety of comparison language: "followed closely by," "marginally lower," "compared to," "the equivalent figures"
  • Range of sentence structures: "Whereas 76%...the equivalent figures...stood at," "a similar pattern prevailed"
  • Academic register: "penetration," "demographics," "cohorts," "downward trend"
  • Accurate data reporting: All figures precise, no confusion between categories
  • Natural flow: Ideas connect seamlessly without forced transitions

Band 9 Sample Answer 3: Pie Chart

[Image: Sample IELTS pie chart showing household expenditure breakdown across five categories for a country in two different years, 2000 and 2020]

The Task

The pie charts show the proportion of household expenditure on different categories in one country in 2000 and 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

Band 9 Model Answer (~180 words)

The two pie charts illustrate how household spending was distributed across five categories in a particular country during the years 2000 and 2020. Overall, housing remained the largest expenditure category throughout the period, while clothing and entertainment accounted for the smallest shares. The most striking change was the considerable increase in spending on housing and healthcare, whereas expenditure on food and clothing decreased substantially. Housing costs represented the single greatest outlay in both years, rising from 25% in 2000 to 32% in 2020. Healthcare expenditure also saw a notable increase, climbing from 8% to 14% over the same period. Conversely, food dropped from being the second-largest category at 22% to just 15% in 2020. Spending on clothing experienced the most dramatic proportional decline, falling from 18% to a mere 8%. Entertainment expenditure, by contrast, remained relatively stable at 12% in 2000 and 11% in 2020, making it the only category to maintain virtually the same share of household budgets across the two years examined.

Why This Is Band 9:

  • Clear overview with key changes highlighted: Identifies what increased, what decreased
  • Natural use of passive structures: "was distributed," "saw a notable increase," "experienced the most dramatic decline"
  • Effective contrast transitions: "Conversely," "by contrast," "whereas"
  • Precise proportion language: "represented," "accounted for," "maintained virtually the same share"
  • Strong concluding sentence: Summarises the entertainment category while connecting back to the overall comparison
  • No repetition: Each percentage point is introduced with varied phrasing
  • Exactly 4 paragraphs: The optimal structure for Task 1

Common Mistakes That Prevent Band 9 in Task 1

Even strong candidates frequently make errors that cap their score at Band 6.5 or 7. Understanding these pitfalls can help you avoid them systematically in your preparation.

1. Missing or Vague Overview

  • The problem: Many candidates jump straight into details without summarising the main trends or features.
  • Why it hurts: Task Achievement criteria heavily penalise responses without a clear overview. This single error can reduce your TA score by 1-2 bands.
  • The fix: Always write your overview as the second paragraph. Ask yourself: "What are the 2-3 most important things this chart shows?"

2. Listing Data Without Analysis

  • The problem: Writing "A was 20%, B was 30%, C was 15%..." reads like a list, not an analysis.
  • Why it hurts: Coherence and Cohesion suffer when data is not grouped logically. It also limits your vocabulary score.
  • The fix: Group related data points together and use comparison language: "While A stood at just 20%, B was considerably higher at 30%."

3. Repetitive Vocabulary

  • The problem: Using "increased" and "decreased" repeatedly throughout the response.
  • Why it hurts: Lexical Resource criteria reward variety. Repetitive vocabulary caps you at Band 6-7.
  • The fix: Use the vocabulary lists in this article. Aim for at least 6-8 different verbs/phrases for trends.

4. Grammar Errors in Complex Sentences

  • The problem: Attempting complex structures but making errors with clauses, articles, or prepositions.
  • Why it hurts: Grammatical errors reduce your GRA score and can affect overall impression.
  • The fix: Practise "which" clauses and participle phrases until they are automatic. Accuracy always beats complexity.

5. Including an Opinion or Conclusion

  • The problem: Adding phrases like "This is good for the economy" or "I think this trend will continue."
  • Why it hurts: Task 1 is objective data description. Opinions and predictions are not required and may reduce your TA score.
  • The fix: Stick to describing what the data shows. Never interpret, evaluate, or predict.

Expert Tips for Achieving Band 9 in Writing Task 1

Structure Is Everything

Follow the 4-paragraph structure religiously: (1) Introduction (paraphrase), (2) Overview (2 main trends), (3) Body Paragraph 1 (detail group A), (4) Body Paragraph 2 (detail group B). This structure has been proven to produce Band 8-9 responses consistently.

Spend 3-4 Minutes Planning

Before writing, identify: the main trends, the highest and lowest values, any significant changes, and the best way to group the data. A brief plan prevents mid-essay restructuring and improves coherence dramatically.

Master the Art of Paraphrasing

The introduction must paraphrase the task prompt, not copy it. Practise replacing verbs (shows → illustrates, compares → contrasts), nouns (percentage → proportion, number → figure), and sentence structure.

Study Real Band 9 Responses Extensively

Read at least 20-30 model answers before your test. Notice how they select data, use comparison language, and structure paragraphs. Internalise the patterns rather than memorising phrases.

Practise Under Timed Conditions

You have 20 minutes for Task 1. Practise with a timer and aim to complete the response in 18 minutes, leaving 2 minutes for checking. Use our free IELTS practice tools to simulate exam conditions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes a Writing Task 1 answer Band 9?

A Band 9 Task 1 response meets all criteria at the highest level: it fully addresses all parts of the task with a clear overview and well-developed key features, uses a wide range of vocabulary with natural collocation and precise meaning, demonstrates sophisticated grammatical structures with full flexibility and accuracy, and presents information with clear progression and skilful paragraphing.

How long should IELTS Writing Task 1 be?

You should write at least 150 words. Band 8-9 responses are typically 160-190 words. Writing significantly more than 200 words may reduce the time available for Task 2, which carries twice the marks.

Do I need to include all data points in Task 1?

No. Band 9 answers select and group key features rather than listing every data point. The skill is in identifying trends, comparisons, and significant features, not in describing every number on the chart.

Can I use the same words as the question in my introduction?

You should paraphrase the question rather than copying it. Change the vocabulary and sentence structure while keeping the same meaning. This demonstrates lexical flexibility, which examiners reward.

Should I write a conclusion in Task 1?

No. Task 1 does not require a conclusion or any opinion. End with your final body paragraph after covering all key features. A conclusion can actually reduce your score if it includes interpretation or prediction.
