OET vs IELTS: At a Glance

[Image: Side-by-side comparison infographic showing OET and IELTS logos with key statistics: test duration, number of sections, cost, and average pass rates for nurses]

If you are a nurse seeking to work in the UK, Australia, Ireland, or another English-speaking country, you will need to demonstrate your English language proficiency through an approved test. The two main options accepted by nursing regulators are the Occupational English Test (OET) and the International English Language Testing System (IELTS). Both tests are accepted by the Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC) in the UK, but they differ significantly in format, content, and approach.

Choosing the right test can save you months of preparation time, hundreds of pounds in test fees, and considerable stress. This comprehensive comparison will help you make an informed decision based on your individual strengths, career goals, and circumstances.

The Key Difference

IELTS is a general academic English test used for university admissions, visa applications, and professional registration across many fields.

OET is a profession-specific English test designed exclusively for healthcare professionals, using real healthcare scenarios and vocabulary.

Format Comparison

OET Format

Listening (45 min): Part A: consultation extracts. Part B: short workplace extracts. Part C: presentation extracts. All healthcare contexts.

Reading (60 min): Part A: rapid reading of 4 short texts. Part B: 6 short workplace texts. Part C: 2 longer presentation texts. All healthcare topics.

Writing (45 min): One profession-specific task. For nurses: a referral letter, discharge letter, or transfer letter based on case notes.

Speaking (20 min): Two role-play scenarios. You play the role of a nurse interacting with a patient, carer, or family member.

IELTS Academic Format

Listening (30 min): 4 sections: everyday conversation, monologue, academic conversation, academic monologue. General topics.

Reading (60 min): 3 long academic passages on general science, social, and academic topics. Not healthcare-specific.

Writing (60 min): Task 1: Describe a graph, chart, or diagram (150 words). Task 2: Write an opinion essay on a general topic (250 words).

Speaking (11-14 min): Part 1: General questions about yourself. Part 2: Talk for 2 minutes on a given topic. Part 3: Abstract discussion.

Format Verdict for Nurses

OET's format is generally more intuitive for nurses. The Writing task (a letter based on case notes) is a task you may have done in your nursing practice. The Speaking role-plays mirror real nurse-patient interactions. The Reading and Listening sections use healthcare vocabulary you already know. IELTS, by contrast, requires you to describe graphs in writing and discuss abstract topics in speaking - skills that most nurses have not practised extensively.

The key difference in scoring is that OET does not require an overall grade - you simply need to achieve the minimum grade in each section. IELTS requires an overall Band 7.0 in addition to the individual section minimums. This means that with OET, a strong performance in three sections cannot be undermined by a weak overall average, whereas with IELTS it can.

OET Score Combining

Both OET and IELTS allow you to combine two test scores. For OET, you can combine scores from two sittings taken within 6 months, provided you achieve at least Grade C+ in all sections across both tests and no single section is below Grade C+. For IELTS, you can combine two test scores under the same conditions, with no single section below Band 6.5 across both tests.

IELTS is accepted by more organisations overall because it is a general-purpose academic test. If you plan to pursue further education (e.g., a Master's degree) alongside your nursing career, IELTS may be the better choice because it is accepted by virtually all universities. OET is accepted by healthcare regulators and employers but is not typically accepted for academic admissions.

Difficulty Analysis for Nurses

Listening Section

Most nurses find OET Listening easier because the contexts are healthcare-related and use vocabulary they are familiar with. The accents in OET are also primarily medical professionals speaking clearly. IELTS Listening uses a wider variety of accents (British, Australian, American, New Zealand) and topics that may be unfamiliar to healthcare professionals.

Reading Section

This is often the most challenging section for nurses in both tests. However, OET Reading Part A (rapid reading of 4 short healthcare texts) is generally considered easier than IELTS Reading passages, which can be dense academic texts on any topic. OET Reading Parts B and C use workplace and presentation texts that nurses encounter professionally.

Writing Section

OET Writing is significantly easier for most nurses. Writing a referral or discharge letter based on case notes is a task that uses vocabulary and formats nurses are familiar with. IELTS Task 1 (graph description) and Task 2 (opinion essay) require skills that most nurses have not practised since their school days.

Speaking Section

Opinions are divided on this section. Some nurses find OET Speaking easier because the role-plays simulate real workplace interactions. Others find IELTS Speaking easier because Part 1 questions about familiar topics (hometown, hobbies, work) are less pressured than clinical role-plays. The OET Speaking criteria, however, are designed for healthcare communication, which works in nurses' favour.

Scenario-Based Recommendations

Scenario 1: Nurse Applying for NMC Registration Only

Recommendation: OET. You only need a test accepted by the NMC, and OET's healthcare-specific content aligns perfectly with your professional background. The shorter preparation time and intuitive format make this the most efficient choice.

Scenario 2: Nurse Who Also Plans to Do a Master's Degree

Recommendation: IELTS. Universities generally require IELTS Academic for admission. Taking IELTS covers both your NMC registration and university requirements in one test.

Scenario 3: Nurse on a Tight Budget

Recommendation: IELTS if you are confident in your academic English. The lower test fee (GBP 185 vs GBP 587) makes it more affordable. However, if you think you might need multiple attempts, the higher pass rate for nurses on OET may make it more cost-effective in the long run.

Scenario 4: Nurse with Limited Study Time

Recommendation: OET. Nurses typically need 4-6 weeks to prepare for OET versus 6-10 weeks for IELTS. The healthcare-specific content means less time learning new vocabulary and more time practising test technique.

Scenario 5: Nurse Applying to Multiple Countries

Recommendation: IELTS if one of those countries only accepts IELTS. Check the requirements of each country's nursing regulator before deciding. If all countries accept both, choose based on your strengths.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which is easier for nurses: OET or IELTS?

Most nurses find OET easier because it uses healthcare-specific vocabulary and scenarios they are already familiar with from their professional training. The OET Writing task (a referral letter) is often more intuitive for nurses than IELTS Academic Writing (graph description and opinion essay). However, the Speaking and Listening sections of IELTS are sometimes considered easier because the topics are more general.

Do all UK employers accept OET?

Yes, the Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC) accepts both OET and IELTS for registration. All NHS employers recognise both tests. However, some university courses may specifically require IELTS, so if you are also considering further education, check the specific requirements of your chosen programme.

Can I switch from IELTS to OET (or vice versa) if I keep failing?

Yes, absolutely. Many nurses who struggle with IELTS switch to OET and pass on their first or second attempt. The key is to honestly assess which sections are causing you problems. If Writing and Reading are your weak points, OET may be the better choice. If Speaking is your main challenge, both tests require strong speaking skills.

Does OET have a higher pass rate for nurses than IELTS?

While official pass rates are not published, anecdotal evidence and preparation course data suggest that nurses generally have a higher pass rate on OET, particularly because the Writing and Reading sections use healthcare contexts. However, individual results vary significantly based on English proficiency and preparation quality.
