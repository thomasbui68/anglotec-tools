IELTS Academic vs General Training: Overview

The International English Language Testing System (IELTS) offers two versions of its test: Academic and General Training. Both tests assess your English proficiency across four skills — Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking — but they differ in purpose, content, difficulty, and scoring.

Choosing the wrong test can cost you time, money, and delay your plans. This guide will help you understand the differences and make the right choice for your specific situation — whether that's university admission, professional registration, immigration, or employment.

What's the Same: Listening and Speaking

The Listening and Speaking sections are identical in both IELTS Academic and IELTS General Training. This means that regardless of which test you choose, you'll face the same listening recordings and the same speaking interview format.

Listening (30 minutes + 10 minutes transfer)

Both tests feature 4 sections with 40 questions total. The recordings are the same, the question types are the same, and the timing is the same. Sections progress from everyday social contexts (Sections 1-2) to academic/educational contexts (Sections 3-4).

Speaking (11-14 minutes)

Both tests use the same three-part structure: Part 1 (introduction and interview, 4-5 minutes), Part 2 (individual long turn/cue card, 3-4 minutes), and Part 3 (two-way discussion, 4-5 minutes). The topics, assessment criteria, and examiner marking are identical.

Time-Saver: Since Listening and Speaking are identical, any preparation you do for these sections applies to both tests. If you're unsure which test to take, you can start preparing Listening and Speaking immediately while you decide.

Key Differences: Reading and Writing

The Reading and Writing sections differ between the two tests. These differences reflect the different purposes of the tests — Academic for higher education and professional contexts, General Training for everyday work and social contexts.

Reading Section: Academic vs General Training in Depth

While both Reading sections have 40 questions and 60 minutes, the content and scoring differ significantly.

Academic Reading

Three long texts (approximately 2,150-2,750 words total) taken from books, journals, magazines, and newspapers. Texts are written for a non-specialist audience but use academic language and concepts. Topics include: environmental science, psychology, education, history, technology, and medicine.

The texts progress in difficulty — Passage 1 is the easiest, Passage 3 the most challenging. Question types include multiple choice, True/False/Not Given, matching headings, sentence completion, and summary completion.

General Training Reading

Section 1 contains two or three short factual texts (notices, advertisements, timetables). Section 2 contains two short work-related texts (job descriptions, training materials, workplace policies). Section 3 contains one longer text on a general interest topic.

General Training texts are more accessible but the scoring is adjusted — you need more correct answers for the same band score.

Writing Section: Academic vs General Training in Depth

Academic Writing Task 1

You are given visual information (a graph, chart, table, diagram, or process) and must write a summary of at least 150 words. You should describe the main features, make comparisons where relevant, and not include your own opinions.

Common task types: line graphs, bar charts, pie charts, tables, maps, processes/diagrams. You need to practise describing trends, making comparisons, and using appropriate academic vocabulary for data description.

General Training Writing Task 1

You are given a situation and must write a letter of at least 150 words. The letter may be formal, semi-formal, or informal. Common scenarios include: making a complaint, requesting information, explaining a situation, inviting someone, or thanking someone.

This task tests your ability to use appropriate tone and register for different contexts — a skill essential for everyday life and work in an English-speaking country.

Writing Task 2 (Same for Both)

Both tests require a 250-word essay in response to a point of view, argument, or problem. Topics for Academic tend to be more abstract and intellectual. Topics for General Training tend to be more practical and everyday. The assessment criteria are the same.

Which IELTS Should You Take? Decision Guide

Take IELTS Academic If:

  • You're applying to a university degree programme (undergraduate or postgraduate)
  • You need professional registration (NMC, GMC, GPhC, HCPC)
  • You're applying for a Tier 4 Student Visa for degree-level study
  • You want to demonstrate academic English proficiency
  • You're an international medical graduate applying for PLAB, MRCP, or similar
  • You're a nurse applying for NMC registration

Take IELTS General Training If:

  • You're applying for immigration to the UK, Australia, Canada, or New Zealand
  • You're applying for work experience or training programmes (non-degree)
  • You need the test for secondary education qualification
  • You're applying for certain visa categories that specifically require General Training

When in Doubt

Always check the specific requirements of the organisation you're applying to. Contact them directly if the requirements are unclear. Booking the wrong test and having to retake costs approximately £185-£200 and wastes valuable time.

Who Accepts Which IELTS Test?

Organisations Accepting IELTS Academic Only

  • UK universities (degree programmes)
  • Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC)
  • General Medical Council (GMC)
  • General Pharmaceutical Council (GPhC)
  • Health and Care Professions Council (HCPC)
  • Most medical royal colleges

Organisations Accepting IELTS General Training

  • UK Visas and Immigration (for certain visa categories)
  • Australian Department of Home Affairs
  • Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC)
  • New Zealand Immigration
  • Some employers (non-professional roles)

Organisations Accepting Either

  • Some Australian professional registration bodies
  • Certain employers (always check first)

Preparation Differences: What Changes Based on Your Choice

Since Listening and Speaking are identical, your preparation for these sections is the same regardless of which test you choose. The differences are in Reading and Writing preparation.

If You're Taking Academic

  • Read academic articles, journals, and quality newspapers (The Guardian, The Economist, New Scientist)
  • Practise describing graphs, charts, and data in writing
  • Build academic vocabulary (Academic Word List)
  • Focus on interpreting visual data quickly and accurately

If You're Taking General Training

  • Read everyday materials: notices, advertisements, instruction manuals
  • Practise writing formal, semi-formal, and informal letters
  • Build general and workplace vocabulary
  • Focus on appropriate tone and register for different contexts

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I switch from Academic to General Training after booking?

No, these are separate tests with separate bookings. You would need to cancel your original booking (subject to cancellation policies) and make a new booking for the other test type.

Is Academic harder than General Training?

Academic has more challenging reading texts and a different writing task. However, General Training requires more correct answers for the same band score. Most candidates find the difficulty roughly equivalent when accounting for scoring adjustments.

Can I use General Training for university applications?

Almost all universities require Academic IELTS. Do not take General Training if you need the test for university admission — your results will not be accepted.

Does the NMC accept General Training?

No. The Nursing and Midwifery Council only accepts IELTS Academic (or OET). They require a minimum score of 7.0 in all four sections, with an overall band of at least 7.0.

Can I combine Academic and General Training scores?

No. You must take the same type of test for all four sections. You cannot mix Academic and General Training scores, nor can you combine scores from different test sittings for most purposes.

Should I take IELTS or OET for healthcare registration?

OET is specifically designed for healthcare professionals and uses medical scenarios. Many healthcare professionals find OET more relevant and achievable. Both are accepted by the NMC and GMC. Consider your strengths: if you prefer medical contexts, OET may be better. If you prefer general topics, choose IELTS Academic.

How long are IELTS results valid?

IELTS results are valid for 2 years from the test date. Most organisations (universities, registration bodies, immigration departments) apply this 2-year validity period.
