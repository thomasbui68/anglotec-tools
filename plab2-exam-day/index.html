What Is PLAB 2 & What to Expect

PLAB 2 (Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board) is an objective structured clinical examination (OSCE) that tests your ability to apply medical knowledge and clinical skills in a UK healthcare setting. It's the final hurdle before GMC registration.

PLAB 2 Format Overview

PLAB 2 consists of 16 clinical stations, each lasting 8 minutes (plus 2 minutes reading time before entering the station). You'll rotate through all 16 stations with 90-second breaks between them. The entire exam takes approximately 3 hours and 20 minutes. Each station presents a realistic clinical scenario that tests your clinical examination, history taking, communication, and practical skills.

Before

Arrival & Registration (30 min before)

  • Arrive at the GMC Assessment Centre in Manchester
  • Present your valid passport at registration

During

  • 2-minute reading time outside each station
  • 8-minute interaction time inside each station
  • 90-second break between stations
  • Two rest stations included (no interaction required)
  • Examiners observe and score your performance
  • Actors (simulated patients) play patient roles
After

Post-Exam (Same Day)

  • Collect your belongings from the locker
  • Receive provisional results the SAME DAY
  • Formal results emailed within 2 weeks
  • If you pass: apply for GMC registration
  • If you don't pass: you can rebook after 2+ months
  • Take time to rest and recover

Important Notes

Mobile phones must be switched off and stored in your locker. You are NOT allowed to wear a white coat or carry a stethoscope — all equipment is provided at each station. Do NOT bring notes, books, or study materials into the exam centre. Arrive well-rested and hydrated.

Understanding the 16 PLAB 2 Stations

Each station tests a different clinical skill. Understanding what each station involves helps you prepare mentally and strategically.

8 MINUTES

History Taking Stations

You'll take a focused history from a simulated patient presenting with a specific complaint. Common scenarios include chest pain, abdominal pain, headache, shortness of breath, joint pain, and mental health concerns.

Key skills tested: Structured history taking, rapport building, red flag identification, safety netting, explanation and planning.

8 MINUTES

Physical Examination Stations

You'll perform a focused physical examination on a simulated patient or manikin. Common examinations include cardiovascular, respiratory, abdominal, neurological, musculoskeletal, and thyroid examinations.

Key skills tested: Correct technique, systematic approach, patient comfort, explanation of findings, professionalism.

8 MINUTES

Practical Procedure Stations

You'll demonstrate a clinical procedure. Common procedures include venepuncture, cannulation, catheterisation, suturing, drug administration, and basic life support (BLS).

Key skills tested: Aseptic technique, informed consent, correct procedure steps, patient safety, post-procedure care.

8 MINUTES

Communication Stations

You'll handle a scenario requiring sensitive communication. Common scenarios include breaking bad news, dealing with an angry relative, explaining a medical error, discussing end-of-life care, and obtaining informed consent.

Key skills tested: Empathy, active listening, clear explanation, shared decision-making, emotional intelligence.

8 MINUTES

Prescribing Stations

You'll be asked to write a prescription or review a drug chart. You'll need to demonstrate knowledge of common drug doses, contraindications, interactions, and monitoring requirements.

Key skills tested: Drug knowledge, safe prescribing, dose calculations, awareness of allergies and interactions.

8 MINUTES

Emergency Management Stations

You'll manage an acutely unwell patient. Scenarios include anaphylaxis, asthma attack, myocardial infarction, seizures, and acute confusion. You'll need to demonstrate ABC (Airway, Breathing, Circulation) approach.

Key skills tested: Prioritisation, emergency protocols, team communication, rapid assessment, calm under pressure.

Proven Strategies to Pass PLAB 2

These strategies, used by successful candidates, will help you maximise your score across all 16 stations.

Strategy 1

Master the 2-Minute Reading Time

The 2 minutes before entering each station are crucial. Read the instructions carefully. Identify: (1) Your role, (2) The patient's situation, (3) What you need to accomplish. Plan your approach mentally. For history stations, plan your opening questions. For examination stations, mentally run through your systematic approach. This planning time separates pass from fail.

Strategy 2

Structure Every Interaction

Examiners score structured responses higher. For history: introduction, presenting complaint, history of complaint, systems review, past history, drugs/allergies, family history, social history, ICE (ideas, concerns, expectations), closing. For examinations: consent, positioning, exposure, systematic examination, thank patient, cover and restore dignity.

Strategy 3

Show Empathy & Professionalism

PLAB 2 is not just about medical knowledge — it's about how you apply it as a UK doctor. Introduce yourself with your name and role. Explain what you're doing. Seek consent. Maintain eye contact. Show genuine concern. These "soft skills" carry significant marks and often make the difference between pass and fail.

Strategy 4

Use the ICE Framework

Always explore the patient's Ideas (what they think is wrong), Concerns (what they're worried about), and Expectations (what they want from you). This patient-centred approach is central to UK medical practice and scores highly in communication stations.

Strategy 5

Practice With a Timer Religiously

8 minutes is shorter than you think. Practice EVERY scenario with a strict 8-minute timer. Learn to gauge your pacing: for history stations, cover the key points in 5-6 minutes, leaving 2-3 minutes for explanation and planning. Running out of time before explaining your management plan costs significant marks.

Strategy 6

Safety Net Every Station

Always end with safety netting: tell the patient what to do if symptoms worsen, when to seek help, and arrange appropriate follow-up. This demonstrates safe medical practice and scores marks in virtually every station type. Never discharge a patient without follow-up plans.

The Night Before & Morning of PLAB 2

Your final 24 hours are about mental preparation, physical readiness, and confidence — not last-minute cramming.

Night Before

Evening Routine

  • Stop studying by 4 PM — seriously, no more
  • Review a checklist of examination structures only
  • Pack all documents and items for the morning
  • Set multiple alarms
  • Eat a light, familiar dinner
  • Do 10 minutes of light exercise or stretching
  • Aim for 8+ hours of sleep
  • Visualise yourself succeeding at each station
Morning Of

Pre-Exam Routine

  • Wake with plenty of time — no rushing
  • Eat a protein-rich breakfast (eggs, oats, fruit)
  • Hydrate well but don't overdo it
  • Use the bathroom before entering
  • Arrive 30-45 minutes early
  • Stay calm — deep breathing exercises
  • Remind yourself: you ARE ready for this
  • Trust your months of preparation

Managing Between Stations

The 90-second break between stations is for mental reset, not reflection. Do NOT analyse your previous station — what's done is done. Use the break to: take a sip of water, take 3 deep breaths, mentally clear your mind, and read the next station's instructions with fresh focus. Each station is an independent assessment — a poor performance in one does NOT affect your score in others.

Frequently Asked Questions

Where is the PLAB 2 exam held?

PLAB 2 is held at the GMC Clinical Assessment Centre in Manchester, UK. There is only one test centre, so all international doctors travel to Manchester for the exam. Plan your travel and accommodation well in advance.

How many times can I take PLAB 2?

You can take PLAB 2 a maximum of 4 times. After your 4th attempt, you must wait 12 months before being eligible to take it again. This makes thorough preparation essential — treat each attempt as if it's your last chance.

What is the passing score for PLAB 2?

There is no fixed passing score. The GMC uses a process called "borderline regression" which takes into account the difficulty of each station. Generally, you need to pass approximately 10-11 out of 16 stations to achieve an overall pass. Focus on performing consistently well across all stations rather than excelling in some and failing others.

How long should I prepare for PLAB 2?

Most successful candidates prepare for 3-6 months. Preparation typically involves: attending a preparation course, practising with study partners, reviewing clinical guidelines (NICE, BNF), and practising scenarios under timed conditions. The depth of preparation depends on your clinical experience and familiarity with UK medical practice.

What happens after I pass PLAB 2?

Congratulations! After passing PLAB 2, you can apply for full GMC registration. You'll need to provide evidence of your medical degree, internship, English language proficiency (IELTS/OET), and PLAB 1 and 2 passes. Once registered, you can apply for jobs in the NHS and begin your UK medical career.

Can I use a calculator in PLAB 2?

No calculators are permitted. Any calculations required (drug doses, fluid rates) must be done mentally or on paper. Practice common medical calculations during your preparation: paediatric drug doses, IV fluid rates, unit conversions. These appear in prescribing and emergency management stations.

How soon do I get my results?

You receive provisional results on the same day as your exam — usually within 1-2 hours of completing all stations. Formal results are emailed to you within 2 weeks and posted to your GMC online account. The provisional result is almost always the same as the formal result.
