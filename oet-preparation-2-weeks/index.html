Why OET Is Different From IELTS

OET uses healthcare-specific scenarios that nurses encounter daily. This is your advantage — you already know the content. You just need to express it in flawless, professional English.

The OET Advantage for Nurses

Unlike IELTS which tests general English, OET uses real nursing scenarios: referral letters, patient handovers, discharge summaries, and ward consultations. You already understand the medical content. Your challenge is professional English expression — and that's exactly what this 2-week plan fixes.

Week 1: Master the Format (Days 1-7)

Day 1

OET Format & Diagnostic

  • Understand the 4 OET sub-tests thoroughly
  • Take a diagnostic practice test
  • Score each section: Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking
  • Identify your 2 weakest areas for priority focus
  • Review OET scoring criteria for each sub-test
  • Time: 5 hours
Day 2

OET Writing: The Referral Letter

  • Learn the 6-paragraph letter structure
  • Study the case notes format and marking criteria
  • Write your first referral letter (180-200 words)
  • Focus on: purpose line, key details, professional tone
  • Learn medical abbreviation rules (no abbreviations!)
  • Analyse 3 sample Grade B letters
Day 3

OET Writing: Practice & Polish

  • Write 2 full referral letters under timed conditions (45 min each)
  • Practice transforming case notes into prose
  • Learn to omit irrelevant case notes
  • Master the opening sentence: "I am writing to refer..."
  • Get feedback on your letters if possible
  • Build your medical phrase bank
Day 4

OET Speaking: The Nursing Consultation

  • Understand the 2-roleplay card format (3 minutes each)
  • Practice 5 roleplay scenarios with a partner or mirror
  • Warm-up: answer 2 personal questions fluently
  • Learn the rapport-building opening phrases
  • Practice explaining medical terms in plain English
  • Record yourself; listen for clarity and tone
Day 5

OET Speaking: Intensive Practice

  • Complete 8 roleplay scenarios today
  • Focus on: empathy, clarity, checking understanding
  • Practice common scenarios: pain management, medication, discharge
  • Use phrases: "Can you tell me more about...?" "I understand that..."
  • Listen to recordings; improve weak areas
  • Build confidence with repetition
Day 6

Listening & Reading Focus

  • Complete 2 full Listening tests (Part A: consultation, Part B: monologues)
  • Complete 2 full Reading tests (Part A: skim & scan, Part B & C: detailed)
  • Practice note-taking during Listening Part A
  • Learn to identify distractors in multiple-choice questions
  • Review all wrong answers in detail
  • Build healthcare vocabulary list (50 words)
Day 7

Full Mock Test & Review

  • Complete a FULL mock test under timed conditions (3 hours)
  • Score every section against OET criteria
  • Review all errors; create a "mistake log"
  • Add 50 new healthcare vocabulary words
  • Rest in the evening — sleep is preparation too

Week 2: Exam-Ready Performance (Days 8-14)

Day 8

Writing Intensive

  • Write 3 referral letters (45 minutes each)
  • Practice different letter types: referral, discharge, transfer
  • Focus on: appropriate opening and closing lines
  • Check word count: must be 180-200 words
  • Proofread each letter for 5 minutes
  • Review case note selection (include only relevant notes)
Day 9

Speaking Intensive

  • Complete 10 roleplay scenarios
  • Practice ALL common nursing topics
  • Focus on: asking open questions, showing empathy
  • Time yourself: 3 minutes per roleplay exactly
  • Record and self-assess using OET criteria
  • Build a personal phrase bank for each scenario type
Day 10

Listening & Reading Mock Tests

  • 2 full Listening tests under timed conditions
  • 2 full Reading tests under timed conditions
  • Focus on Listening Part A: note-taking accuracy
  • Focus on Reading Part A: speed (15 minutes max)
  • Review every wrong answer; understand WHY it's wrong
  • Add 50 more healthcare vocabulary words
Day 11

Mixed Practice Day

  • 1 full Writing test (2 letters)
  • 5 Speaking roleplays with recording
  • 1 Listening test
  • Review your best Writing samples for confidence
  • Polish your strongest Speaking scenarios
  • Review vocabulary: should have 200+ words by now
Day 12

Final Full Mock Test

  • Complete your FINAL full mock test
  • Simulate exam day: wake time, breaks, environment
  • This score predicts your exam performance
  • Analyse remaining weak spots — drill them today
  • Review your Writing grade criteria checklist
  • Final Speaking practice: 3 roleplays
Day 13

Light Review & Mental Prep

  • Review your vocabulary list one final time
  • Read 2 sample Grade B referral letters
  • Do 2 light Speaking warm-up roleplays
  • Prepare your exam day documents (ID, stationery)
  • Set your alarm; plan your travel route
  • Stop studying by 5 PM — rest your brain
Day 14 — EXAM DAY

Show What You Know

  • Wake 2 hours early; eat a healthy breakfast
  • Arrive 30 minutes before your scheduled time
  • Bring valid ID and proof of test booking
  • Warm up: speak English aloud for 5 minutes before Speaking
  • Stay calm — you've prepared for 2 solid weeks
  • Trust your training. You've got this, nurse.

OET Writing: The Grade B Checklist

Writing is where most nurses lose marks. Follow this checklist to ensure a Grade B or above on your referral letter.

Structure & Content (6 Marks)

✓

Open with clear purpose: "I am writing to refer/request/update..."

✓

Include ALL relevant case notes; omit irrelevant ones

✓

Organise information logically with clear paragraphs

✓

Close with appropriate request or expectation

Language & Tone (6 Marks)

✓

Use professional, formal medical tone throughout

✓

NO abbreviations (write "blood pressure" not "BP")

✓

Transform case notes into full sentences

✓

Use appropriate medical terminology correctly

Grammar & Accuracy (6 Marks)

✓

Word count between 180-200 words

✓

No spelling errors (especially medical terms)

✓

Correct verb tenses (past for history, present for current)

✓

Punctuation used correctly throughout

Common OET Writing Opening Lines

Referral: "I am writing to refer [patient name], a [age]-year-old [patient type], who requires your assessment and management of [condition]."

Discharge: "I am writing to discharge [patient name] into your care following [treatment/procedure]."

Transfer: "I am writing to request the transfer of [patient name] to your facility for [reason]."

Update: "I am writing to update you on the condition of [patient name], who was admitted on [date] with [condition]."

OET Speaking: Essential Nursing Phrases

Memorise these professional phrases for your Speaking roleplay. They demonstrate clinical competence and empathy — both scored in OET.

Rapport Building

Opening the Consultation

  • "Good morning, I'm [Name], your nurse today."
  • "Thank you for coming in today. How can I help you?"
  • "I can see from your notes that you're here about..."
  • "Before we begin, is there anything you'd like to ask me?"
  • "I'll be looking after you during your stay."
Information Gathering

Assessment Questions

  • "Can you describe the pain — where exactly is it?"
  • "When did you first notice these symptoms?"
  • "On a scale of 1 to 10, how would you rate the pain?"
  • "Have you experienced anything like this before?"
  • "Are you currently taking any medications?"
Explanation

Explaining Treatment

  • "I'd like to explain what will happen next..."
  • "The doctor has prescribed [medication] for you."
  • "This medication should help to reduce the..."
  • "You may experience some side effects, such as..."
  • "It's important to take this exactly as prescribed."
Empathy

Showing Understanding

  • "I understand this must be difficult for you."
  • "That sounds very challenging. I'm here to help."
  • "It's completely normal to feel worried about..."
  • "I can see you're in some discomfort. Let's address that."
  • "Your concerns are absolutely valid, and I want to reassure you."
Closing

Ending the Consultation

  • "Do you have any questions about what we've discussed?"
  • "Is there anything else you'd like me to explain?"
  • "I'll come back to check on you in [timeframe]."
  • "Please don't hesitate to call if you need anything."
  • "Thank you for your time. Take care."
Checking Understanding

Confirming Comprehension

  • "Does that make sense?"
  • "Would you like me to go over that again?"
  • "Can you tell me in your own words what you'll do?"
  • "Is there anything that's unclear?"
  • "Shall I write that down for you?"

Frequently Asked Questions

Is 2 weeks enough to prepare for OET?

Yes, for nurses with solid medical knowledge and intermediate English, 2 weeks of intensive preparation (5-6 hours daily) is sufficient to achieve Grade B. Your medical knowledge is your advantage — you mainly need to polish your professional English expression.

Which OET sub-test is hardest for nurses?

Writing is consistently the most challenging sub-test for nurses. The referral letter format requires transforming case notes into professional prose with correct grammar, spelling, and tone. Spend 40% of your preparation time on Writing.

Do I need to know UK nursing terminology?

OET accepts both British and American spelling and terminology. However, since most nurses take OET for UK/NHS registration, familiarity with UK terms (e.g., "ward" not "unit", "consultant" not "attending") is helpful but not required for scoring.

What's the difference between OET and IELTS for nurses?

OET uses healthcare-specific scenarios that reflect your workplace. IELTS tests general academic English. Most nurses find OET more relevant and achieve higher scores because the content is familiar. The NHS accepts both qualifications.

How many practice letters should I write?

In 2 weeks, aim to write at least 15-20 referral letters. Each letter should be timed (45 minutes) and reviewed against the OET criteria. Quality feedback matters more than quantity — get a teacher or experienced nurse to review your letters if possible.

Can I pass OET if my general English is weak?

OET tests professional healthcare English, not general conversation. Many nurses with "weak" general English pass OET because they understand the medical content. Focus on healthcare-specific vocabulary and professional communication phrases.

What score do I need for NMC registration?

The Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC) requires Grade B (350+) in all four OET sub-tests, OR Grade B in Reading, Listening, and Speaking plus Grade C+ in Writing. Check the latest NMC requirements as they may update.
