How International Doctors Can Work in the UK

Complete 2025 guide: GMC registration, PLAB exams, visa applications, NHS jobs & free preparation tools.

The United Kingdom remains one of the most sought-after destinations for international medical graduates. The NHS employs over 150,000 doctors from more than 150 countries, and demand for qualified medical professionals continues to grow. However, navigating the pathway from medical graduate to practising doctor in the UK can seem daunting. This comprehensive guide breaks down every step — from verifying your medical qualification to securing your first NHS position — with practical advice, timelines, and free resources to support your journey.

Overview: The Complete Pathway

There are several routes for international doctors to work in the UK, but the most common pathway involves these key stages. The entire process typically takes 12 to 24 months from starting preparations to beginning your first NHS post:

Typical Timeline

Month 1-2 Verify primary medical qualification with GMC; gather documents
Month 2-4 Prepare for and take IELTS Academic or OET Medicine
Month 4-8 Prepare for and pass PLAB Part 1 (written exam)
Month 8-12 Prepare for and pass PLAB Part 2 (practical OSCE)
Month 12-14 Apply for and receive GMC registration with licence to practise
Month 14-18 Apply for NHS jobs, attend interviews, receive job offer
Month 18-20 Apply for Health and Care Worker visa with Certificate of Sponsorship
Month 20-24 Arrive in UK, complete Trust induction, start work

Step 1: Verify Your Primary Medical Qualification

Before you can proceed with any other step, the General Medical Council (GMC) must recognise your primary medical qualification (PMQ). Your medical school must be listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools, and you must have completed an internship or equivalent after your degree.

Requirements:

  • A primary medical qualification acceptable to the GMC (typically a 5-6 year degree followed by internship)
  • Your medical school must be listed in the World Directory of Medical Schools
  • You must have completed a period of postgraduate training or internship after your degree
  • You must provide certified translations of all documents not in English

Document Checklist for PMQ Verification

  • Primary medical degree certificate (original or certified copy)
  • Medical school transcript
  • Internship/house officer completion certificate
  • Certificate of Good Standing from your medical regulatory body
  • Passport (valid for at least 6 months)
  • Certified English translations of all non-English documents

Step 2: English Language Requirements

All doctors practising in the UK must demonstrate proficiency in English. The GMC accepts two tests:

TestMinimum ScoreValidityBest For
IELTS Academic7.5 overall (min 7.0 in each section)2 yearsDoctors who need strong academic English; widely recognised
OET MedicineGrade B in all four sections (350+)2 yearsDoctors who prefer medical context; healthcare-specific vocabulary

Which Should You Choose?

IELTS Academic is the traditional choice and is widely accepted. However, many international doctors find OET Medicine more appropriate because the test uses healthcare-specific scenarios and vocabulary you are already familiar with. OET Writing involves a referral letter — a task you will actually perform in practice. Both tests are accepted equally by the GMC.

Preparation Tip

Start English preparation early — ideally 3-6 months before your test date. Many international doctors find the Writing and Speaking sections most challenging. Use our free IELTS Speaking Practice tool and IELTS Writing Practice tool to improve your scores with AI-powered feedback.

Step 3: PLAB or Recognised Qualification

The Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board (PLAB) test assesses whether you can demonstrate the same knowledge and skills as a doctor starting the second year of their Foundation Programme training in the UK. PLAB has two parts:

PLAB Part 1

  • Format: 180 multiple-choice questions (best-of-five)
  • Duration: 3 hours
  • Location: Available in multiple countries worldwide
  • Pass mark: Varies by exam sitting (typically 60-65%)
  • Cost: £247 (2025)
  • Preparation time: Most candidates need 3-6 months of dedicated study

Part 1 tests your ability to apply medical knowledge to clinical scenarios. Topics cover all major medical and surgical specialties, with emphasis on common conditions, emergencies, and basic sciences relevant to clinical practice.

PLAB Part 2

  • Format: Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE) — 18 stations
  • Duration: Approximately 3 hours (8 minutes per station)
  • Location: Manchester, UK only
  • Pass mark: Set by borderline regression method
  • Cost: £906 (2025)
  • Preparation time: Most candidates need 2-4 months, including a trip to Manchester for the exam

Part 2 is an OSCE that tests your clinical skills, communication, and professionalism. Stations include history taking, physical examination, practical procedures, counselling, and simulated patient encounters. This is where many candidates struggle — the OSCE format requires specific technique that differs from clinical practice in many countries.

Bypassing PLAB

You may not need PLAB if you hold certain recognised postgraduate qualifications, including: Membership of the Royal Colleges (MRCP, MRCS, MRCOG, etc.), GMC-recognised qualifications from certain countries, or completion of a GMC-approved training programme. Check the GMC website for the full list of accepted qualifications.

🩺 OSCE Practice for PLAB Part 2

PLAB Part 2 is an OSCE — the same format as the NMC OSCE for nurses. While the clinical content differs, the communication skills, time management, and station approach are very similar. Step 4: Apply for GMC Registration

Once you have passed your English language test and PLAB (or hold a recognised qualification), you can apply for GMC registration with a licence to practise. This is the legal requirement to work as a doctor in the UK.

The Registration Process:

  1. Create a GMC Online Account

    Register at gmc-uk.org and start your application. You will need your passport, PMQ details, and English language test results.

  2. Submit Your Application and Documents

    Upload all required documents including your medical degree, internship certificate, Certificate of Good Standing, English test results, and PLAB pass certificates (if applicable). All non-English documents must be officially translated.

  3. Identity Check

    You must attend an identity check in person at the GMC office in London or at designated overseas locations. Bring your original passport and other identity documents.

  4. Pay the Registration Fee

    The full registration fee is £161 (2025). You must also pay an annual retention fee to maintain your registration.

  5. Receive Your Registration

    Processing typically takes 2-5 working days after your identity check. You will receive your GMC reference number and can verify your status on the medical register.

Step 5: Finding NHS Jobs

With GMC registration in hand, you can now apply for NHS positions. Here is where to look:

Key Job Platforms:

  • NHS Jobs (jobs.nhs.uk): The official NHS recruitment website. Create a profile, upload your CV, and set up job alerts for your specialty.
  • NHS Scotland Recruitment: For positions in Scotland (separate system from England).
  • Health Careers: Information about different medical specialties and career paths in the NHS.
  • Agency Recruitment: Many international doctors secure their first NHS post through medical recruitment agencies that specialise in international placements.
  • Trust Websites: Individual NHS Trusts often advertise vacancies directly on their websites.

What NHS Employers Look For:

  • Full GMC registration with licence to practise
  • Relevant clinical experience (minimum 1-2 years post-internship)
  • Demonstrated English language proficiency
  • Right to work in the UK (Health and Care Worker visa)
  • Good communication and teamwork skills
  • Familiarity with NHS values and the GMC's Good Medical Practice

First Job Strategy

Many international doctors start in a non-training post (often called a "trust grade" or "service post") to gain NHS experience before applying for specialty training. These positions are more readily available, provide valuable UK clinical experience, and demonstrate your commitment to working in the NHS. After 6-12 months, you will be in a much stronger position to apply for competitive training programmes.

Step 6: UK Visa Application

International doctors from outside the UK and Ireland need a visa to work in the NHS. The Health and Care Worker visa is the most appropriate route for medical professionals.

Health and Care Worker Visa Requirements:

  • A job offer from an NHS Trust or eligible UK employer
  • A Certificate of Sponsorship (CoS) from your employer
  • Proof of English language proficiency (your IELTS/OET results)
  • Adequate funds to support yourself (£1,270 unless your employer covers this)
  • Tuberculosis test results (if from a listed country)
  • Payment of the visa fee (£284 for up to 3 years; £568 for over 3 years)
  • Immigration Health Surcharge (£1,035 per year — though many NHS employers reimburse this)

Visa Processing Timeline:

Processing times vary by country but typically range from 3 to 8 weeks. Priority processing services are available in some countries for an additional fee. You can apply up to 3 months before your intended start date.

Step 7: Arrival and Starting Work

Congratulations — you are nearly there! Once you arrive in the UK, there are several important steps:

Before You Start:

  • National Insurance Number: Apply immediately — required for tax and pension contributions
  • GP Registration: Register with a local General Practitioner for your own healthcare
  • Bank Account: Open a UK bank account (your employer will need this to pay your salary)
  • Accommodation: Many NHS Trusts offer temporary accommodation or have partnerships with local landlords
  • BRC (Biometric Residence Card): Collect if you applied outside the UK

Trust Induction:

Your first week will involve mandatory Trust induction including: mandatory training (fire safety, infection control, information governance), introduction to hospital systems and electronic records, meeting your clinical supervisor, occupational health clearance, and ID badge and access card issuance. This typically takes 3-5 days before you start clinical work.

Alternative Routes for International Doctors

PLAB is not the only pathway. Consider these alternatives:

Medical Training Initiative (MTI)

The MTI scheme allows international doctors to come to the UK for up to 24 months of supervised training in the NHS. You need: GMC registration (without PLAB in some cases), a postgraduate qualification, 3+ years of clinical experience, and a sponsor (usually a Royal College). The MTI is an excellent pathway for experienced doctors seeking UK training exposure.

Royal College Memberships

If you pass a Royal College membership exam (MRCP, MRCS, MRCOG, MRCPCH, etc.), you can apply for GMC Specialist or GP Registration. This bypasses PLAB entirely and allows direct entry into higher specialty training. This route requires significant postgraduate experience and examination success.

GMC-Recognised Overseas Qualifications

Doctors from certain countries with specific qualifications may be exempt from PLAB. Check the GMC website for the current list of recognised qualifications. This list changes periodically, so verify the most up-to-date information.

Top Tips for Success

From Doctors Who Have Done It

  • Start English preparation early: Many doctors underestimate how much time is needed to reach IELTS 7.5 or OET Grade B. Begin 6+ months before your test.
  • Join online communities: Facebook groups and forums for international doctors provide invaluable peer support and up-to-date information.
  • Understand the NHS culture: Read the GMC's "Good Medical Practice" and familiarise yourself with NHS values before interviews.
  • Consider non-training posts first: A trust-grade post gives you NHS experience, a UK reference, and time to apply for competitive training positions.
  • Prepare financially: Budget for at least £5,000 total costs, and have a contingency fund for unexpected expenses.
  • Use all free resources: Our free AI-powered preparation tools can significantly reduce the cost of exam preparation.
  • Be patient and persistent: The process is lengthy and complex. Setbacks are normal. Stay organised and keep moving forward.

Frequently Asked Questions

What qualifications do I need to work as a doctor in the UK?
To work as a doctor in the UK, you need: a medical degree recognised by the GMC, GMC registration (with a licence to practise), proof of English language proficiency (IELTS Academic 7.5 or OET Medicine Grade B), and either PLAB pass or GMC-recognised postgraduate qualification. You will also need a visa (typically Health and Care Worker visa) and an NHS job offer.
What is the PLAB exam?
PLAB (Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board) is an exam that assesses whether international medical graduates can demonstrate the same skills and knowledge as a doctor starting their second year of Foundation Programme training in the UK. PLAB 1 is a written knowledge test; PLAB 2 is a practical Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE).
How long does it take for an international doctor to start working in the UK?
The timeline varies but typically takes 12-24 months. This includes: 3-6 months for GMC registration and document verification, 3-6 months for PLAB preparation and testing, 2-3 months for visa application, and 2-6 months for NHS job searching and interviews. Some doctors with recognised qualifications may bypass PLAB, significantly reducing this timeline.
Can I work in the UK without PLAB?
Yes, if you hold a GMC-recognised postgraduate qualification such as MRCP, MRCS, MRCOG, or FRCA. Doctors from certain countries with specific qualifications may also be exempt. Additionally, the Medical Training Initiative (MTI) scheme may allow training posts without PLAB.
How much do doctors earn in the NHS?
NHS doctor salaries vary by grade and specialty. As of 2025, Foundation Year 1 doctors earn approximately £36,000 per year, Foundation Year 2 approximately £42,000, and specialty trainees earn between £43,000 and £63,000 depending on the year of training. Consultants earn from £99,000 to over £130,000. Many NHS posts also include additional pay for on-call and weekend work.
Is IELTS or OET better for GMC registration?
Both are accepted equally by the GMC. IELTS Academic is more widely recognised internationally, while OET Medicine uses healthcare-specific scenarios that many doctors find more relevant to their experience.
