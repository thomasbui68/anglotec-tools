What is NHS Staff Bank Nursing?

NHS staff banks are internal pools of healthcare workers who fill shifts within a specific NHS trust or group of trusts. Unlike agency nursing, which involves external recruitment companies, bank nursing is managed by the NHS itself. Bank nurses are employed directly by the trust or through NHS Professionals (NHSP), the largest staff bank in England. Bank nursing offers the flexibility of temporary work with the benefits and familiarity of NHS employment. It is an ideal option for nurses who want flexible hours, extra income on top of a permanent role, or the security of working within one healthcare system.

The Hidden Value of Bank Nursing

While agency rates appear higher, bank nurses receive NHS pension contributions (worth 20-30% of salary), sick pay, annual leave pay, maternity/paternity pay, and access to NHS training. When you factor in these benefits, the total compensation often exceeds agency work.

How to Register with an NHS Staff Bank

1

Choose Your Staff Bank

Most NHS trusts run their own staff bank. Some have joined together to create larger banks covering multiple hospitals. The largest is NHS Professionals (NHSP), which covers many trusts across England.

Types of NHS Staff Banks

  • NHS Professionals (NHSP): Largest staff bank in England, covers 50+ trusts. Apply online at nhsp.uk
  • Trust-specific banks: Individual hospitals run their own banks. Apply through the trust website
  • Collaborative banks: Groups of trusts share a bank system, offering shifts across multiple hospitals
2

Complete the Application and Compliance

The registration process is similar to permanent NHS employment. You will need to provide documentation and attend an interview or compliance check.

Required Documentation

  • NMC PIN and registration certificate
  • Right to work in the UK (passport/visa)
  • DBS certificate (less than 3 years old, on Update Service preferred)
  • Occupational health clearance
  • Core training certificates (BLS, manual handling, safeguarding adults and children, fire safety, information governance)
  • 2 clinical references
  • Proof of professional indemnity (NMC registration covers this)

Processing Time

Bank registration typically takes 4-8 weeks. Start the process well before you want to begin working. Having your DBS on the Update Service and up-to-date training certificates speeds up the process significantly.

3

Complete Your Mandatory Training

All bank nurses must complete mandatory training before their first shift. Most trusts offer e-learning modules that can be completed online.

Typical Mandatory Training Modules

  • Health and safety
  • Fire safety
  • Manual handling
  • Infection prevention and control
  • Safeguarding adults (Level 2)
  • Safeguarding children (Level 2)
  • Information governance and data protection
  • Equality and diversity
  • Conflict resolution
  • Basic life support (practical session required)
4

Booking Shifts

Once registered, you can book shifts through the staff bank app or website. Shifts are released in advance and can be booked on a first-come, first-served basis.

Shift Booking Tips

  • Check the app daily for new shift releases
  • Book shifts as early as possible (popular wards fill quickly)
  • Build relationships with ward managers for direct bookings
  • Be reliable: failing to turn up for booked shifts results in removal from the bank
  • Start with wards you know, then expand to new areas
  • Consider a mix of day, night, and weekend shifts for variety
5

NHS Professionals (NHSP): The Largest Bank

NHSP is the largest NHS staff bank, covering over 50 NHS trusts. Working through NHSP gives you access to shifts across a wide geographic area, all with NHS employment benefits.

NHSP Key Benefits

  • Access to shifts across multiple trusts
  • NHS pension scheme membership
  • Sick pay after qualifying period
  • Maternity/paternity pay
  • Annual leave entitlement (pro-rata)
  • Online shift booking via app
  • Fast-track registration for those with recent NHS experience

How to join: Visit nhsp.uk and complete the online application. You will need all standard compliance documents.

Bank Nursing: Best of Both Worlds

Bank nursing combines the flexibility of temporary work with the benefits and security of NHS employment. For nurses seeking work-life balance, extra income, or a gradual return to practice, it is the ideal solution.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is bank nursing different from agency nursing?

Bank nurses are employed by the NHS (or NHSP) and work within one trust or group of trusts. Agency nurses work through external recruitment agencies and can work anywhere. Bank nurses receive NHS benefits (pension, sick pay); agency nurses typically do not.

Can I work for both a staff bank and an agency?

Yes, many nurses work both bank and agency shifts. However, some trusts restrict this, so check your bank contract. Ensure you are not breaching any exclusivity clauses.

Do bank nurses get NHS pension?

Yes, bank nurses employed through NHSP or trust banks are eligible for the NHS pension scheme. This is a significant advantage over agency work. Agency nurses must make their own pension arrangements.

How many hours can I work on the bank?

There is no upper limit, but you must ensure safe working hours. The Working Time Regulations require adequate rest between shifts. If you also have a permanent NHS job, check your contract for any restrictions on additional work.

Can I transition from bank to permanent?

Yes, many nurses use bank work as a pathway to permanent employment. Working bank shifts allows you to build relationships, demonstrate your skills, and often leads to permanent job offers when vacancies arise.
