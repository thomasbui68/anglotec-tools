Understanding IELTS Speaking Part 3

IELTS Speaking Part 3 is the two-way discussion section, lasting 4-5 minutes. The examiner asks broader, more abstract questions connected to the topic of your Part 2 cue card. This section tests your ability to discuss complex ideas, express opinions, and engage in analytical conversation.

Part 3 accounts for approximately 35% of your speaking score. It is where the examiner differentiates between Band 6 and Band 7+ candidates. Strong performance here requires not just good English, but the ability to think critically and articulate abstract concepts.

The questions become progressively more abstract. The examiner may begin with something relatively straightforward ("What sports are popular in your country?") before moving to deeper analysis ("Do you think competitive sport creates or reduces social division?"). This progression allows you to demonstrate increasing sophistication.

Part 3 at a Glance

  • Duration: 4-5 minutes
  • Questions: 4-6 questions on 2-3 sub-topics
  • Score weight: ~35% of speaking score
  • Focus: Abstract concepts, opinions, analysis, speculation
  • Style: Formal two-way discussion (the examiner may challenge your views)

Key Strategies for Part 3

1. Buy Thinking Time

Use phrases like "That's a fascinating question," "I've never considered that before," or "There are several ways to look at this." This sounds natural and gives you 3-4 seconds to organise your thoughts.

2. Show Both Sides

For analytical questions, present multiple perspectives before stating your view. Use "On the one hand... on the other hand..." or "While some argue that... others believe..."

3. Use Abstract Language

Shift from personal examples ("I think...") to general statements ("It could be argued that...", "Many people would contend that...", "There is a widespread belief that...")

4. Speculate Freely

Questions about the future or hypotheticals are common. Use "I imagine that...", "It seems likely that...", "One possibility is that...", "Were this to happen, then..."

5. Extend Your Answers

After answering, add: "The reason I say this is..." / "A good example of this would be..." / "This is particularly relevant because..." / "The implications of this are significant because..."

6. Handle Challenges Gracefully

If the examiner challenges your view with "But don't you think...?", don't panic. This is normal. Say "That's a valid point. However, I would argue that..." or "I see your perspective, though I still believe..."

Structuring Your Answers

A well-structured Part 3 answer impresses examiners. Use these frameworks:

Pattern A: Direct Answer + Reason + Example + Implication

"I believe that [direct answer]. The primary reason for this is [reason]. For instance, [example]. This suggests that [implication]."

Pattern B: Balanced View + Your Position

"This is a complex issue. On one hand, [argument A]. On the other hand, [argument B]. Personally, I would argue that [your position], primarily because [reason]."

Pattern C: Speculation About the Future

"It seems probable that [prediction]. Several factors support this view. Firstly, [factor 1]. Secondly, [factor 2]. That said, [counter-possibility]."

Topic Areas with Model Answers

Below are model answers across the most common Part 3 topic areas. Study the language patterns and analytical approach rather than memorising specific answers.

Education & Learning

Q: Do you think the traditional education system is still relevant in the digital age?

"That's a thought-provoking question. I believe traditional education still holds considerable value, though it undoubtedly needs to evolve. The fundamental purpose of schools extends far beyond information delivery; they serve as environments for social development, critical thinking, and character formation. These aspects cannot be replicated purely through digital means.

However, the traditional model of lectures and textbook-based learning is increasingly outdated. The internet has democratised access to information, meaning that memorising facts has become less valuable than learning how to evaluate and apply knowledge. Therefore, I would argue that the most effective approach is a hybrid model that combines the social and developmental benefits of physical schools with the personalised, flexible advantages of digital learning tools."

Q: Should education be free for everyone?

"In principle, I believe that access to quality education should be considered a fundamental right rather than a privilege. The societal benefits of an educated population are immense, including higher economic productivity, lower crime rates, and greater social cohesion.

That said, the practical challenges of funding universal free education are considerable. Governments have finite budgets, and completely free tertiary education would require substantial tax increases or reallocation from other essential services. Some countries, like Germany and Finland, have managed this successfully through progressive taxation, which suggests it is achievable.

A more pragmatic approach might be a mixed system: free primary and secondary education for all, with means-tested support for higher education. This would ensure everyone has foundational skills while acknowledging that those who benefit most financially from university degrees could reasonably contribute toward their cost."

Q: What role should technology play in the classroom?

"Technology should function as a tool to enhance learning rather than replace effective teaching. When used thoughtfully, it can personalise instruction, provide immediate feedback, and connect students with resources beyond their physical location.

However, there are legitimate concerns about over-reliance on screens, particularly for younger children. Research has suggested that excessive screen time can negatively impact attention spans and social development. Furthermore, the digital divide means that not all students have equal access to devices and high-speed internet, which risks exacerbating educational inequality.

Therefore, I would advocate for a balanced approach: using technology where it clearly adds value, such as for adaptive learning platforms and collaborative projects, while maintaining plenty of offline activities that develop interpersonal skills and hands-on creativity."

Q: Is it better to have a broad education or specialise early?

"This is a debate with compelling arguments on both sides. Proponents of early specialisation argue that it allows students to develop deep expertise in their chosen field, which is increasingly valuable in competitive job markets. There is some evidence that those who focus early achieve mastery more quickly.

Conversely, I am inclined to support a broader educational foundation, at least through secondary school. The modern economy is characterised by rapid change, and many of today's jobs may not exist in twenty years. A broad education develops transferable skills such as critical thinking, communication, and adaptability that prepare students for multiple career paths. Moreover, exposure to diverse subjects allows young people to discover passions they might never have encountered in a narrow curriculum.

Ultimately, I believe students should maintain breadth until university level, at which point specialisation becomes more appropriate."

Q: How has the role of teachers changed?

"The role of teachers has undergone a fundamental transformation in recent decades. Historically, teachers were primarily sources of information, essentially human encyclopaedias from whom students acquired knowledge. In the internet age, this function has become largely obsolete.

Today, effective teachers function more as facilitators, mentors, and learning designers. Their primary role is to inspire curiosity, guide critical thinking, and create meaningful learning experiences. Rather than simply delivering content, they help students navigate vast amounts of information, distinguish reliable sources from misinformation, and apply knowledge to real-world problems.

This shift places greater demands on teachers. They must now be technologically literate, skilled in differentiated instruction, and capable of fostering social-emotional development alongside academic learning. It is, in many ways, a more challenging but also more rewarding profession than it once was."

Technology & Society

Q: Has social media made people more or less connected?

"This is a nuanced question that defies a simple answer. On a superficial level, social media has undeniably connected more people than ever before. We can maintain relationships across vast distances and reconnect with people we might otherwise have lost touch with.

However, I would argue that the quality of these connections has often deteriorated. Social media interactions tend to be brief, curated, and performative rather than genuine. There is compelling research suggesting that heavy social media use correlates with increased loneliness, anxiety, and depression, particularly among younger users.

Furthermore, the algorithms that drive these platforms tend to create echo chambers, exposing us primarily to views we already hold and potentially narrowing our perspectives. So while we may have more connections in a quantitative sense, the depth and authenticity of human relationships may actually have diminished."

Q: Should governments regulate artificial intelligence?

"I believe that thoughtful regulation of artificial intelligence is not only desirable but essential. AI is developing at an extraordinary pace, and without appropriate safeguards, there are significant risks to employment, privacy, and even democratic processes.

The challenge lies in crafting regulation that protects society without stifling innovation. Overly restrictive policies could drive research to countries with laxer standards, creating a regulatory race to the bottom. International cooperation would therefore be ideal, though achieving consensus among nations with different values and priorities is enormously difficult.

I would suggest focusing regulation on high-risk applications: autonomous weapons, facial recognition in public spaces, and AI systems that make consequential decisions about employment or criminal justice. For lower-risk applications, a lighter touch that encourages responsible development while allowing experimentation seems more appropriate."

Q: Is automation a threat to employment?

"Automation undoubtedly poses significant challenges to certain sectors and categories of workers. Routine manual and cognitive tasks are increasingly being performed by machines, and this trend is likely to accelerate. Workers in manufacturing, administrative roles, and even some professional fields such as accounting and legal research face genuine disruption.

However, history suggests that technological advancement, while eliminating some jobs, typically creates new ones that we cannot yet imagine. The challenge is that this transition can be extremely painful for affected individuals and communities, and the new jobs created may require different skills or be located in different regions.

Therefore, I would argue that automation itself is not the enemy; rather, the problem is inadequate preparation for the transition. Governments and businesses should invest heavily in retraining programmes, social safety nets, and education systems that emphasise adaptability and creativity over routine skills."

Q: Do people rely too much on technology?

"In many respects, yes. Our dependence on technology has reached a point where many people would struggle to function without their smartphones, GPS navigation, or digital assistants. This over-reliance has several concerning consequences.

Firstly, it appears to be eroding certain cognitive abilities. When we outsource our memory and navigation to devices, we may be weakening the neural pathways associated with those functions. There is also the question of attention spans; the constant stimulation provided by technology makes it increasingly difficult to engage in sustained deep thinking.

That said, I would not advocate rejecting technology entirely. The benefits are too substantial. Rather, I believe we need to develop more mindful relationships with our devices, setting boundaries and preserving time for analogue activities. Digital literacy education should include not just how to use technology, but also how to live well alongside it."

Q: How will technology change the way we work in the future?

"I anticipate several transformative changes to the nature of work. Remote and hybrid work arrangements, which accelerated dramatically during the pandemic, are likely to become permanent fixtures for many industries. This will have profound implications for urban planning, as the daily commute to central business districts becomes less universal.

AI will increasingly handle routine analytical and creative tasks, pushing human workers toward roles that require emotional intelligence, complex problem-solving, and ethical judgment. The concept of a single career may become obsolete, with individuals needing to pivot between multiple roles throughout their working lives.

Perhaps most radically, there are growing discussions about universal basic income and shorter working weeks as productivity increases. While these ideas remain controversial, they reflect a recognition that the relationship between work, income, and meaning may need fundamental rethinking in an age of abundance."

Environment & Health

Q: Whose responsibility is it to tackle climate change?

"Addressing climate change requires coordinated action at every level of society. Governments bear the greatest responsibility because only they have the authority and resources to implement systemic changes: transitioning energy grids, regulating industries, and investing in sustainable infrastructure. International agreements are essential because climate change transcends borders.

However, placing all responsibility on governments lets individuals and corporations off too lightly. Businesses must be held accountable for their environmental impact, and consumers have power through their purchasing decisions. Individual choices about diet, transport, and consumption, while seemingly small in isolation, collectively make a significant difference.

I would argue that it is ultimately a shared responsibility, with different actors playing complementary roles. The most progress occurs when all levels work in concert toward common goals."

Q: Is modern life more stressful than in the past?

"I believe modern life presents a different kind of stress rather than necessarily more stress. Previous generations faced existential threats such as war, disease, and poverty that are less prevalent in many parts of the world today. Their stress was often physical and immediate.

Contemporary stress tends to be more chronic and psychological. The constant connectivity enabled by technology means many people never truly switch off from work. Social media creates pressure to present idealised versions of our lives. Economic uncertainty, despite material abundance, generates pervasive anxiety about housing, education, and retirement.

Furthermore, while we have made remarkable progress in treating physical illness, mental health services remain woefully inadequate in most countries. The stigma surrounding mental health also prevents many from seeking help. So while we may be safer from physical dangers, the psychological demands of modern life are considerable and insufficiently addressed."

Q: Should unhealthy foods be taxed?

"I support the taxation of unhealthy foods, provided it is implemented thoughtfully. There is substantial evidence from countries such as Mexico and the UK that sugar taxes reduce consumption of sugary beverages, particularly among lower-income groups who are disproportionately affected by diet-related diseases.

However, taxation alone is insufficient and potentially regressive. Simply making unhealthy food more expensive without providing affordable healthy alternatives risks punishing people who live in "food deserts" where fresh produce is unavailable or prohibitively expensive.

A comprehensive approach would combine taxation with subsidies for healthy foods, better nutritional education, and regulation of food marketing aimed at children. The revenue generated from such taxes could fund these complementary measures. Used as one tool among many, taxation can be effective; used in isolation, it may simply burden those already struggling."

Q: How can cities be made more environmentally friendly?

"Creating environmentally friendly cities requires a multifaceted approach spanning urban planning, transport, energy, and green spaces. The most impactful single change would be reducing car dependency through investment in public transport, cycling infrastructure, and pedestrian-friendly urban design. Cities like Copenhagen and Amsterdam have demonstrated that this is achievable and popular.

Buildings are another major area for improvement. Strict energy efficiency standards for new construction and retrofitting programmes for existing buildings can dramatically reduce emissions. Integrating renewable energy sources and smart grid technology enables cities to generate and manage clean power more effectively.

Finally, urban greening initiatives such as parks, green roofs, and urban forests provide multiple benefits: carbon sequestration, improved air quality, heat island mitigation, and enhanced mental health for residents. The most sustainable cities recognise that environmental and human wellbeing are inseparable."

Q: What are the main causes of mental health issues in modern society?

"Mental health issues in contemporary society stem from a complex interplay of biological, psychological, and social factors. Several societal trends appear particularly relevant.

Social isolation is a significant contributor, which is somewhat paradoxical given our hyper-connected world. Despite having hundreds of online connections, many people lack deep, supportive relationships. The erosion of community structures, such as religious organisations, extended families, and local clubs, has left a void.

Economic precarity is another major factor. The gig economy, zero-hours contracts, and soaring housing costs create persistent uncertainty that takes a psychological toll. Young people, in particular, face unprecedented pressures around education, employment, and establishing independent lives.

Finally, the information environment itself is problematic. Constant exposure to negative news, unrealistic standards on social media, and the erosion of boundaries between work and personal life create a background hum of anxiety. Addressing these issues requires both individual coping strategies and systemic social change."

Work & Money

Q: Is job satisfaction more important than salary?

"This is a deeply personal question, and the answer likely depends on one's circumstances. For individuals struggling to meet basic needs, salary is understandably the priority. Financial security is a prerequisite for wellbeing, and no amount of job satisfaction compensates for the stress of not being able to pay rent or afford healthcare.

However, once basic needs are met, research consistently shows that additional income yields diminishing returns in terms of happiness. Job satisfaction, conversely, correlates strongly with overall life satisfaction, mental health, and even longevity. People who find their work meaningful tend to be more engaged, productive, and resilient.

I would argue that the ideal is finding work that provides both adequate compensation and genuine fulfillment. When forced to choose, younger people with fewer obligations might reasonably prioritise experience and growth, while those with families may need to prioritise stability. Neither choice is inherently wrong."

Q: Should there be a maximum limit on how much people can earn?

"This is a controversial proposition. Proponents argue that extreme wealth concentration is morally problematic and economically inefficient, citing the fact that the world's richest individuals possess more wealth than billions of people combined. They suggest that income caps would reduce inequality and generate revenue for public services.

Opponents argue that such caps would eliminate incentives for innovation and risk-taking. Many transformative businesses were created by individuals motivated in part by the prospect of significant financial rewards. Furthermore, implementing a global cap is practically impossible, and unilateral implementation would likely drive high earners to relocate.

My view is that while direct caps are problematic, extremely high marginal tax rates on income above certain thresholds, combined with robust wealth taxes and crackdowns on tax avoidance, could achieve similar redistributive goals without the same disincentive effects."

Q: How has the concept of retirement changed?

"The traditional model of retirement, where individuals stop working entirely at a fixed age and live on pension income, is becoming increasingly obsolete. Several factors are driving this transformation.

Increased life expectancy means that retirement periods now routinely last twenty to thirty years, which many pension systems were not designed to support financially. Simultaneously, the nature of work has shifted; many modern jobs are less physically demanding than those of previous generations, making it feasible to work longer.

There is also a growing recognition that complete withdrawal from work can be detrimental to mental and physical health. Many people find purpose, identity, and social connection through their careers, and abrupt cessation can lead to isolation and decline.

The emerging model seems to be a gradual transition, with people reducing hours, shifting to consultancy or mentorship roles, or pursuing encore careers in different fields. Retirement is becoming less a cliff edge and more a gradual slope."

Q: What skills will be most valuable in the future job market?

"As automation and artificial intelligence continue to advance, the skills that will be most valuable are those that complement technology rather than compete with it. Routine cognitive and manual tasks are increasingly automated, so the premium is on uniquely human capabilities.

Critical thinking and complex problem-solving will be essential. The ability to analyse ambiguous situations, evaluate evidence, and make sound judgments in the absence of clear rules is difficult to automate and highly valued across industries.

Emotional intelligence and interpersonal skills will also become more important. Roles involving caregiving, leadership, negotiation, and creative collaboration are likely to grow. Similarly, creativity and adaptability, the capacity to learn continuously and pivot between roles, will be crucial.

Finally, digital literacy will be a baseline requirement across virtually all professions. The most successful individuals will combine technical competence with distinctly human skills."

Q: Should companies be allowed to ask employees to work overtime?

"This depends entirely on the circumstances. Voluntary overtime, where employees choose to work additional hours and are fairly compensated, is generally acceptable. Many workers appreciate the opportunity to increase their earnings, particularly in industries with seasonal demand.

Mandatory or implicitly coerced overtime is more problematic. In some workplace cultures, employees feel pressured to work excessive hours to demonstrate commitment or avoid jeopardising their positions. This can lead to burnout, health problems, and diminished productivity in the long term. There should be legal protections ensuring that overtime is genuinely voluntary and that employees who decline are not penalised.

I believe there should also be reasonable limits on total working hours to protect health and safety. Some countries have implemented "right to disconnect" laws that prevent employers from contacting workers outside hours, which seems a sensible development."

Society & Culture

Q: Is multiculturalism a strength or weakness for a country?

"I firmly believe multiculturalism is a significant strength when managed well. Diverse societies benefit from a wider range of perspectives, ideas, and approaches to problem-solving. Immigrants contribute economically, fill labour gaps, and enrich cultural life through food, arts, and traditions. Cities like London and Toronto demonstrate that multiculturalism can create vibrant, dynamic, and innovative communities.

However, I acknowledge that rapid demographic change can create tensions if not accompanied by thoughtful integration policies. Successful multiculturalism requires shared values, mutual respect, and investment in social cohesion. When communities live in parallel without meaningful interaction, distrust can develop.

Therefore, the challenge is not whether to be multicultural, but how to ensure that diversity is accompanied by genuine inclusion and shared belonging. Countries that achieve this balance reap enormous benefits."

Q: Should the government ban dangerous sports?

"I do not believe governments should ban dangerous sports outright. Adults should generally have the autonomy to make informed decisions about risks they are willing to take, provided they do not harm others. Many activities carry inherent dangers, from mountain climbing to motor racing, yet participants derive immense satisfaction, personal growth, and even identity from them.

However, there is a legitimate role for regulation rather than prohibition. Requiring safety standards, mandatory training, proper equipment, and insurance coverage can reduce risks significantly without eliminating the activity. Age restrictions and requirements for parental consent for minors are also reasonable.

The key distinction is between protecting people from external harms caused by others and protecting them from their own informed choices. Governments should focus on the former while providing information and safeguards for the latter."

Q: How important is it to preserve traditional customs?

"Preserving traditional customs is important, though the extent of importance varies by context. Customs that strengthen social bonds, transmit valuable knowledge, and provide a sense of identity and continuity deserve protection. Festivals, ceremonies, and crafts connect people to their heritage and offer meaning in an increasingly homogenised world.

However, not all traditions deserve preservation. Customs that are discriminatory, harmful, or incompatible with human rights should be allowed to evolve or disappear. The challenge is distinguishing between traditions that enrich society and those that constrain progress.

I believe the most constructive approach is living traditions that adapt to contemporary contexts rather than fossilised practices preserved purely for tourism. When young people find relevance in traditional customs and can reinterpret them creatively, they are most likely to endure and thrive."

Q: Do celebrities have a responsibility to be good role models?

"This is a complex question about the nature of celebrity and influence. Celebrities undoubtedly have enormous influence, particularly over young people. Their behaviour, endorsements, and public statements shape attitudes and aspirations. With this influence comes a degree of responsibility.

However, I am reluctant to say they have an obligation to be role models. Celebrities are human beings with flaws and private lives. Holding them to unrealistic moral standards is unfair and can lead to the harmful phenomena of cancel culture and invasive media scrutiny.

I would distinguish between private behaviour, which should remain private, and public actions that cause demonstrable harm. When celebrities promote dangerous products, spread misinformation, or engage in harmful behaviour that is emulated by followers, criticism is warranted. Ultimately, the responsibility lies partly with media consumers and parents to teach critical engagement with celebrity culture rather than uncritical admiration."

Q: Is individualism or collectivism better for society?

"Both individualism and collectivism offer valuable insights, and the most successful societies find a balance between them. Pure individualism can lead to fragmentation, inequality, and weakened social bonds. When everyone pursues only their own interests, public goods such as infrastructure, education, and environmental protection are neglected.

Conversely, excessive collectivism can suppress creativity, diversity, and personal freedom. Societies that demand conformity may achieve stability but at the cost of innovation and personal fulfillment.

I believe the ideal lies in what might be called "community individualism": a society that protects individual rights and freedoms while fostering strong communities and shared responsibility for collective wellbeing. Scandinavian countries arguably approach this balance, combining robust social safety nets with respect for individual choice and expression."

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I say "I don't know" in Part 3?

Avoid simply saying "I don't know." Instead, say "I haven't given that much thought, but I suppose..." or "That's outside my expertise, though I imagine..." and then speculate. The examiner wants to hear your English, not test your knowledge.

Why does the examiner interrupt me?

The examiner must move through 4-6 questions in 4-5 minutes. If you speak for too long, they may interrupt to stay on schedule. This is normal and does not mean your answer was bad. Keep your answers to 40-60 seconds.

Should I agree with the examiner?

No. The examiner's views are irrelevant. They may challenge you to test your ability to defend your position. Stay calm and support your opinion with reasons. It's fine to say "I see your point, though I still believe..."

How formal should my language be?

More formal than Part 1 and 2. Avoid slang and overly casual expressions. Use abstract vocabulary, formal connectors ("furthermore," "consequently," "nevertheless"), and complex grammatical structures.

Can I use personal examples?

Personal examples are acceptable but should be brief. Part 3 focuses on abstract, general discussion. Use societal examples rather than personal stories. Say "In many countries..." rather than "In my country..." when possible.

What if I don't understand a question?

Politely ask for clarification. Say "I'm not entirely sure what you mean by that. Could you rephrase the question?" or "Do you mean...?" This is better than answering the wrong question.
