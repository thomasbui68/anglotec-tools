International doctors must obtain GMC registration by passing PLAB 1 and PLAB 2, or through a recognised postgraduate qualification. They also need a work visa and a job offer from an NHS employer.
GMC registration (General Medical Council) is the legal requirement to practise medicine in the UK. It ensures doctors meet the necessary standards of medical knowledge, skill, and behaviour. There are different types: provisional, full, and specialist/GP registration.
PLAB stands for Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board. Most international medical graduates need to pass PLAB 1 (written) and PLAB 2 (practical OSCE) to qualify for GMC registration, unless they have an approved postgraduate qualification.
Doctors with certain postgraduate qualifications such as MRCP, MRCS, FRCA, or equivalent Royal College exams may apply for GMC registration without PLAB. They must also meet English language requirements.
The GMC accepts IELTS Academic with 7.5 overall (minimum 7.0 in each section) or OET with Grade B in all sub-tests. These must be achieved in a single sitting within the last two years.
Foundation Year 1 doctors earn approximately £36,000 to £42,000. Specialist trainees earn £43,000 to £58,000. Consultants earn £93,000 to £126,000. Salaries increase with experience, location weighting, and additional duties.
The NHS doctor career pathway includes: Foundation Programme (FY1, FY2), Core/Specialty Training (CT1-3, ST1-7+), and Consultant or GP. International doctors often enter at non-training grade posts before entering training programmes.
The Medical Training Initiative (MTI) allows international doctors to undertake up to 24 months of training in the UK. It is supported by the Academy of Medical Royal Colleges and provides GMC registration and a training post.
International doctors typically need a Skilled Worker visa (formerly Tier 2 General). The NHS is a licensed sponsor, and medical roles are on the shortage occupation list, making the visa process more straightforward.
Non-training posts include Trust Grade, Staff Grade, Clinical Fellow, and Locum positions. These provide valuable UK clinical experience while doctors apply for competitive specialty training programmes.
You can apply through NHS Jobs (jobs.nhs.uk), hospital trust websites, medical recruitment agencies, and specialist recruitment services. A well-prepared CV and cover letter tailored to UK standards are essential.
A doctor in an FY1-equivalent Trust Grade post typically earns between £36,000 and £42,000 per year, depending on location, hours, and additional pay for on-call duties and London weighting.
GMC registration typically takes 2 to 4 months from initial application, assuming all documents are in order. This includes qualification verification, English language verification, and PLAB certificate processing.
Yes, but entry into UK specialty training is competitive. International doctors often work in non-training posts first to gain UK experience, then apply through the national recruitment rounds for their chosen specialty.
A Clinical Attachment is an unpaid, short-term observational placement in an NHS hospital. It helps international doctors familiarise themselves with the NHS system, improve communication skills, and build professional networks.
Yes, all doctors in the UK must have professional indemnity insurance. NHS employees are covered by the Clinical Negligence Scheme for Trusts (CNST). Some doctors also purchase additional private indemnity.
Revalidation is the process by which doctors demonstrate they remain fit to practise. It happens every five years and involves annual appraisals, continuing professional development, and colleague feedback.
