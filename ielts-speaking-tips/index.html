Understanding the IELTS Speaking Test Format

Before diving into tips, you must understand exactly what you are preparing for. The IELTS Speaking test lasts 11-14 minutes and is divided into three distinct parts. Each part tests different aspects of your spoken English ability:

PartDurationFormatWhat It Tests
Part 14-5 minutesGeneral questions about yourself, family, work, studies, interestsAbility to communicate opinions and information on everyday topics
Part 23-4 minutes (inc. prep)Individual long turn: 1 min prep + 2 min speaking on a given topicAbility to speak at length on a topic using appropriate language
Part 34-5 minutesAbstract discussion questions related to Part 2 topicAbility to express and justify opinions, analyse and speculate

Your performance is scored across four criteria, each worth 25% of your total speaking score: Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation. Understanding these criteria is essential because it tells you exactly what the examiner is listening for.

Key Insight

  • The Speaking test is the same for both Academic and General Training IELTS
  • Your test is recorded for monitoring and remarking purposes
  • The examiner is trained to assess your English, not your opinions — there are no "wrong" answers
  • Band 7 requires you to be a "good user" who handles complex language well

Part 1: Introduction and Interview Tips

Part 1 is designed to be the most comfortable section. The examiner will ask you questions about familiar topics — your hometown, your studies or work, your hobbies, daily routines, and preferences. The key here is not to give one-word answers. You need to expand, elaborate, and showcase your language skills from the very first question.

Use the PREP Method

The PREP method stands for Point, Reason, Example, Point. This simple framework ensures you always give a full, structured answer:

  • Point — State your answer clearly
  • Reason — Explain why
  • Example — Give a specific example from your experience
  • Point — Circle back to your main point
Sample Answer — Band 7+

Question: "Do you enjoy your work as a nurse?"

Answer: "Yes, I absolutely love being a nurse. (Point) The main reason is that it gives me a profound sense of purpose every single day. (Reason) For example, last month I cared for an elderly patient who was recovering from surgery, and seeing her walk out of the hospital independently after two weeks was incredibly rewarding. (Example) So yes, despite the challenges, nursing is genuinely fulfilling for me. (Point)"

Part 1 Dos and Don'ts

Do: Speak naturally, use a range of vocabulary, give extended answers, show enthusiasm, and use discourse markers like "actually," "to be honest," and "that reminds me." Don't: Memorise scripts, use overly formal language, give one-word answers, or ask the examiner to repeat questions you understood.

Common Part 1 Topics to Prepare

  • Work/Studies: "What do you do?" "Why did you choose this career?" "What do you enjoy most about your work?"
  • Hometown: "Where are you from?" "What do you like about your hometown?" "Has it changed much?"
  • Free Time: "What do you do in your free time?" "Do you prefer indoor or outdoor activities?"
  • Transport: "How did you get here today?" "What is public transport like where you live?"
  • Food: "What kind of food do you like?" "Do you prefer eating at home or in restaurants?"

Part 2: Long Turn (Cue Card) — Your Secret Weapon

Part 2 is where many candidates struggle, but it is also where you can make the biggest impression. You will receive a cue card with a topic and four bullet points to address. You have one minute to prepare and two minutes to speak. The examiner will not interrupt you during your two minutes.

The 1-Minute Preparation Strategy

That one minute of preparation time is gold. Use it wisely. Write down keywords only — not full sentences. Create a simple mind map with the topic in the centre and branches for each bullet point. Include one vivid example or story you can tell. Think about tense: if describing a past event, ensure you use past tenses consistently.

Sample Cue Card & Answer — Band 8

Topic: Describe a time when you helped someone. You should say:

  • Who you helped
  • How you helped them
  • Why they needed help
  • And explain how you felt about it

Answer: "I would like to talk about a time when I helped a colleague who was struggling with the IELTS exam preparation. She is a fellow nurse from the Philippines who had failed the speaking component twice and was feeling incredibly discouraged. I helped her by setting up a study schedule and practising mock speaking tests with her every evening after our shift. She needed help because she lacked confidence and tended to freeze during Part 2. I felt absolutely delighted when she finally scored Band 7.5 and received her NMC registration. It reinforced my belief that a little support can make a tremendous difference in someone's life."

Structure Your 2 Minutes

Follow this structure for every Part 2 answer: (1) Introduction — paraphrase the topic. (2) Background — set the scene with details. (3) Main story — use specific examples and vivid descriptions. (4) Reflection — explain your feelings or what you learned. This structure naturally fills two minutes and ensures you cover all bullet points.

Pro Tip for Part 2

If you run out of things to say before two minutes, add a reflection: "Looking back on that experience..." or "This taught me that..." If you still have time, extend with: "And I suppose that's something that has stayed with me ever since." Practice with a timer — most candidates finish too early.

Part 3: Two-Way Discussion — Show Your Critical Thinking

Part 3 is where Band 7+ answers separate themselves from Band 6. The examiner asks abstract, analytical questions related to your Part 2 topic. These questions require you to discuss issues in society, not just personal experiences. You need to demonstrate critical thinking.

Use the OPINION Framework

  • Opinion — State your view clearly
  • Perspective — Consider the other side
  • Illustrate — Use examples (personal or general)
  • Nuance — Add complexity to your answer
  • Impact — Discuss consequences or implications
  • Outlook — Conclude with a forward-looking statement
Part 3 Sample — Band 7+

Question: "Do you think people help each other less nowadays than in the past?"

Answer: "That's an interesting question. On the one hand, I would argue that people are actually more connected and willing to help than ever before, thanks to technology and social media platforms that allow us to support causes globally. However, I can also see the opposite perspective — in urban environments, people often seem too preoccupied with their own lives to offer assistance to strangers. From my experience working in hospitals, I have noticed that while professional support networks are strong, casual community bonds may have weakened. Ultimately, I believe the nature of helping has evolved rather than diminished. Perhaps the real challenge is finding a balance between digital connectivity and genuine, face-to-face human compassion."

What Examiners Look for in Band 7+

To achieve Band 7 or above, you must demonstrate competence across all four marking criteria. Here is what each criterion looks like at Band 7:

CriterionBand 6 DescriptionBand 7 Description
Fluency & CoherenceWilling to speak at length though may lose coherenceSpeaks at length without noticeable effort, uses a range of connectives
Lexical ResourceHas a wide enough vocabulary to discuss topicsUses some less common lexical items, shows awareness of style and collocation
Grammatical RangeMix of simple and complex structures with some errorsFrequently produces error-free sentences, uses complex structures flexibly
PronunciationGenerally intelligible throughoutShows all positive features of Band 6 and some of Band 8

Common IELTS Speaking Topics for 2025

While IELTS topics change periodically, certain themes recur consistently. Prepare vocabulary and ideas for these categories:

  • Health & Healthcare: Public health systems, mental health awareness, exercise and diet, healthcare technology
  • Technology: Social media impact, artificial intelligence, remote working, digital education
  • Environment: Climate change, pollution, renewable energy, sustainable living
  • Education: Online learning, traditional vs. modern methods, lifelong learning, university education
  • Work: Career changes, job satisfaction, work-life balance, remote work, retirement
  • Society: Community, volunteering, ageing population, urbanisation, cultural traditions
  • Transport & Travel: Public transport, tourism, electric vehicles, future of travel

Common Mistakes That Lower Your Score

Even strong English speakers make these critical errors:

  • Memorising answers: Examiners can tell immediately. Your speech becomes robotic and unnatural.
  • Overusing fillers: "Um," "ah," and "you know" are acceptable occasionally but should not dominate.
  • Ignoring the question: Always answer what is asked. Going off-topic loses marks for coherence.
  • Simple vocabulary: Repeating "good," "bad," "nice," and "very" limits your lexical resource score.
  • Grammar mistakes with basic structures: Errors with articles, prepositions, and subject-verb agreement are especially costly.
  • Speaking too fast or too slow: Aim for a natural, conversational pace with appropriate pausing.
  • Not using the full 2 minutes in Part 2: If you finish early, you are missing an opportunity to showcase your English.

Replace Basic Words

  • "Good" → excellent, beneficial, advantageous, remarkable, outstanding
  • "Bad" → detrimental, unfavourable, problematic, concerning, undesirable
  • "Very" → extremely, remarkably, considerably, tremendously, exceedingly
  • "Nice" → delightful, pleasant, charming, impressive, appealing
  • "Big" → substantial, considerable, immense, significant, extensive

A Daily Practice Routine That Works

Consistency beats intensity. Follow this daily 30-minute routine:

  1. Minutes 0-5: Warm up by talking about your day aloud in English. Describe what you did, how you felt, what you plan to do tomorrow.
  2. Minutes 5-15: Practice one Part 1 topic. Record yourself answering 5 questions. Listen back and note filler words or grammar mistakes.
  3. Minutes 15-25: Do a full Part 2 practice. Pick a random cue card, time your preparation (1 minute), and speak for the full 2 minutes. Record and review.
  4. Minutes 25-30: Read a news article and form opinions on it. Practice expressing these opinions aloud as if answering Part 3 questions.

Vocabulary and Idioms for Band 7+

Natural, idiomatic language signals to the examiner that you are a comfortable, confident English speaker. Here are expressions organised by function:

Expressing Opinions

  • "From my perspective..." / "As I see it..." / "I am inclined to believe that..."
  • "I am convinced that..." / "It seems to me that..." / "I would argue that..."

Hedging and Nuancing

  • "To some extent..." / "It depends on the context..." / "Broadly speaking..."
  • "There is some truth to the idea that..." / "It is not necessarily the case that..."

Adding Examples

  • "A prime example of this would be..." / "This is perfectly illustrated by..."
  • "To give you a concrete example..." / "Case in point..."

Useful Idioms

  • "It was a real eye-opener" — something that taught you something new
  • "I learned the hard way" — learned through difficult experience
  • "It is a double-edged sword" — has both advantages and disadvantages
  • "To go the extra mile" — to make more effort than expected
  • "It is not rocket science" — it is not difficult to understand

Pronunciation Tips for Clarity

You do not need a British accent to score Band 7+. The examiner assesses:

  • Individual sounds: Practise problematic sounds (/θ/, /ð/, /v/, /w/).
  • Word stress: Incorrect stress can make words unrecognisable. Use online dictionaries to check stress patterns.
  • Sentence stress and intonation: Vary your pitch. Flat intonation sounds disengaged.
  • Chunking: Group words naturally. Pause between ideas, not in the middle of phrases.
  • Connected speech: Practise linking words: "want to" → "wanna," "going to" → "gonna."

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the 3 parts of the IELTS Speaking test?
The IELTS Speaking test has three parts: Part 1 is a general introduction and interview about familiar topics (4-5 minutes). Part 2 requires you to speak about a topic for 2 minutes with 1 minute preparation. Part 3 is a two-way discussion on abstract topics related to Part 2 (4-5 minutes).
How can I improve my IELTS Speaking score to Band 7?
To score Band 7+ in IELTS Speaking, focus on fluency and coherence, use a range of vocabulary including idiomatic expressions, demonstrate grammatical range and accuracy, and work on clear pronunciation. Practice daily with sample questions, record yourself, use discourse markers, and avoid simple 'yes/no' answers.
What topics are common in IELTS Speaking Part 2?
Common IELTS Speaking Part 2 topics include describing a person you admire, a place you have visited, an important decision you made, a favourite book or film, a memorable journey, a skill you want to learn, an achievement you are proud of, and a change you would like to see in your hometown.
Is the IELTS Speaking test the same for Academic and General Training?
Yes. The IELTS Speaking test format and content are identical for both Academic and General Training versions. The test is always a face-to-face interview with a certified examiner.
How long should my Part 2 answer be?
You should speak for the full 2 minutes. If you finish early, the examiner will wait in silence. Use the full time to showcase your English by adding detail, examples, and personal reflections.
Can I use idioms in the IELTS Speaking test?
Yes, using idioms appropriately can boost your lexical resource score. However, only use idioms you are completely confident with. Incorrect usage will lower your score.
