Understanding NHS Interviews

NHS interviews are structured around values-based recruitment, which means interviewers assess not only your clinical competence but also how your personal values align with the NHS Constitution. The NHS employs over 1.4 million people, making it one of the largest employers in the world, and maintaining consistent standards of care across such a vast organisation requires a strong, shared values framework.

Every NHS interview will include questions designed to test:

  • Your understanding of the 6 NHS core values
  • Your knowledge of the 6 C's of nursing (for nursing roles)
  • Your ability to demonstrate values through past experiences
  • Your clinical knowledge and decision-making skills
  • Your understanding of equality, diversity, and safeguarding
  • Your commitment to professional development

The interview typically lasts 30-60 minutes and may involve a panel of 2-4 interviewers including a ward manager, a senior nurse, and a representative from HR or another department. Some interviews also include practical assessments or written tests.

The 6 NHS Core Values

These six values are the foundation of the NHS Constitution. Every answer you give in your interview should reflect at least one of these values.

🤝

Working Together for Patients

Patient care is our highest priority. We work across teams and organisations to deliver coordinated, seamless care that puts patients first.

💙

Respect and Dignity

We value every person as an individual. We respect their privacy, autonomy, and right to be involved in decisions about their care.

⭐

Commitment to Quality of Care

We strive for excellence in everything we do. We continuously improve our skills, services, and outcomes to provide the best possible care.

❤️

Compassion

We respond with humanity and kindness to each person's pain, distress, anxiety, or need. We search for the things we can do to improve their experience.

🛡️

Improving Lives

We work to improve health and wellbeing for individuals, communities, and populations. We aim to prevent illness and promote healthy lifestyles.

👥

Everyone Counts

We maximise our resources for the benefit of the whole community. We make sure nobody is excluded, discriminated against, or left behind.

How to Use Values in Your Answers

When answering any interview question, explicitly connect your response to one or more NHS values. For example: "I always prioritise Respect and Dignity by ensuring patients are fully informed about their care, and I demonstrate Working Together by collaborating closely with the multidisciplinary team." This shows the panel that you understand and internalise the NHS framework.

The 6 C's of Nursing

Introduced by NHS England Chief Nursing Officer Jane Cummings in 2012, the 6 C's define the core values and behaviours expected of all nursing staff.

C

Care

Delivering care is our core function. Care defines us and our work. Patients must be able to trust that they will receive high-quality care consistently.

C

Compassion

Intelligent kindness delivered through relationships based on empathy, respect, and dignity. How care is given matters as much as the clinical act itself.

C

Competence

Having the knowledge, skills, and ability to deliver effective care and treatment based on research and evidence. Continuous professional development.

C

Communication

Central to successful caring relationships and effective teamwork. Listening is as important as what we say. Communication saves lives.

C

Courage

The strength to do the right thing for patients and colleagues, to speak up when we have concerns, and to hold ourselves to account.

C

Commitment

Dedication to our patients and families, to our profession, and to delivering high-quality care. A visible commitment to improving outcomes.

NHS Values Interview Questions & Model Answers

Each question is mapped to a specific NHS value. Study these model answers and adapt them with your own experiences.

Value 1: Working Together for Patients

Questions that assess teamwork, collaboration, and patient-centred care

Tell us about a time you worked effectively as part of a multidisciplinary team.
In my current role, I was caring for a patient with complex needs who required input from physiotherapy, occupational therapy, dietetics, and the discharge team. I recognised that fragmented communication was causing delays in the care plan. I took the initiative to organise a multidisciplinary team (MDT) meeting, ensuring all relevant professionals were present. I prepared a clear summary of the patient's current status, goals, and my nursing observations to make the meeting efficient. As a result, we developed a coordinated discharge plan that reduced the patient's hospital stay by four days while ensuring safe transition to community care. This experience reinforced my belief that Working Together for Patients means taking ownership of communication and actively facilitating collaboration rather than waiting for others to coordinate.
Key: MDT, communication, patient outcome
How would you handle a situation where a colleague is not contributing equally to patient care?
I would first reflect on whether there might be underlying reasons for their behaviour, such as personal difficulties, workload concerns, or a lack of confidence in specific tasks. I would approach them privately and respectfully to offer support: "I have noticed you seem under pressure. Is there anything I can help with?" If the issue persisted and impacted patient care, I would escalate to my line manager following our local policy, always documenting my observations factually. Throughout, my priority would remain patient safety and maintaining a supportive team environment, which reflects the NHS value of Working Together for Patients. I would never ignore behaviour that compromises care, but I would always address it with compassion and professionalism first.
Key: Empathy first, escalate appropriately, documentation

Value 2: Respect and Dignity

Questions about patient autonomy, confidentiality, and cultural sensitivity

Describe a situation where you had to advocate for a patient's dignity.
I was caring for an elderly patient with dementia who became distressed during personal care. She repeatedly said "stop" and tried to push our hands away. I immediately paused the procedure, spoke to her calmly at eye level, and explained each step in simple terms. I asked for her consent before continuing: "Is it okay if we help you get washed now?" I also ensured the bay curtains were fully closed, the door was shut, and only essential staff were present. I documented her preferences and communicated them to the team in handover, ensuring consistency in her care. This approach upholds Respect and Dignity by recognising that every patient has the right to be treated as an individual with their own preferences, regardless of their cognitive capacity.
Key: Consent, communication, documentation, individualised care
How do you ensure you treat patients from diverse cultural backgrounds with respect?
I approach every patient as an individual and avoid making assumptions based on their background. I ask open questions about their cultural or religious needs: "Are there any cultural practices or preferences we should be aware of to make you more comfortable?" I familiarise myself with our Trust's equality and diversity policies and use resources like interpreter services when needed. I once cared for a Muslim patient who required prayer at specific times; I worked with the team to coordinate care around her schedule. By actively listening and adapting care to honour each person's beliefs and preferences, I demonstrate that Everyone Counts and that respect for diversity is integral to person-centred care.
Key: Individualised approach, asking not assuming, adaptation

Value 3: Commitment to Quality of Care

Questions about standards, improvement, and evidence-based practice

Tell us about a time you identified an area for improvement in your clinical practice.
During a medication round, I noticed that several patients were consistently receiving their analgesia late, which affected their pain management and overall comfort. Rather than accepting this as routine, I audited the medication round timing over a two-week period and found that competing priorities during morning shifts were the primary cause. I presented my findings to my ward manager and proposed a pilot of staggered medication rounds, with non-urgent medications administered at slightly different times to allow adequate time for pain relief administration. After implementing this change, on-time analgesia administration improved from 62% to 91%. This demonstrates my Commitment to Quality of Care through proactive identification of problems, evidence-based analysis, and implementation of solutions.
Key: Audit, evidence, solution, measurable outcome
How do you keep your clinical knowledge and skills up to date?
I take a structured approach to continuing professional development. I am registered with the NMC and complete my mandatory training annually. Beyond this, I regularly read nursing journals such as the British Journal of Nursing and Nursing Standard. I attend study days and in-service training offered by my Trust. I am also completing a module in acute care through my university, which contributes toward my degree. I reflect on my practice using a reflective journal and discuss learning points with my mentor. I believe that Commitment to Quality of Care requires a lifelong dedication to learning, as evidence-based practice evolves continuously and our patients deserve care informed by the latest research.
Key: Multiple CPD methods, reflection, evidence-based

Value 4: Compassion

Questions about empathy, emotional intelligence, and patient-centred communication

Describe a time you showed compassion to a patient or their family member.
I was caring for a patient who had received a terminal cancer diagnosis. Her husband was clearly distressed and struggling to process the information. After ensuring my patient's immediate clinical needs were met, I sat with him in a quiet relatives' room. I listened actively as he expressed his fears and asked questions, without rushing him. I offered to contact the palliative care team to discuss his concerns about symptom management at home. I also provided him with information about local support services and the chaplaincy team. Before leaving, I checked when would be a good time for me to return with an update. He later told my matron that this conversation gave him the strength to support his wife. This embodies Compassion — not just clinical care, but responding to emotional needs with humanity and kindness.
Key: Emotional support, active listening, signposting, feedback
How do you maintain compassion when you are under pressure and have many patients to care for?
Pressure is an inevitable part of nursing, but I believe compassion is not about the amount of time spent with a patient — it is about the quality of that interaction. Even during busy shifts, I make a conscious effort to introduce myself, make eye contact, explain what I am doing, and ask patients if they need anything. I use my handover time effectively to prioritise patients with the greatest needs. I also practise self-care outside of work, including exercise and adequate rest, because I recognise that burnout diminishes my capacity for compassion. When I feel overwhelmed, I use our Trust's support services and debrief with colleagues. By looking after myself, I ensure I can consistently provide the Compassionate care every patient deserves.
Key: Quality over quantity, self-care, prioritisation

Value 5: Improving Lives

Questions about health promotion, prevention, and public health

How do you contribute to improving the health and wellbeing of your patients beyond their immediate clinical needs?
I view every patient interaction as an opportunity for health promotion. For example, when caring for a patient admitted with a chest infection who was a smoker, I used motivational interviewing techniques to discuss smoking cessation. I provided NHS Smokefree information and arranged for a referral to the specialist cessation service before discharge. For patients with diabetes, I ensure they understand their condition by using teach-back methods and providing appropriate literature. I also actively participate in community health events, such as blood pressure awareness campaigns, which align with the NHS value of Improving Lives by addressing prevention as well as treatment. Small interventions during hospital admissions can have lasting impacts on patients' long-term health.
Key: Health promotion, motivational interviewing, prevention
Give an example of how you have supported a patient to make positive lifestyle changes.
I cared for a patient with newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes who was anxious about managing his condition. Rather than simply handing him leaflets, I spent time understanding his concerns and daily routine. I identified that he was confused about carbohydrate counting and worried about giving up foods he enjoyed. I arranged a session with the diabetes specialist nurse and dietitian. I also created a simple blood glucose monitoring diary with him and reviewed it together over several days. By discharge, he felt confident managing his condition and had already seen improvements in his readings. This reflects the NHS value of Improving Lives by empowering patients with the knowledge and skills to take control of their health.
Key: Individualised education, empowerment, follow-up

Value 6: Everyone Counts

Questions about equality, fairness, and resource allocation

How do you ensure fairness when allocating your time and attention across multiple patients?
I prioritise based on clinical need using my professional judgement and early warning scores. However, I also recognise that vulnerability is not always captured by acuity scores. I make a conscious effort to check on patients who may be easily overlooked, such as those who are quiet, cognitively impaired, or have limited English proficiency. I use our Trust's interpreter services rather than relying on family members, ensuring accurate communication. I advocate for patients who may struggle to advocate for themselves. By treating every patient as equally deserving of high-quality care, regardless of their background or ability to communicate, I demonstrate that Everyone Counts in the NHS.
Key: Clinical prioritisation + equity, advocacy, inclusion
Describe how you have challenged discrimination or inequality in your workplace.
I overheard a colleague making dismissive comments about a homeless patient's repeated admissions, suggesting they were "wasting a bed." I addressed this privately by reminding them that every patient is entitled to compassionate, non-judgemental care regardless of their circumstances, which is core to the NHS Constitution. I also reported the incident to my ward manager as a values concern, as such attitudes can compromise care quality. I later participated in a team workshop on health inequalities, which I found valuable. Challenging discrimination requires Courage, one of the 6 C's, and is essential to upholding the principle that Everyone Counts in our healthcare system.
Key: Challenged directly, reported appropriately, participated in education

Behavioural Questions: The STAR Method

The STAR method is the gold standard for answering behavioural interview questions in NHS interviews. It provides a clear, structured framework that ensures you cover all essential elements of your example.

The STAR Framework

S — Situation: Briefly describe the context. Where were you? What was happening? Keep this concise (2-3 sentences).

T — Task: What was your responsibility? What needed to be done? Be specific about your role.

A — Action: What did YOU do? Use "I" statements. This should be the longest part of your answer. Detail your specific actions, decisions, and reasoning.

R — Result: What was the outcome? Quantify if possible. What did you learn? How did it improve practice?

Example STAR Answer

Situation: "I was working on a busy medical ward when a patient with a history of falls became agitated and attempted to get out of bed unsupervised."

Task: "My task was to ensure the patient's immediate safety while managing the concerns of other patients on the bay."

Action: "I calmly approached the patient, used de-escalation techniques I had learned in my training, and reassured them. I lowered the bed, activated the sensor mat, and increased observation levels. I also communicated with the medical team to review whether their medication might be contributing to the agitation."

Result: "The patient remained safe, no fall occurred, and the medication review identified a contributing factor that was adjusted. I also shared this learning in our team huddle, and the ward subsequently implemented a falls risk flag system for similar patients."

Band 5 Staff Nurse Interview Questions

Band 5 interviews focus on your foundational nursing skills, understanding of NHS values, and ability to work safely within a team. Expect scenario-based questions testing your clinical reasoning.

You are alone on the ward and a patient becomes acutely unwell. What do you do?
My first action is to ensure the patient is safe: check airway, breathing, and circulation. I would call for help immediately using the emergency bell or asking a colleague to summon the crash team. While waiting, I would follow the ABCDE approach, administer oxygen if indicated, and prepare emergency equipment. I would delegate tasks to available team members and document all actions. Patient safety is always my priority, and I would never delay calling for help out of pride or uncertainty.
How would you prioritise your workload on a busy shift?
I use the ABC prioritisation framework: Airway, Breathing, Circulation first, then time-critical medications, then essential care needs. I communicate with my team about priorities and delegate tasks where appropriate. I reassess priorities throughout the shift as patient conditions change. Effective handover at the start of the shift is essential to understanding which patients need the most attention.

Band 6 Senior Staff Nurse Interview Questions

Band 6 interviews assess your leadership potential, ability to mentor junior staff, and capacity to manage more complex clinical situations.

How would you support a newly qualified nurse who is struggling with their workload?
I would first have a private, supportive conversation to understand the specific challenges they face. I would review their workload allocation and ensure it is appropriate for a newly qualified nurse. I would offer to buddy them with an experienced nurse, schedule regular check-ins, and identify specific training needs. I would also encourage them to use our preceptorship programme. My goal is to build their confidence through graduated exposure while ensuring patient safety and preventing burnout.
Describe how you would lead a shift as the senior nurse.
As the senior nurse, I begin with a comprehensive safety huddle, allocating patients based on acuity and staff competence. I maintain situational awareness, conducting regular ward rounds and being visible and accessible. I support junior staff with clinical decisions, escalate concerns promptly, and ensure effective communication with the multidisciplinary team. I take responsibility for incident management, complaints, and ensuring all safety protocols are followed. At the end of the shift, I conduct a thorough handover.

Band 7 Ward Manager / Charge Nurse Interview Questions

Band 7 interviews focus on strategic leadership, service development, staff management, and financial accountability.

How would you improve patient safety and quality on your ward?
I would begin by analysing current data: incident reports, audit results, patient feedback, and staff surveys. I would involve the whole team in identifying priority areas and co-develop an action plan with clear, measurable objectives. I would implement regular safety huddles, promote a just culture for incident reporting, and ensure robust governance processes. I would monitor progress through monthly dashboards and celebrate improvements to maintain staff engagement. Leadership in quality improvement requires visible commitment, evidence-based decisions, and empowering every team member to contribute.
How do you manage staffing pressures while maintaining safe care?
I use a strategic approach to workforce planning, analysing patient acuity data to ensure safe staffing ratios. I develop a flexible bank and agency strategy, invest in training existing staff for role expansion, and work closely with our workforce team to address recruitment gaps. During immediate shortages, I use our escalation policy, redeploy staff appropriately, and communicate transparently with the team. I also analyse sickness patterns and implement wellbeing initiatives to reduce absence. My priority is always balancing staff wellbeing with patient safety.

Common Interview Mistakes to Avoid

1. Giving Generic Answers

"I am a team player who works hard and cares about patients" tells the panel nothing. Every candidate says this. You must provide specific examples with concrete outcomes.

2. Not Knowing the NHS Values

Failing to mention the 6 NHS values or 6 C's suggests you have not prepared. Explicitly reference them in your answers to show alignment with the organisation.

3. Badmouthing Previous Employers or Colleagues

Even if you had a genuinely difficult experience, speaking negatively about others reflects poorly on your professionalism. Frame challenges as learning experiences.

4. Not Using the STAR Method

Rambling, unstructured answers make it hard for the panel to assess your competence. The STAR method keeps your answers focused and comprehensive.

5. Failing to Ask Questions

When asked "Do you have any questions?" saying no suggests a lack of interest. Prepare 2-3 thoughtful questions about the team, development opportunities, or the ward's priorities.

Expert Tips for NHS Interview Success

Research Your Trust

Review the Trust's latest CQC report, annual priorities, and recent news. Mentioning specific initiatives shows genuine interest and thorough preparation.

Prepare 8-10 STAR Examples

Prepare versatile examples covering teamwork, conflict resolution, patient advocacy, clinical decision-making, and quality improvement. Adapt them to different questions.

Practise Out Loud

Rehearse your answers with a colleague, mentor, or in front of a mirror. This builds confidence and helps you refine your timing and delivery.

Dress Professionally

First impressions matter. Wear professional attire that reflects the seriousness of the role. Arrive 15 minutes early to compose yourself.

Know Your NMC Code

Familiarise yourself with The Code: Professional Standards of Practice and Behaviour for Nurses. Reference specific standards when answering clinical questions.

Show Enthusiasm for Development

NHS employers value candidates committed to learning. Mention specific courses, qualifications, or career pathways you are pursuing or interested in.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the 6 NHS values I need to know?
The 6 NHS values are: (1) Working Together for Patients, (2) Respect and Dignity, (3) Commitment to Quality of Care, (4) Compassion, (5) Improving Lives, and (6) Everyone Counts. These are defined in the NHS Constitution and form the basis of values-based recruitment.
What are the 6 C's of nursing?
The 6 C's are: Care, Compassion, Competence, Communication, Courage, and Commitment. They were introduced by the Chief Nursing Officer for England in 2012 and define the behaviours expected of all nursing staff.
How long does an NHS interview last?
NHS interviews typically last 30 to 60 minutes depending on the band and role. Band 5 interviews are usually 30-45 minutes, while Band 6 and 7 interviews can extend to 45-60 minutes. Some roles also include practical assessments.
What should I wear to an NHS interview?
Dress professionally in business attire. For women, a suit, smart dress, or tailored separates. For men, a suit or smart trousers with a shirt and tie. Even if the ward is casual, your interview attire should reflect the formality of the occasion.
How do I prepare for scenario-based questions?
Practise applying clinical reasoning to common scenarios: deteriorating patients, medication errors, difficult colleagues, safeguarding concerns, and ethical dilemmas. Use the ABC approach for clinical scenarios and always prioritise patient safety. Our AI tool generates realistic scenarios for practice.
Should I mention the NHS values in every answer?
You do not need to explicitly name a value in every single answer, but your responses should demonstrate alignment with the values. Where natural, explicitly reference the relevant value: "This demonstrates Respect and Dignity because..." This shows the panel you understand and live the values.
Can I use the same example for multiple questions?
Ideally, prepare different examples for different competencies. However, a well-structured example can sometimes be adapted to answer different questions by emphasising different aspects of the situation. Ensure your answer directly addresses what was asked.
