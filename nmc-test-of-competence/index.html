What is the NMC Test of Competence?

The Test of Competence (ToC) is the examination that all internationally educated nurses and midwives must pass to register with the Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC) and practice as a registered nurse in the United Kingdom. The test ensures that all nurses, regardless of where they trained, meet the same high standards required for safe and effective practice in the UK.

Overview of the Test of Competence

The Test of Competence has been updated as of August 2021 (Test of Competence 2021). It consists of two parts that assess different aspects of nursing competence.

  • Part 1: CBT — Computer-Based Test taken in your home country
  • Part 2: OSCE — Practical clinical exam taken in the UK
  • Both parts must be passed within 2 years of each other
  • Maximum 3 attempts at each part
  • Tests against NMC Standards of Proficiency for Registered Nurses
  • Based on UK nursing practice and NHS protocols

Who Needs to Take the Test?

The Test of Competence applies to:

  • All nurses trained outside the European Economic Area (EEA)
  • Nurses from countries not covered by EU mutual recognition
  • Nurses re-entering the register after a significant absence
  • This includes nurses from: Philippines, India, Nigeria, Pakistan, Jamaica, Kenya, Ghana, Zimbabwe, UAE, and all non-EEA countries
  • Both adult/general nurses and specialist nurses (mental health, pediatric, learning disability)

Important: Updated Test of Competence 2021

In August 2021, the NMC introduced a new Test of Competence that replaced the previous version. The new test reflects updated NMC standards and is designed to be more reflective of real-world nursing practice. If you took the old CBT before August 2021, you may need to take the new version. Always check the NMC website for the current test blueprint and ensure you're preparing for the right version.

Part 1: CBT (Computer-Based Test)

The CBT is the first part of the Test of Competence. It is a multiple-choice exam that tests your theoretical nursing knowledge and decision-making abilities.

CBT Quick Facts

  • Format: 120 multiple-choice questions
  • Duration: 4 hours (240 minutes)
  • Location: Pearson VUE test centres worldwide
  • Fee: £83
  • Pass mark: Variable, determined by the NMC using standard-setting procedures
  • Results: Typically within 48 hours
  • Attempts: Maximum 3 attempts

CBT Exam Structure

The 120 questions are divided into two parts:

  • Part A: 60 questions covering clinical topics (50% of questions)
    • Assessment and care planning
    • Professional values and decision-making
    • Communication and interpersonal skills
    • Nursing practice and clinical governance
  • Part B: 60 questions covering specific nursing fields
    • Adult nursing specific scenarios
    • Medication calculations
    • Pathophysiology and pharmacology
    • UK-specific nursing protocols and guidelines
  • Questions may include clinical scenarios, images, charts, and calculations
  • All questions must be answered — there is no negative marking

Key CBT Topic Areas

  • Medicines Management: Drug calculations, dosage, side effects, contraindications
  • Assessment: Patient assessment tools, NEWS2 scoring, early warning signs
  • Care Planning: Person-centered care, documentation, care pathways
  • Professional Values: NMC Code, safeguarding, consent, confidentiality, ethics
  • Infection Control: Hand hygiene, PPE, aseptic technique, isolation precautions
  • Mental Capacity: Mental Capacity Act 2005, DOLS, best interests decisions
  • Safeguarding: Adults and children at risk, reporting procedures
  • Communication: Effective communication, documentation, SBAR handover
  • Pathophysiology: Common conditions: diabetes, COPD, heart failure, stroke
  • UK Healthcare System: NHS structure, roles and responsibilities, policies
💡

CBT Preparation Tips

Study the NMC Code and Standards of Proficiency thoroughly — many questions test your understanding of professional values. Practice medication calculations daily — these are high-yield questions. Familiarize yourself with UK-specific protocols (NEWS2, SBAR, 6 Cs of Nursing). Use the Pearson VUE practice tests to get comfortable with the computer interface. Join online study groups on Facebook for peer support. Book your CBT after at least 4-6 weeks of dedicated study.

Part 2: OSCE (Objective Structured Clinical Examination)

The OSCE is a practical clinical examination that tests your nursing skills in simulated scenarios. It is taken in the UK after you have arrived and secured employment.

OSCE Quick Facts

  • Format: 10 stations (4 APIE, 4 Skills, 2 Professional Values)
  • Duration: Approximately 3-4 hours
  • Location: Approved NMC OSCE test centres across the UK
  • Fee: £794 for first attempt
  • Pass requirement: Must pass all stations (with some compensatory marking)
  • Results: Within 5 working days
  • Attempts: Maximum 3 attempts within 2 years of passing CBT

OSCE Station Breakdown

Common OSCE Skills Assessed

  • Vital Signs: BP, pulse, respirations, SpO2, temperature, NEWS2 scoring
  • Medication Administration: Oral, subcutaneous, intramuscular, intravenous
  • IV Therapy: IV cannulation, fluid management, IV medication
  • Wound Care: Wound assessment, dressing application, aseptic technique
  • Urinary Catheterization: Male and female catheter insertion
  • Cardiac Monitoring: ECG, basic life support (BLS/CPR)
  • Manual Handling: Safe patient transfer and positioning
  • Infection Control: Hand hygiene, PPE, aseptic non-touch technique
  • Peak Flow: Respiratory assessment technique
  • Blood Glucose Monitoring: Capillary blood glucose testing

Professional Values Stations

These stations assess your communication and professional judgment in challenging scenarios.

  • Breaking bad news to a patient or relative
  • Communicating with a distressed or confused patient
  • Dealing with a complaint or concern
  • Escalating concerns about patient safety (SPEAK UP)
  • Teamworking scenarios with difficult colleagues
  • Advocating for a patient who cannot advocate for themselves
  • Mental capacity assessment scenarios
  • End-of-life conversations and escalation decisions

Key skills: empathy, active listening, clear communication, ethical decision-making, knowing when and how to escalate

Most NHS Employers Cover OSCE Costs

The vast majority of NHS employers cover the OSCE exam fee (£794) for their recruited international nurses. Many also cover or contribute to CBT costs, provide paid OSCE training (typically 4-6 weeks), and pay for travel and accommodation to the OSCE centre. Always confirm what your employer covers before signing your contract. Private care homes may have different policies — clarify this during your recruitment process.

How to Pass the NMC Test of Competence: Expert Tips

Passing the Test of Competence requires focused preparation. Follow these proven strategies to maximize your chances of passing first time.

CBT Preparation Strategies

  • Download and study the NMC Test of Competence Blueprint thoroughly
  • Master the NMC Code (The Code: Professional standards of practice and behaviour)
  • Study the NMC Standards of Proficiency for Registered Nurses
  • Practice medication calculations daily — at least 10 questions
  • Learn UK-specific nursing frameworks: 6 Cs, SBAR, NEWS2, 5 Rights
  • Understand the Mental Capacity Act 2005 and safeguarding procedures
  • Use Pearson VUE practice tests and familiarization materials
  • Join CBT study groups on Facebook for peer support
  • Read UK nursing guidelines from NICE, RCN, and NHS England
  • Practice time management — 4 hours sounds long but goes quickly
  • Review your incorrect answers to understand your weak areas

OSCE Preparation Strategies

  • Attend your employer's OSCE training program — never skip this
  • Practice clinical skills daily — repetition builds muscle memory
  • Practice with colleagues — peer practice is invaluable
  • Record yourself doing skills and review the recordings
  • Study the APIE framework: Assessment, Planning, Implementation, Evaluation
  • Learn UK-specific protocols and documentation standards
  • Practice explaining procedures to patients in clear, simple English
  • Master NEWS2 scoring — it's essential for APIE stations
  • Practice handover using SBAR format
  • Learn drug calculation formulas and practice under timed conditions
  • Study the difference between your home country's practice and UK practice
  • Get adequate rest before the exam — fatigue affects performance

Common Reasons Nurses Fail the OSCE

Based on test centre data, the most common reasons for failing include: medication calculation errors (always double-check), failure to use aseptic non-touch technique properly, incomplete patient assessments (missing vital signs or observations), poor documentation (not writing everything down), failure to escalate concerns appropriately, not checking patient identity before procedures, and running out of time due to poor time management. Focus your preparation on these high-risk areas.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many times can I take the NMC Test of Competence?

You have a maximum of 3 attempts at the CBT and 3 attempts at the OSCE within 2 years of each other. If you fail the CBT 3 times, you must wait 6 months before reapplying to the NMC. If you fail the OSCE 3 times, you must restart the entire NMC application process. Most nurses (over 85%) pass both parts within their first 2 attempts with proper preparation.

How long does the NMC registration process take?

The complete process typically takes 4-8 months: Initial NMC application and eligibility assessment (2-4 weeks), CBT preparation and testing (2-4 weeks), job application and visa processing (4-8 weeks), arrival in UK and OSCE training (4-6 weeks), OSCE testing (1-2 weeks), and final NMC PIN issuance (1-2 weeks after passing OSCE).

Can I work as a nurse in the UK before passing the OSCE?

Yes, most NHS trusts employ international nurses as pre-registration nurses or healthcare support workers while they prepare for and take their OSCE. You work under supervision and typically earn £18,000-£22,000 during this period. Once you pass the OSCE and receive your NMC PIN, you transition to a Band 5 registered nurse role (£28,407+).

What happens if I fail my OSCE?

If you fail your OSCE, you will receive detailed feedback on which stations you failed and why. Your employer will support you in preparing for your next attempt. You can rebook your OSCE (subject to availability) and typically need to pay the £794 fee again (some employers cover resit costs). Use the feedback to focus your preparation on weak areas. You have 3 attempts total within 2 years of passing your CBT.

Is the NMC Test of Competence different for mental health nurses?

The CBT contains questions relevant to your nursing field (adult, mental health, pediatric, or learning disability). The OSCE scenarios are also tailored to your specific nursing branch. Mental health nurses, for example, will encounter scenarios involving mental health assessments, de-escalation techniques, and therapeutic communication. Make sure the NMC has your correct nursing branch on record.

Can I prepare for the OSCE before arriving in the UK?

Yes, you should start preparing before arrival. Study the NMC Standards, practice clinical skills, learn UK nursing protocols, and watch OSCE preparation videos. However, much of the preparation happens during your employer's OSCE training program in the UK, which typically runs for 4-6 weeks before your exam. This training uses the same equipment and simulates the actual test conditions.

Do I need to take the CBT before or after getting a UK job offer?

You need to pass the CBT before the NMC will issue you an Decision Letter (which confirms you're eligible for registration). Most NHS employers prefer candidates who have already passed their CBT, as this shortens the recruitment timeline. However, some employers will make a conditional offer while you're preparing for the CBT. We recommend passing the CBT as early as possible in your application process.
