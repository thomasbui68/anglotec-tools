NHS Pension Scheme

Annual Leave & Sick Pay

NHS annual leave increases with service, and the sick pay scheme is significantly more generous than private sector employers.

Frequently Asked Questions

▶ What is the starting salary for a newly qualified NHS nurse in 2026?

The starting NHS Band 5 nurse salary in 2026 is £28,834 per year (Pay Point 1 on the Agenda for Change scale). This applies to all newly qualified nurses and internationally recruited nurses entering the NHS at Band 5. With London weighting, this increases to £34,601 in Inner London, £33,159 in Outer London, and £30,276 in Fringe areas.

▶ How much does an NHS Band 5 nurse take home per month?

A Band 5 nurse at the starting salary of £28,834 takes home approximately £1,870–£1,950 per month after deductions. This is after income tax (20% on earnings above £12,570), National Insurance (8% on earnings above £12,570), and NHS pension contribution (5% of salary). Your exact take-home pay depends on your tax code, student loan repayments (if applicable), and any additional pension contributions you choose to make.

▶ How quickly can I progress from Band 5 to Band 6?

Most nurses can progress from Band 5 to Band 6 within 18 months to 3 years. To move up, you'll typically need to: complete your preceptorship year, demonstrate competence in your role, take on additional responsibilities, complete relevant training or courses, and apply for Band 6 vacancies when they arise. Some trusts offer internal development programmes that fast-track progression. Band 6 salaries start at £35,922, so the move represents a significant pay increase of over £7,000.

▶ Do internationally recruited nurses start at Band 5?

Yes — virtually all internationally recruited nurses start at NHS Band 5, regardless of their experience overseas. This is because the NHS needs to assess your clinical competence within the UK healthcare system before promoting you. However, your previous experience is taken into account when placing you on the pay scale: nurses with significant overseas experience may start at Pay Point 2 (£30,348) or Point 3 (£31,776) rather than Point 1. After 6–12 months of satisfactory performance, many trusts will review your pay point placement.

▶ Is the NHS Band 5 salary enough to live on in the UK?

Yes — the NHS Band 5 salary of £28,834 is a living wage across most of the UK. Outside London, monthly living costs (rent, bills, food, transport) typically range from £1,200–£1,600, leaving £300–£700 for savings, sending money home, or discretionary spending. In London, higher rents mean tighter budgeting, but the London weighting (£34,601 in Inner London) significantly helps. Many nurses share accommodation initially to reduce costs. After 2–3 years at Band 6, most nurses find themselves very comfortable financially.

▶ How much overtime can NHS nurses work?

There's no fixed limit on overtime, but NHS trusts must comply with Working Time Regulations. Most trusts limit overtime to ensure patient safety and staff wellbeing. Overtime rates are generous: Saturdays pay 1.3x, Sundays 1.6x, and bank holidays 2.0x your hourly rate. Night shifts (8pm–6am) receive a 37% enhancement. Many Band 5 nurses increase their annual earnings by £3,000–£6,000 through regular overtime and weekend working.

▶ Do NHS nurses get pay rises every year?

Yes — NHS nurses receive two types of pay increases. First, you automatically progress one pay point each year (e.g., from Point 1 to Point 2), increasing your salary by approximately £1,500. Second, the government negotiates annual pay rises for all NHS staff, typically 1–5% depending on inflation and political decisions. In 2025/26, a 5.5% pay rise was implemented. These increases are applied to the entire pay scale, meaning all pay points go up.

▶ How does NHS nurse pay compare to other countries?

NHS nurse salaries are competitive internationally. Band 5 starting pay (£28,834) converts to approximately: $36,500 USD, €33,500 EUR, or AU$55,000. When factoring in the pension, benefits, and free healthcare, total compensation is often higher than in countries with higher base salaries but fewer benefits. Nurses in the US may earn more in some states but typically have much worse pensions, less job security, and higher healthcare costs. Australian nurses earn comparable amounts but with a higher cost of living.
