Understanding the Band 6 Nurse Role

A Band 6 nurse is a senior staff nurse or junior sister/charge nurse position within the NHS Agenda for Change pay system. This role represents a significant step up from Band 5, involving greater clinical responsibility, leadership duties, and often specialist clinical skills.

Band 6 roles typically include: team leadership, mentorship and supervision of junior staff, care coordination, quality improvement participation, and specialist clinical practice. Understanding these expectations is essential because interview questions are designed to assess your readiness for each of these responsibilities.

Before your interview, thoroughly read the job description and person specification. Identify the essential and desirable criteria. Every answer you give should demonstrate how you meet these criteria with specific examples.

NHS Values: The Foundation of Every Answer

The NHS Constitution defines six core values that underpin everything the NHS does. Your interview answers must demonstrate these values explicitly. Examiners are often marking against a values-based framework.

The Six NHS Values

1. Working together for patients: Patient care comes first. Show how you collaborate across teams and put patient needs above everything else.

2. Respect and dignity: Treat every patient with respect, dignity, and compassion. Show how you value diversity and individual choice.

3. Commitment to quality of care: Strive for excellence, continuous improvement, and evidence-based practice. Show your dedication to high standards.

4. Compassion: Respond to patients with humanity and kindness. Show empathy in your examples — not just clinical competence.

5. Improving lives: Focus on making a positive difference to patient outcomes and experiences. Show innovation and proactive care.

6. Everyone counts: Value every person equally, maximise resources, and don't discriminate. Show equity and fairness in your decision-making.

Values Integration: Weave NHS Values into every answer. For example: "I demonstrated compassion by sitting with the patient and listening to their concerns, and I showed working together for patients by liaising with the multidisciplinary team."

The STAR Method: Structuring Perfect Answers

The STAR method is the gold standard for answering behavioural interview questions. It provides a clear structure that ensures you give complete, evidence-based answers without rambling.

Situation: Set the scene. Where were you? What was your role? Keep this brief — 1-2 sentences maximum.
Task: What was required of you? What was the challenge or objective? Again, keep this concise — 1-2 sentences.
Action: What did you specifically do? Use "I" not "we." This is the longest part of your answer — 3-5 sentences detailing your specific contributions.
Result: What was the outcome? Quantify where possible. What did you learn? How did it change your practice? End on a positive, reflective note.

Example STAR Answer

Question: "Tell me about a time you dealt with a difficult colleague."

Situation: "In my current role on the acute medical ward, I was working with a healthcare assistant who was consistently arriving late for shifts."

Task: "I needed to address the situation while maintaining team cohesion and ensuring patient care wasn't affected."

Action: "I arranged a private conversation at a neutral time. I started by asking if everything was okay, as the lateness was unusual. It transpired they were having childcare difficulties. I explained the impact on the team without being accusatory. Together we explored solutions, including adjusting their start time by 30 minutes temporarily and helping them access our trust's childcare support service."

Result: "Their attendance improved significantly within two weeks. The experience taught me that difficult behaviours often have underlying causes, and that a supportive approach is usually more effective than disciplinary action. I now apply this principle when managing any team issue."

Clinical Scenario Questions: How to Approach Them

Scenario-based questions present a clinical situation and ask what you would do. These assess your clinical reasoning, prioritisation, and decision-making under pressure.

Example Scenario: Deteriorating Patient

Scenario: "You are the Band 6 nurse in charge. A patient on your ward who was admitted with a chest infection has become increasingly breathless over the past hour. Their observations are: RR 28, SpO2 88% on air, HR 115, BP 95/60. The FY1 doctor is busy with another unwell patient. What do you do?"

Good Answer Structure:

1. Immediate safety: "I would recognise this as a deteriorating patient using the NEWS2 score. The combined observations indicate significant respiratory compromise and possible sepsis."

2. Immediate actions: "I would ensure the patient is in a safe position, administer oxygen to maintain SpO2 above 94%, ensure IV access, and request immediate bloods including FBC, U&E, CRP, and blood cultures."

3. Escalation: "I would use the SBAR framework to communicate with the FY1 and, if they're unavailable, escalate directly to the medical registrar as per the sepsis pathway. I would not wait — sepsis requires immediate action."

4. Broader management: "I would initiate the sepsis six bundle, document everything, inform the relatives, and ensure continuous monitoring."

ABCDE Approach: For any acute scenario, structure your answer using the ABCDE (Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure) approach. This demonstrates systematic clinical assessment and is what examiners want to hear.

Leadership Questions: Demonstrating Band 6 Readiness

Leadership is the key differentiator between Band 5 and Band 6. Your answers must show you can lead, not just participate.

Delegation Framework

When asked about delegation, use the 5 Rights of Delegation:

  • Right task: Is this appropriate to delegate?
  • Right person: Does this person have the competence?
  • Right circumstances: Is this the right time and context?
  • Right direction: Have I given clear instructions?
  • Right supervision: Will I monitor appropriately and be available?

Conflict Resolution

Use the DESC model: Describe the situation, Express your concerns, Suggest alternatives, Consequences (positive). This shows a structured, professional approach to difficult conversations.

Safeguarding Questions: Essential Knowledge

Safeguarding questions assess your knowledge of legal frameworks and your ability to act when you have concerns.

Key Principles

Adults: The Care Act 2014 sets out the legal framework. The six principles are: Empowerment, Prevention, Proportionality, Protection, Partnership, Accountability.

Children: Working Together to Safeguard Children (2018) is the key guidance. Remember: the welfare of the child is paramount.

Your answer should always include: Recognising signs, documenting concerns, reporting to the designated safeguarding lead, and never investigating yourself.

Never Say: "I would investigate the concern myself" or "I would wait for more evidence before reporting." Safeguarding concerns must be reported immediately to the designated safeguarding lead, even if you're unsure.

How to Prepare for Your Band 6 Interview

Research Phase (1-2 weeks before)

  • Study the job description and person specification thoroughly
  • Research the trust: values, recent CQC report, key priorities, challenges
  • Learn about the specific ward/department: specialities, patient demographics
  • Review the NHS Constitution and 6 Values
  • Refresh your knowledge of relevant policies (safeguarding, sepsis, falls, dementia care)

Practice Phase (1 week before)

  • Prepare STAR examples for at least 15 common questions
  • Practise answering aloud — don't just think about answers
  • Record yourself and review for clarity, pace, and filler words
  • Practise with a colleague or mentor who can give feedback
  • Time your answers — 2-3 minutes per question is ideal

Final Preparation (day before)

  • Review your prepared examples
  • Prepare your outfit (professional, comfortable)
  • Plan your journey with extra time
  • Prepare questions to ask the panel
  • Get a good night's sleep

Interview Presentation Tips

Many Band 6 interviews include a presentation component. Common topics include: "What I would bring to this role," "A quality improvement project I have led," or "How I would develop this service."

Presentation Structure

1. Introduction (10%): Who you are, what you'll cover.

2. Context (20%): Set the scene — why this topic matters.

3. Content (50%): Your main points with evidence/examples.

4. Conclusion (15%): Summarise key messages.

5. Call to action (5%): Why you're the right person.

Presentation Tip: Keep slides minimal (6-8 max). Use visuals, not text. Practise your timing — going over the allocated time suggests poor planning. Leave time for questions.

Intelligent Questions to Ask the Panel

At the end of your interview, you'll be invited to ask questions. This is not optional — asking thoughtful questions demonstrates engagement and serious interest.

Good Questions to Ask

  • "What are the key priorities for this ward/department in the next 12 months?"
  • "How does the trust support Band 6 nurses to develop leadership skills?"
  • "What does success look like in this role at the 6-month review?"
  • "How does the team approach quality improvement and learning from incidents?"
  • "What opportunities are there for specialist training and development?"

Questions to Avoid

  • Questions about pay or annual leave (save for after the offer)
  • Questions answered in the job description (shows lack of preparation)
  • Questions about reducing your responsibilities

Frequently Asked Questions About Band 6 Interviews

How long does an NHS Band 6 interview last?

Typically 45-60 minutes, though this varies by trust. Some include a presentation (10-15 minutes) followed by a panel interview (30-45 minutes). Check your invitation letter for the specific format.

How many people will be on the interview panel?

Usually 3-4 people: a ward manager or matron, a senior nurse from another area, a HR representative, and sometimes a medical colleague or patient representative. The chair will introduce everyone at the start.

What should I wear to an NHS Band 6 interview?

Professional business attire. A suit or smart dress for women, suit and tie for men. Even if the ward dress code is scrubs, your interview attire should be formal. First impressions matter.

Can I use notes during the interview?

It's generally better not to rely on notes. However, having a small notebook with key points you want to cover is acceptable. Don't read from it — use it as a prompt only.

How soon will I hear back after the interview?

Most trusts inform candidates within 5-10 working days. Some may call the same day. If you haven't heard within two weeks, it's appropriate to contact HR for an update.

What if I don't get the job?

Always request feedback. Ask specifically: "What would have strengthened my application?" and "What should I focus on for future interviews?" Use this feedback to improve. Many successful Band 6 nurses were unsuccessful in their first interview.
