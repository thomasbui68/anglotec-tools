What is Agency Nursing?

Agency nursing involves working through a recruitment agency that supplies nurses to NHS trusts, private hospitals, care homes, and other healthcare settings on a temporary basis. Agency nurses fill staffing gaps caused by sickness, annual leave, vacancies, or seasonal demand. In return, they receive significantly higher hourly rates than permanent staff nurses. Agency nursing offers flexibility, variety, and the potential to earn 50-100% more than a standard Band 5 salary. For many nurses, it is a lifestyle choice; for others, it is a stepping stone between permanent roles or a way to gain experience across multiple specialties.

Pros and Cons of Agency Nursing

Advantages

  • Significantly higher pay than permanent roles
  • Complete flexibility over when you work
  • Choose your shifts and locations
  • Experience different wards and hospitals
  • No office politics or long-term commitments
  • Can take extended breaks between contracts
  • Opportunity to travel across the UK
  • Some agencies offer benefits packages

Disadvantages

  • No guaranteed hours (work is ad-hoc)
  • No NHS pension contributions
  • No sick pay or annual leave (unless umbrella)
  • Responsible for your own tax (if LTD company)
  • Constantly adapting to new wards and teams
  • No career progression structure
  • Travel between assignments
  • IR35 tax considerations

Getting Started as an Agency Nurse

1

Meet the Requirements

Before you can register with an agency, you must meet certain minimum requirements. Most agencies require at least 6 months of post-registration experience in the UK, though some accept newly qualified nurses for certain roles.

Minimum Requirements

  • NMC PIN (active registration)
  • Minimum 6 months UK post-registration experience (preferably 12+)
  • Right to work in the UK
  • DBS certificate (less than 12 months old)
  • Occupational health clearance
  • Core training certificates (BLS, manual handling, safeguarding)
  • Professional indemnity insurance
  • 2-3 clinical references
2

Choose Your Employment Structure

Agency nurses can work through three main structures, each with different tax implications and benefits. Choose the one that best suits your circumstances.

Option 1: PAYE (Pay As You Earn)

The agency pays you directly, deducting tax and National Insurance at source. Simple, but the lowest take-home pay. Some agencies offer limited benefits (sick pay, holiday pay).

Option 2: Umbrella Company

You are employed by an umbrella company that contracts with the agency. Tax and NI are deducted. You may claim some expenses. Some umbrellas offer benefits. Most popular option for agency nurses.

Option 3: Limited Company

You set up your own limited company and contract directly. Highest potential take-home pay but more complex. You handle your own tax, VAT, and accounts. Best for nurses working full-time agency. Seek accountant advice.

3

Register with Agencies

Register with multiple agencies to maximise your shift opportunities. The UK has dozens of nursing agencies, from large nationals to smaller specialist agencies.

Major UK Nursing Agencies

  • agencies like HCL, MSI, and Thornberry
  • NHS Professionals (staff bank for NHS trusts)
  • Specialist agencies for ICU, A&E, and theatres
  • Local agencies with strong relationships with specific trusts

Registration Process

Each agency will require: completed application form, CV, NMC PIN verification, DBS check, occupational health clearance, training certificates, references, and a compliance interview. The process typically takes 2-4 weeks.

4

Maximise Your Agency Income

Experienced agency nurses use strategies to maximise their earnings while maintaining work-life balance.

Income Maximisation Tips

  • Work nights and weekends for premium rates
  • Specialise in high-demand areas (ITU, A&E, theatres)
  • Build relationships with ward managers for regular bookings
  • Be reliable: cancellations damage your reputation
  • Work across multiple agencies for more opportunities
  • Consider working in London for higher rates
  • Book blocks of shifts (e.g., 5 nights in a row) efficiently
  • Negotiate rates after proving yourself on a ward
5

Understand IR35 and Tax

IR35 is UK tax legislation that affects contractors working through limited companies. Agency nurses must understand how IR35 applies to them to avoid unexpected tax bills.

IR35 Key Points

IR35 determines whether you are genuinely self-employed or a "disguised employee." If your contract falls "inside IR35," you will be taxed as an employee. Most agency nursing contracts are now assessed for IR35 status by the trust or agency. Seek professional advice from a contractor accountant.

Is Agency Nursing Right for You?

Agency nursing suits nurses who value flexibility and higher pay over job security and benefits. It is ideal for: nurses between permanent roles, nurses seeking variety, those funding travel or further education, and nurses in high-demand specialties wanting to maximise income.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much more do agency nurses earn than permanent staff?

Agency nurses typically earn 50-100% more per hour than permanent staff. A Band 5 staff nurse earns approximately 18-20 GBP/hour, while an agency Band 5 earns 25-35 GBP/hour. Specialist agency nurses can earn significantly more.

Can newly qualified nurses do agency work?

Most agencies require at least 6 months of post-registration experience. Some accept newly qualified nurses for less acute settings, but most wards prefer experienced agency nurses who can hit the ground running.

Do agency nurses get NHS pension?

No, agency nurses do not receive NHS pension contributions unless they also work in the NHS staff bank. You should make your own pension arrangements, particularly if agency nursing is your long-term plan.

Can I combine agency work with a permanent NHS job?

Yes, many permanent NHS nurses work extra agency shifts on their days off. Check your permanent contract for any restrictions on working for other employers. You must also manage your working hours within safe limits.

What specialties pay the most for agency nurses?

ITU/ICU, A&E, theatres (scrub and recovery), and specialist areas like endoscopy and cardiac catheterisation typically command the highest agency rates. Mental health and community nursing generally offer lower rates but more regular work.
