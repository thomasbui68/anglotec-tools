The NHS stands for the National Health Service. It is the publicly funded healthcare system in the UK, providing comprehensive medical care free at the point of use. It is one of the largest employers in the world.
A Band 5 nurse is a newly qualified registered nurse in the NHS Agenda for Change pay structure. It is the standard entry-level grade for registered nurses with full NMC registration.
As of 2025, Band 5 NHS nurses earn between approximately £29,000 and £36,000 per year. With London weighting or enhancements for unsocial hours, this can be higher.
Yes, all nurses must be registered with the Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC) before they can work as a registered nurse in the NHS. This requires passing the CBT, OSCE, and meeting English language requirements.
You need either IELTS Academic with 7.0 in all sections (or 6.5 in writing with 7.0 in others) or OET with Grade B in all sub-tests. These must be achieved in a single sitting for NMC registration.
International nurses typically need a Health and Care Worker Visa. This is a sponsored visa that allows you to work in the NHS. Your employer must be a licensed sponsor.
You can apply through NHS Jobs (jobs.nhs.uk), recruitment agencies, or directly on NHS Trust websites. You will need your NMC registration or proof that you are in the process of obtaining it.
The CBT (Computer-Based Test) is the first part of the NMC registration process. It is a multiple-choice theory exam taken at a Pearson VUE test centre, testing your nursing knowledge and decision-making skills.
The OSCE (Objective Structured Clinical Examination) is the second part of NMC registration. It is a practical clinical exam with 10 stations testing your hands-on nursing skills in a UK clinical context.
The full NMC registration process, from initial application to passing the OSCE, typically takes 3 to 6 months. This includes document verification, CBT, travel to the UK, and OSCE preparation and testing.
Yes, many international nurses work as healthcare assistants (HCAs) or nursing assistants while completing their NMC registration. This provides valuable UK healthcare experience.
You need: your passport, nursing degree certificate, nursing licence from your home country, English test results, NMC registration evidence (or proof of application), CV, and references.
Many NHS Trusts offer relocation packages for international nurses, including flight reimbursement, initial accommodation, OSCE training, and support with NMC registration costs. Packages vary by Trust.
Agenda for Change is the NHS pay and grading system. It sets out nine pay bands. Nurses typically start at Band 5 and can progress to Band 6 (specialist), Band 7 (advanced), and higher with experience and further qualifications.
Yes, your partner and children under 18 can apply as dependants. They can work and study in the UK. You will need to show you can support them financially.
NHS nurses typically work 37.5 hours per week. This is usually spread over shifts including days, nights, and weekends. Full-time contracts include paid annual leave and pension contributions.
The cost of living varies by location. Outside London, a single nurse can expect monthly costs of approximately £1,200 to £1,600 including rent, utilities, food, and transport. London costs are significantly higher.
The NMC OSCE costs £794 for the first attempt. Some NHS employers cover or reimburse this cost as part of their relocation package. Re-sit fees are £397 for partial and £794 for full.
Many NHS Trusts provide paid OSCE preparation courses, study materials, mock exams, clinical skills training, and dedicated study time before your OSCE. Check what your employing Trust offers.
Yes, after gaining experience as a Band 5 nurse, you can apply for specialist roles at Band 6 or Band 7, pursue postgraduate qualifications, or enter specialist training programmes in areas like critical care, oncology, or midwifery.
