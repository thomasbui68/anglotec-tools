Overview of OET Reading

The OET Reading sub-test lasts 60 minutes and consists of three parts with a total of 42 questions. The test is designed to assess your ability to read and understand different types of healthcare-related texts, ranging from short workplace communications to longer articles on medical topics.

Unlike general English reading tests, OET Reading uses authentic healthcare materials. You will encounter hospital policies, patient information leaflets, medical journal articles, and internal communications. This profession-specific approach means that nursing knowledge can genuinely help you understand the context, though the questions always test reading skills, not medical expertise.

A score of 350 (Grade B) is required by most nursing regulators, including the UK's Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC). This typically requires answering at least 30-32 questions correctly, though the exact conversion can vary slightly between test administrations.

OET Reading at a Glance

  • Duration: 60 minutes
  • Total questions: 42
  • Parts: 3 distinct sections
  • Passing score: 350 (Grade B) - approximately 30+ correct
  • Texts: Authentic healthcare materials

Part A: Expeditious Reading

Part A tests your ability to locate specific information quickly across multiple texts. You have 15 minutes to answer 20 questions based on 4 short texts (approximately 650 words total). This part is highly time-pressured and requires efficient scanning techniques.

Part A Question Types

Matching (Questions 1-8 typically)

Match questions or statements to the correct text (A, B, C, or D). Each text may be used once, more than once, or not at all.

Short Answer (Questions 9-14 typically)

Answer using a word or short phrase from the text. There will be a word limit (e.g., NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS).

Sentence Completion (Questions 15-20 typically)

Complete sentences using information from the texts. Again, stick to the word limit specified.

Part A Strategy

  1. 1. Glance at all 4 texts (30 seconds): Note the title and type of each text (e.g., drug information, symptoms, treatment guidelines).
  2. 2. Read the first question: Identify the key words you're looking for.
  3. 3. Scan the texts for key words: Your eyes should move quickly over the text looking for the specific information.
  4. 4. Answer and move on: Don't read every word. Locate the information, write the answer, and go to the next question.
  5. 5. Skip difficult questions: If you can't find an answer within 30 seconds, move on and return later.

Part B: Careful Reading - Short Texts

Part B consists of 6 short workplace texts (100-150 words each), with one multiple-choice question per text. The texts are authentic healthcare communications: hospital memos, policy extracts, guidelines, patient information, and staff notices.

Part B Strategy

Step 1: Read the question first

Know what you're looking for before reading the text.

Step 2: Skim the text for gist

Read quickly to understand the main message. Note who wrote it and why.

Step 3: Read all options carefully

Eliminate obviously wrong answers. Be careful of options that use exact words from the text - these are often distractors.

Step 4: Choose the best paraphrase

The correct answer often uses different words from the text (synonyms) but conveys the same meaning.

Common Part B Text Types

Hospital Policy Updates

Changes to visiting hours, hand hygiene protocols, discharge procedures, medication administration policies.

Staff Memos & Notices

Training announcements, shift changes, equipment maintenance, new staff introductions.

Patient Information

Pre-operative instructions, post-discharge advice, appointment reminders, clinic schedules.

Guidelines & Protocols

Wound care protocols, infection control procedures, emergency response steps, referral pathways.

Part C: Careful Reading - Long Texts

Part C is the most challenging section. It consists of 2 long texts (approximately 800 words each), with 8 multiple-choice questions per text (16 total). These texts are typically taken from medical journals, research reviews, or opinion pieces on healthcare topics. The questions test your ability to understand opinions, attitudes, implications, and the writer's purpose.

Part C Question Types

Gist/Opinion Questions

"The writer's main purpose is to..." / "What is the writer's attitude toward...?" These require understanding the overall argument and tone.

Detail Questions

"According to the text, which of the following is true about...?" These test specific information comprehension.

Inference Questions

"The writer implies that..." / "It can be inferred from the text that..." These require reading between the lines.

Reference Questions

"The word 'it' in line 15 refers to..." / "What does 'this approach' refer to?" These test understanding of text cohesion.

Part C Strategy

  1. 1. Read the title and subheadings: Get a sense of the topic and structure before reading.
  2. 2. Skim the entire text (2-3 minutes): Read quickly to understand the main argument and structure. Don't worry about every word.
  3. 3. Read questions for Text 1: Underline key words in the questions but NOT the options yet.
  4. 4. Read Text 1 carefully: Now read at a normal pace, keeping the questions in mind.
  5. 5. Answer the questions: Eliminate wrong options. Be suspicious of options with exact text matches - paraphrases are more likely correct.
  6. 6. Repeat for Text 2.

Skimming & Scanning Techniques

These two techniques are fundamental to OET Reading success. Mastering them will dramatically improve your speed and accuracy.

Skimming (for gist)

Used to get the general idea of a text quickly.

  • - Read the title and any subheadings
  • - Read the first sentence of each paragraph
  • - Read the final paragraph or conclusion
  • - Note any highlighted/bold terms
  • - Ignore unfamiliar words
  • - Aim: understand the main topic in 60-90 seconds

Scanning (for specific information)

Used to find specific details like dates, names, or numbers.

  • - Know exactly what you're looking for
  • - Move your eyes quickly across the text
  • - Look for key words from the question
  • - Use your finger or pen to guide your eyes
  • - Stop only when you find the relevant section
  • - Read the surrounding context carefully

Medical Vocabulary for OET Reading

Familiarity with common medical terminology will improve both your speed and comprehension:

Prefixes

hyper- (excessive), hypo- (deficient), brady- (slow), tachy- (fast), dys- (difficult/painful), anti- (against), neo- (new)

Root Words

cardi- (heart), pneumo- (lung/lung), hepato- (liver), nephro- (kidney), gastro- (stomach), neuro- (nerve), osteo- (bone)

Suffixes

-itis (inflammation), -ectomy (removal), -oscopy (viewing), -emia (blood condition), -uria (urine), -algia (pain), -plasty (repair)

Common Abbreviations

BP (blood pressure), HR (heart rate), RR (respiratory rate), SpO2 (oxygen saturation), CRP (C-reactive protein), UTI (urinary tract infection), COPD (chronic obstructive pulmonary disease)

Nursing Terms

assessment, intervention, evaluation, documentation, handover, escalation, protocol, compliance, adherence, holistic

Academic Verbs

demonstrate, indicate, suggest, establish, evaluate, assess, compare, investigate, conclude, acknowledge

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Spending too long on Part A

Part A has a fixed 15-minute limit. Practice finishing within this time. If you're stuck, guess and move on.

Mistake 2: Reading every word

OET Reading rewards strategic reading, not thorough reading. Skim for gist, scan for details, and only read carefully when you need to answer a specific question.

Mistake 3: Choosing answers that match words exactly

In Parts B and C, correct answers are usually paraphrased. If an option uses the exact same words as the text, it's likely a distractor.

Mistake 4: Using knowledge instead of the text

Always base your answers on what the text says, not on your nursing knowledge. The text is always correct for the purposes of the test.

Mistake 5: Poor time distribution

Many candidates spend too long on Part B and rush Part C. Part C carries the most marks per question - allocate your time accordingly.

4-Week Study Plan

Week 1: Foundation

- Learn the test format and question types
- Practise skimming and scanning techniques
- Complete 2 Part A practice sets under timed conditions
- Build medical vocabulary (20 terms per day)

Week 2: Part A Focus

- Complete 4-5 Part A practice sets
- Analyse errors: was it vocabulary, timing, or technique?
- Practise matching and short answer questions
- Continue vocabulary building

Week 3: Parts B & C Focus

- Practise Part B multiple-choice strategies
- Work through 3-4 Part C texts with full analysis
- Focus on paraphrase recognition
- Practise full tests under timed conditions

Week 4: Full Practice & Review

- Complete 3-4 full reading tests (60 minutes each)
- Review all mistakes carefully
- Refine time management strategy
- Practise with authentic healthcare texts online

Frequently Asked Questions

How many questions do I need to get right for a B grade?

You typically need 30-32 correct answers out of 42 for a Grade B (350+). The exact conversion may vary slightly between test versions.

Is there extra time to transfer answers?

No. For Part A, you write answers directly on the answer sheet during the 15 minutes. For Parts B and C, you mark answers as you go since they share the 45-minute timeframe.

Can I use a dictionary?

No dictionaries or reference materials are permitted. Building your medical vocabulary before the test is essential.

Does my nursing knowledge help?

Yes, background knowledge helps you understand the context, but you must base all answers on the text provided, not on what you know.

How can I improve my reading speed?

Read healthcare articles daily (BMJ, Nursing Times). Practise timed reading. Focus on skimming and scanning rather than reading every word. Build your vocabulary - unfamiliar words slow you down.

What if I don't finish all questions?

Never leave questions blank. There is no penalty for wrong answers, so guess if you run out of time. Develop a time management strategy and stick to it.
