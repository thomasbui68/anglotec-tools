Understanding the OET: What You're Really Being Tested On

The Occupational English Test (OET) is not a general English exam. Unlike IELTS or TOEFL, the OET is designed specifically for healthcare professionals and evaluates your ability to communicate effectively in medical contexts. This distinction is crucial — and it's why so many candidates with excellent general English skills still struggle.

The exam tests four sub-tests: Writing, Speaking, Reading, and Listening. Each sub-test simulates real workplace scenarios. For example, the Writing sub-test requires you to write a referral letter based on case notes — something you might actually do in clinical practice. The Speaking sub-test involves two role-play scenarios where you interact with a patient or carer.

The OET uses a grading system from A (highest) to E (lowest). Most regulatory bodies require at least a Grade B (350+) in each sub-test, though some accept Grade C+ in certain sections. Always check the specific requirements of your target registration body before you begin preparing.

Why Most Candidates Fail the OET First Time

Research from the OET Centre reveals that approximately 35-40% of candidates do not achieve their required grade on the first attempt. Understanding why is the first step toward ensuring you don't become part of that statistic.

The Top Reasons for OET Failure

1. Treating OET Like IELTS — Many candidates apply IELTS strategies to OET and fail. OET Writing requires specific letter formats, professional tone, and selective information transfer from case notes. IELTS essay structures simply don't apply.

2. Poor Time Management in Reading — The Reading sub-test gives you 60 minutes for 42 questions across three parts. Part A is particularly time-pressured (15 minutes for 20 questions). Candidates who haven't practised under timed conditions consistently run out of time.

3. Ignoring the Assessment Criteria — Each sub-test has publicly available assessment criteria. Yet most candidates never study them. For Writing, the criteria include Purpose, Content, Conciseness & Clarity, Genre & Style, Organisation & Layout, and Language. Knowing exactly what examiners look for transforms your preparation.

4. Insufficient Speaking Practice — The Speaking role-plays require specific interactional skills: relationship building, structuring the consultation, showing empathy, and explaining medical concepts simply. Reading about these skills isn't enough — you need deliberate practice with feedback.

5. Weak Listening for Specific Information — OET Listening Part A requires extracting specific details from consultations. Candidates who rely on general listening comprehension rather than targeted note-taking strategies miss crucial information.

Avoid This Mistake: Don't wait until you feel "ready" to take your first mock test. Take a diagnostic test in your first week of preparation to identify your weakest areas. Many candidates discover their weaknesses too late.

Your 12-Week OET Study Plan to Pass First Time

A structured study plan is non-negotiable if you want to pass OET first time. This 12-week plan assumes approximately 15-20 hours of study per week and targets all four sub-tests systematically.

Weeks 1-3: Foundation Building

Focus on understanding the exam format and assessment criteria. Take a full diagnostic mock test in Week 1 to establish your baseline scores. Study the official OET assessment criteria for each sub-test. Begin building your medical vocabulary by profession — nurses need different terminology than doctors or pharmacists.

During this phase, complete at least two practice tasks per sub-test per week. Don't worry about timing yet; focus on understanding what each task requires. Review sample answers for Writing and Speaking to internalise the expected standard.

Weeks 4-6: Skill Development

Now begin timed practice. For Reading, practise Part A under the 15-minute time limit. For Listening, practise note-taking while audio plays only once. Writing practice should include peer or tutor feedback — self-assessment alone isn't sufficient.

Speaking practice becomes critical now. Record yourself completing role-plays and assess against the criteria: Intelligibility, Fluency, Appropriateness, Resources of Grammar and Expression, and Relationship Building. Consider joining a study group or finding a speaking partner.

Weeks 7-9: Intensive Practice

Complete full mock tests under exam conditions every weekend. Analyse every error meticulously. For Reading and Listening, identify patterns in your mistakes — are you missing specific question types? For Writing, focus on the criteria where you consistently score lowest.

By Week 9, you should be consistently scoring at or above your target grade in at least three sub-tests. The fourth sub-test becomes your primary focus for the remaining weeks.

Weeks 10-12: Refinement and Exam Simulation

Complete at least four full mock exams under strict exam conditions. Fine-tune your exam day strategy: which Reading part do you tackle first? How do you manage the Writing 45-minute limit? What is your Speaking warm-up routine?

Reduce new content learning. Focus on consolidating what you know and building exam confidence. The final week should be lighter — over-studying right before the exam increases anxiety without improving performance.

OET Writing: How to Write a Grade B Referral Letter

The OET Writing sub-test is the section where most candidates struggle to achieve Grade B. It requires you to transform case notes into a coherent, professionally appropriate letter in just 45 minutes. Here's how to master it.

Understanding the Task

You receive case notes (typically from a hospital, clinic, or community setting) and must write a letter to another healthcare professional. Common letter types include referral letters, discharge letters, transfer letters, and letters of advice. The key skill is selective information transfer — you must decide which information to include, which to exclude, and how to organise it.

The Six Assessment Criteria

CriterionWeightWhat Examiners Look For
PurposeHighClear statement of why you're writing, immediately obvious to the reader
ContentHighAll necessary information included; irrelevant information excluded
Conciseness & ClarityMediumInformation presented efficiently without unnecessary words
Genre & StyleMediumAppropriate professional tone, correct register for the recipient
Organisation & LayoutMediumLogical structure with clear paragraphs and appropriate sequencing
LanguageMediumAccurate grammar, spelling, punctuation, and vocabulary

Step-by-Step Writing Process

Step 1: Analyse the Task (5 minutes) — Read the writing task carefully. Who are you writing to? What is your role? What is the purpose? Underline key information in the case notes that relates to your purpose.

Step 2: Select and Organise Content (5 minutes) — Not all case notes belong in your letter. Include information relevant to the recipient's role. Exclude routine details, duplicated information, and anything the recipient already knows. Group selected information into logical paragraphs.

Step 3: Write the Letter (30 minutes) — Open with a clear purpose statement. Use organised paragraphs (typically: reason for referral, relevant history, current status/management, request). Maintain professional tone throughout. Use appropriate medical terminology but explain complex concepts simply.

Step 4: Review and Edit (5 minutes) — Check for grammatical errors, spelling mistakes, and punctuation. Verify that your purpose is clear and all necessary information is included. Ensure the letter makes sense without the case notes.

Common Writing Mistakes: Copying case notes directly without transformation; including irrelevant information; missing the purpose statement; using informal language; poor paragraph organisation; exceeding the word count guideline (180-200 words is typical).

OET Speaking: Master the Role-Play Scenarios

The OET Speaking sub-test lasts approximately 20 minutes and consists of two five-minute role-plays. A trained interlocutor plays the role of a patient, relative, or carer while you play your professional role. Unlike other speaking tests, OET Speaking assesses your ability to communicate effectively in a healthcare consultation.

The Five Assessment Criteria

Intelligibility: Can the patient understand you? This includes pronunciation, stress, rhythm, and accent. You don't need a British accent, but your speech must be clear and easy to follow.

Fluency: Do you speak at an appropriate pace without excessive hesitation? Natural pauses for thought are acceptable; long silences or frequent self-correction are not.

Appropriateness: Is your language suitable for the patient? This includes using lay terms when explaining conditions, showing empathy, and maintaining professional boundaries.

Resources of Grammar and Expression: Can you use a range of grammatical structures accurately? Do you have sufficient vocabulary to express complex ideas?

Relationship Building: Do you establish rapport with the patient? This includes greetings, showing understanding, involving the patient in decisions, and closing the consultation appropriately.

Role-Play Strategy

Preparation Time (3 minutes): Read the role-play card carefully. Identify your role, the setting, the patient's situation, and the tasks you need to accomplish. Plan your opening and think about key phrases you'll need.

During the Role-Play: Begin with a warm greeting and introduction. Use open questions to gather information. Show empathy through phrases like "I understand this must be difficult" or "That sounds challenging." Check understanding regularly. Summarise key points. Close by confirming next steps.

OET Reading: Strategies for Each Part

The OET Reading sub-test consists of three parts, each testing different reading skills. You have 60 minutes total, and successful time management is essential.

Part A: Expeditious Reading (15 minutes, 20 marks)

Part A tests your ability to locate specific information quickly from four short texts. You typically have matching questions, short-answer questions, and sentence completion. The texts might include a policy document, a drug information sheet, a guideline extract, and a hospital protocol.

Strategy: Skim all four texts briefly to understand what each contains. Read the first question, identify keywords, then scan the texts for those keywords. Don't read every word — you're looking for specific information. Work quickly; if you can't find an answer, move on and return later.

Part B: Careful Reading (45 minutes shared with Part C, 6 marks)

Part B presents six short workplace texts (100-150 words each) such as emails, memos, notices, or guidelines. Each text has one multiple-choice question with three options. This part tests your ability to identify main points, gist, and specific details.

Strategy: Read the text fully — it's short enough. Then read the question and all three options carefully. Eliminate obviously wrong options. Choose the option that best matches the text, not the one that seems generally true about healthcare.

Part C: Careful Reading (45 minutes shared with Part B, 16 marks)

Part C consists of two longer texts (700-800 words each) on healthcare topics, with eight multiple-choice questions per text. These texts require deeper comprehension including inference, attitude, and reference.

Strategy: Read the questions first to know what to look for. Read the text paragraph by paragraph, answering questions as you go. For inference questions, look for what the writer suggests rather than states directly. Pay attention to reference words (it, they, this, these) and what they refer to.

OET Listening: Never Miss a Key Detail

The OET Listening sub-test has three parts, all using healthcare-related audio recordings. The total duration is approximately 40 minutes, and each recording plays once only.

Part A: Consultation Extracts (24 marks)

You hear two recorded consultations between a healthcare professional and a patient. You must complete health professional's notes using information from the recording. This part tests your ability to extract specific information.

Strategy: Before the audio plays, read through the notes quickly. Predict what type of information fits each gap (a date? a symptom? a medication?). Listen for keywords and synonyms. Write answers in note form — you don't need full sentences. Check spelling if you have time at the end.

Part B: Short Workplace Extracts (6 marks)

You hear six short recordings from healthcare workplace settings. Each recording has one multiple-choice question with three options. This part tests gist and specific detail.

Strategy: Read the question and options before the audio. Listen for the overall purpose of the recording first, then for specific details that answer the question. Don't choose an option just because you heard the words — make sure it answers the question.

Part C: Presentation Extracts (12 marks)

You hear two longer recordings of healthcare professionals giving presentations or having discussions. Each has six multiple-choice questions. This part tests understanding of main ideas, opinions, and attitudes.

Strategy: Use the preparation time to read all six questions for the recording. Identify the topic and what you need to listen for. During listening, note any opinions or attitudes expressed. Part C questions often ask about why something was mentioned or what the speaker thinks about something.

Common Mistakes That Prevent First-Time OET Success

✓
Not studying the assessment criteria — Many candidates practise without knowing what examiners actually look for. Download and study the official criteria from the OET website before you do any practice.
✓
Insufficient timed practice — The biggest difference between OET practice and the real exam is time pressure. At least 50% of your practice should be timed.
✓
Writing letters without feedback — Writing ten letters without feedback teaches you less than writing three letters with detailed feedback on each. Get expert feedback on every letter you write.
✓
Neglecting weak areas — It's tempting to practise what you're already good at. Focus your energy on your weakest sub-test until it reaches your target level.
✓
Poor vocabulary recording — Keep a vocabulary notebook organised by topic. Review it weekly. Medical collocations (e.g., "administer medication," "monitor closely") are particularly important.
✓
Not practising note-taking for Listening — OET Listening requires simultaneous listening and writing. If you haven't practised this specific skill, you'll struggle in the exam.

Exam Day Strategy: What to Do on the Day

Your preparation culminates in exam day. Here's how to ensure you perform at your best.

The Night Before

Stop studying by 6 PM. Review your vocabulary notebook briefly, then relax. Prepare your documents (ID, confirmation letter) and plan your journey. Go to bed at your normal time — don't try to sleep early, as this can cause restlessness.

Morning of the Exam

Eat a balanced breakfast. Arrive at the test centre 30 minutes early. Bring water and any permitted items. Avoid discussing the exam with other candidates — it often increases anxiety without providing useful information.

During the Exam

For Reading, start with Part A to secure those 20 marks before moving to Parts B and C. For Writing, stick to your planned structure. For Listening, use the preparation time actively — read ahead, don't dwell on answers you missed. For Speaking, treat the interlocutor as a real patient — show genuine warmth and professionalism.

Remember: If you don't know an answer, make an educated guess. There is no negative marking in OET. A guessed answer has a chance of being correct; a blank answer has none.

Frequently Asked Questions About Passing OET

How long does it take to prepare for OET?

Most candidates need 8-12 weeks of dedicated study (15-20 hours per week) to reach Grade B standard. Those with stronger English foundations may need less time; those with weaker English may need more. A diagnostic test in Week 1 helps determine your starting point.

Is OET easier than IELTS?

OET is not necessarily easier, but it is more relevant for healthcare professionals. If you have clinical experience and strong medical vocabulary, you may find OET content more intuitive than IELTS topics. However, the writing and speaking tasks have specific requirements that must be learned.

What score is Grade B in OET?

Grade B corresponds to a score of 350-439 out of 500. Most UK healthcare regulators (NMC, GMC, GPhC) require at least Grade B in all four sub-tests. Some accept Grade C+ in writing when combined with higher scores in other areas.

Can I pass OET without coaching?

Yes, self-study is possible with discipline and the right resources. However, getting feedback on your Writing and Speaking is essential — self-assessment of these skills is notoriously unreliable. Consider at least some sessions with an OET tutor or use AI-powered feedback tools.

How many times can I take OET?

There is no limit to how many times you can take OET. However, each attempt costs approximately £335 (varies by venue). Investing in thorough preparation before your first attempt is far more cost-effective than retaking.

How long are OET results valid?

OET results are valid for two years for most registration purposes. Check with your specific regulatory body, as requirements can vary.

Which OET sub-test is the hardest?

This varies by candidate. Writing is the most commonly failed sub-test, particularly among candidates who haven't practised the specific letter format. Non-native speakers often find Speaking challenging due to the interactional demands. Take a diagnostic test to identify your personal weak areas.
