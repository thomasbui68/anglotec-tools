OSCE Essentials 2026

The Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE) is a practical nursing assessment that all internationally educated nurses must pass to join the Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC) register in the UK. The OSCE tests your ability to apply clinical skills and knowledge in simulated scenarios that reflect real UK nursing practice. It is the final step in the NMC registration process after you have received your eligibility letter and passed your English language requirements.

As of 2026, the NMC OSCE consists of 10 stations. These include APIE stations (Assessment, Planning, Implementation, Evaluation), skill stations, and a professional values and behaviours station. Some stations are linked, meaning they follow a patient scenario across multiple stages of care. The exam structure has been updated from the previous 6-station format to provide a more comprehensive assessment of clinical competence.

The NMC OSCE includes several station types: APIE stations test your ability to assess a patient, plan their care, implement interventions, and evaluate outcomes. Skill stations test specific clinical procedures such as medication administration, wound care, or vital signs measurement. Professional values stations assess communication, safeguarding awareness, and professional conduct. Linked stations connect across the exam, following a patient journey through different stages.

Each OSCE station lasts approximately 12-17 minutes, with some variation depending on the station type. APIE stations typically allow 15-17 minutes each, while standalone skill stations may be 12-15 minutes. Between stations, you have 1-2 minutes to read the scenario brief and prepare. The entire OSCE exam takes approximately 3-4 hours to complete, including briefing time and breaks.

The NMC OSCE uses a pass/fail system with no numerical score provided to candidates. To pass, you must achieve the required standard across all stations. There is no compensation — failing one station means failing the entire OSCE. The exact passing standard is set by the NMC and applied consistently by examiners. You will receive feedback on areas for improvement if you are unsuccessful.

You can take the OSCE up to three times. If you fail on the third attempt, your application for NMC registration will close and you will need to restart the entire registration process, including reapplying and providing updated documents. This three-attempt limit makes thorough preparation essential. Many employers provide structured OSCE preparation programmes to maximise first-time pass rates.

APIE stands for Assessment, Planning, Implementation, and Evaluation. These are the core stages of the nursing process tested in the OSCE. In an Assessment station, you gather patient information through history-taking and physical assessment. In Planning, you develop a care plan based on your assessment findings. In Implementation, you carry out nursing interventions. In Evaluation, you assess whether your care has been effective and modify the plan if needed. APIE stations test both your clinical knowledge and your ability to apply the nursing process systematically.

The OSCE tests a wide range of clinical skills including: vital signs measurement (BP, pulse, temperature, respirations, SpO2), medication administration including dosage calculation, intramuscular and subcutaneous injection technique, wound assessment and dressing, catheterisation care, moving and handling, infection control procedures, basic life support (BLS), peak flow measurement, blood glucose monitoring, and effective handover communication. The specific skills tested vary by exam form.

Effective OSCE preparation includes: studying the NMC OSCE test specifications and blueprints thoroughly; practising clinical skills repeatedly until they become automatic; rehearsing common scenarios with colleagues or through simulation; understanding UK nursing protocols and documentation standards; improving communication skills including handover (ISBAR) structure; practising under timed conditions; attending your employer's OSCE preparation programme; and using Anglotec AI's free OSCE practice scenarios to familiarise yourself with station formats.

Between stations, you have 1-2 minutes to read the station brief outside the examination room. Use this time effectively: read the scenario carefully, identify the patient and situation, note any specific instructions, take a deep breath to compose yourself, and mentally prepare for the station. Do not dwell on the previous station — whether it went well or poorly, focus entirely on the current task. Clear your mind and approach each station fresh.

If you fail the OSCE, you will receive detailed feedback on the areas where you did not meet the required standard. You can book a resit, paying the resit fee of £397. Your employer will typically provide additional preparation support before your resit. You have up to three attempts in total. Many nurses who fail first time pass on their second attempt after targeted preparation addressing their weak areas.

Yes, you need to be familiar with UK nursing protocols and standards, as the OSCE tests your ability to practise safely within the UK healthcare system. Key areas include: National Early Warning Score (NEWS2) for patient assessment, the 6 Cs of nursing, the Nursing and Midwifery Council Code, NHS infection prevention and control policies, the Mental Capacity Act principles, safeguarding procedures, and documentation standards using the SOAP or SOAPIE format. Your employer's preparation programme should cover these.

The professional values station assesses your understanding of nursing ethics, professional conduct, and patient-centred care. You may be asked to discuss how you would handle ethical dilemmas, demonstrate awareness of equality and diversity, show understanding of safeguarding responsibilities, or discuss professional boundaries. This station tests the attitudes and values that underpin safe, effective nursing practice in the UK.

Note-taking policies vary by test centre. In most cases, you can make brief notes during the reading time between stations, but you cannot take notes into the examination room unless specifically permitted for certain station types. Check with your test centre for their specific policies. Memory aids or reference materials are never permitted.

Medication calculation is a common weakness for internationally educated nurses, as calculation methods and units may differ from your home country. Practise regularly using UK-style questions: work with tablets, liquid medicines, and IV calculations. Learn the UK's standard units and rounding conventions. Your employer should provide calculation tests during preparation. Use online resources and Anglotec AI's free calculation practice to build confidence.

Communication is assessed throughout the OSCE, not just in dedicated stations. Examiners evaluate your ability to: introduce yourself professionally, explain procedures to patients clearly, obtain consent, show empathy and compassion, use appropriate verbal and non-verbal communication, hand over effectively using ISBAR, document accurately, and communicate with patients who may have cognitive impairment, language barriers, or emotional distress.

You should wear your work uniform or professional nursing attire as specified by your employer. Ensure your uniform is clean, ironed, and complies with your trust's uniform policy. Wear comfortable, professional shoes suitable for clinical work. Do not wear excessive jewellery, strong perfume, or anything that could compromise infection control. Your appearance should reflect professional nursing standards.

You can typically book your OSCE within 4-6 weeks of receiving your eligibility letter, depending on test centre availability. New test centres in Manchester and Glasgow have increased capacity. Book as soon as you feel prepared — waiting too long can lead to skill deterioration, while rushing unprepared risks failure. Most employers recommend 2-4 weeks of structured preparation before sitting the OSCE.

Linked stations present a continuous patient scenario across multiple stations. For example, you might assess a patient in one station, then plan their care in the next, implement interventions in a third, and evaluate outcomes in a fourth. The patient condition may evolve between stations. Linked stations test your ability to manage patient care holistically over time, as you would in real clinical practice.

OSCE Preparation Strategies

The key to OSCE success is deliberate, structured practice. Do not simply read about stations — practise them physically. Set up mock stations with colleagues, time yourself strictly, and seek feedback on every aspect of your performance. Focus particularly on communication skills, as these are assessed in every station. Learn UK documentation standards and practise writing clear, concise records. Master medication calculations until they are automatic. Most importantly, approach the OSCE as a professional nurse — the exam tests not just skills but the professional values that underpin safe nursing practice.
