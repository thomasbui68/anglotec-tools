The UK offers world-class universities, internationally recognised degrees, shorter course durations (3 years for bachelor’s, 1 year for master’s), diverse culture, and excellent post-study work opportunities. Four UK universities rank in the global top 10.
Requirements vary by course and university. Generally, you need: academic qualifications (equivalent to UK A-Levels or a bachelor’s degree), English language proof (IELTS 6.0-7.0), a personal statement, and references.
Undergraduate fees range from £11,000 to £40,000 per year. Postgraduate fees range from £12,000 to £45,000. Medical and MBA programmes are typically at the higher end.
Yes, popular scholarships include the Chevening Scholarship, Commonwealth Scholarship, Gates Cambridge, university-specific awards, and country-specific funding. Competition is strong, so apply early.
For a Student visa (formerly Tier 4), you generally need IELTS for UKVI with at least 5.5 overall and no band below 5.5 (CEFR B2 level). Your university may require higher scores for admission.
You need a Student visa (formerly Tier 4 General Student visa). You must have a Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies (CAS) from a licensed student sponsor university.
You must show you have enough money to cover tuition fees for one year plus living costs of £1,023 per month (or £1,334 per month if studying in London) for up to 9 months.
Student visa applications typically take 3 weeks from your biometric appointment if applying from outside the UK. Priority and super-priority services are available for faster processing at additional cost.
Yes, on a Student visa you can work up to 20 hours per week during term time and full-time during holidays. You cannot be self-employed or work as a professional sportsperson.
The Graduate Visa (post-study work visa) allows international students who complete a UK degree to stay and work for 2 years (3 years for PhD graduates) without needing employer sponsorship.
Options include: university halls of residence, private student halls, shared houses (HMOs), and private flats. University halls are recommended for first-year students.
University halls cost approximately £100 to £250 per week depending on location and facilities. London and major cities are more expensive. Private shared housing can be more affordable.
UCAS stands for Universities and Colleges Admissions Service. It is the centralised application system for UK undergraduate courses. You can apply to up to five courses through a single UCAS application.
For undergraduate courses via UCAS, the deadline is usually mid-January for equal consideration. Oxford, Cambridge, and Medicine courses have an earlier deadline in mid-October. Postgraduate deadlines vary by university.
You must pay the Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) as part of your visa application. This gives you access to the NHS. Some students also choose private health insurance for additional coverage.
From September 2023, only students on postgraduate research programmes (PhD) can bring dependants. Undergraduate and taught postgraduate students cannot bring family members on a Student visa.
A CAS is a unique reference number issued by your university confirming they have accepted you. You need it to apply for your Student visa. It is valid for six months.
Yes, UK degrees are highly respected and recognised globally. The UK has rigorous quality assurance through the Quality Assurance Agency (QAA), ensuring international recognition of your qualification.
Outside London, students need approximately £900 to £1,200 per month. In London, budget £1,200 to £1,600 per month. This includes rent, food, transport, bills, and social activities.
