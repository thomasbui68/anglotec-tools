IELTS Writing is the section that concerns most test takers, and for good reason. It requires not just English language skills but also the ability to organise ideas, develop arguments, and write under time pressure. These 20 frequently asked questions cover everything from basic test format to advanced scoring criteria, helping you understand exactly what examiners expect and how to achieve your target band score.

IELTS Writing Essentials

Task 1: Minimum 150 words (1/3 of score) | Task 2: Minimum 250 words (2/3 of score) | Total time: 60 minutes | Scored on 4 criteria each worth 25% | Band scores from 0-9 in half-band increments | Computer and paper formats available

The IELTS Writing test consists of two tasks and lasts 60 minutes. Task 1 requires you to write at least 150 words describing visual information (a graph, chart, table, diagram, or process) for Academic IELTS, or writing a letter for General Training IELTS. Task 2 requires you to write at least 250 words responding to a point of view, argument, or problem. Task 2 carries twice the weight of Task 1 in scoring.

IELTS Writing is scored using four criteria, each weighted equally at 25%: Task Response (how well you address the task), Coherence and Cohesion (organisation and flow), Lexical Resource (vocabulary range and accuracy), and Grammatical Range and Accuracy (variety and correctness of grammar). Each criterion is scored on a band scale of 0-9, and the average produces your Writing band score.

You must write at least 150 words for Task 1 and at least 250 words for Task 2. There is no penalty for writing more, but excessively long responses may contain more errors and take time away from checking. If you write significantly fewer words than required, you will be penalised under the Task Response criterion and may receive a reduced band score.

A good target is 160-180 words for Task 1 and 260-280 words for Task 2. This gives you enough space to develop your response fully without excessive length that might introduce errors. Quality matters more than quantity — a well-structured 260-word essay will score higher than a disorganised 350-word essay.

Yes, you can write more than 250 words, and there is no upper word limit. However, writing significantly more does not guarantee a higher score and may increase the risk of errors. Examiners assess quality, not quantity. Focus on developing clear, well-supported arguments rather than simply writing more words.

If you write fewer than 150 words for Task 1 or fewer than 250 words for Task 2, you will be penalised. The penalty is applied under Task Response and can reduce your band score by 0.5 to 1 full band depending on how far below the minimum you are. Always check your word count and ensure you meet the minimum requirement for both tasks.

Handwriting does not directly affect your score as there is no specific criterion for it. However, if the examiner cannot read your writing, they cannot assess your content fairly. Illegible handwriting may lead to lower scores simply because your good ideas and vocabulary cannot be understood. If your handwriting is poor, consider the computer-delivered IELTS test where you type your responses.

For paper-based IELTS, you should write in pencil for both Listening and Reading, but for Writing, you may use either pen or pencil. Check the specific instructions at your test centre, as policies can vary. For computer-delivered IELTS, you type your responses and this question does not apply.

You should spend approximately 20 minutes on Task 1 and 40 minutes on Task 2. Task 2 carries twice the marks of Task 1, so allocating more time to it makes sense. Plan your time carefully: spend a few minutes planning, most of the time writing, and leave a few minutes at the end to check your work for errors.

You can choose which task to do first, though most candidates start with Task 1 since it appears first in the test booklet. Some candidates prefer to start with Task 2 because it is worth more marks and they want to ensure they complete it fully. Either approach is valid — choose the strategy that works best for you based on your practice experience.

Academic IELTS Task 1 may include: line graphs, bar charts, pie charts, tables, diagrams showing a process, maps showing changes over time, or combinations of these. You need to describe the main features, make comparisons where relevant, and summarise the information. No interpretation or opinion is required — just factual description.

General Training Task 1 requires you to write a letter in response to a given situation. The letter may be formal, semi-formal, or informal. Common topics include: requesting information, making a complaint, explaining a situation, inviting someone, thanking someone, or applying for a position. The letter type (formal/informal) is specified in the task instructions.

IELTS Task 2 essay questions typically fall into these categories: Opinion (Agree/Disagree) — do you agree or disagree with a statement? Discussion (Both Views) — discuss both views and give your opinion. Problem and Solution — identify problems and propose solutions. Advantages and Disadvantages — analyse the pros and cons. Double Question — answer two direct questions. Understanding each type helps you structure your response correctly.

Paragraph structure is extremely important. Well-organised paragraphs demonstrate Coherence and Cohesion, which accounts for 25% of your score. For Task 2, a standard structure includes: introduction (2 sentences), body paragraph 1 (main idea + support), body paragraph 2 (main idea + support), and conclusion (1-2 sentences). Each paragraph should contain one main idea developed with explanations and examples.

Idioms should be used sparingly and only when you are confident they are appropriate. While natural, accurate idiom use can demonstrate strong Lexical Resource, inappropriate or incorrect idiom use can harm your score. Academic writing (Task 2) generally requires a formal tone where idioms are less common. If you use idioms, ensure they fit the context and are used correctly.

Yes, spelling matters. Frequent spelling errors will reduce your score under the Lexical Resource and Grammatical Range and Accuracy criteria. British or American spelling is accepted, but you should be consistent throughout your response. If you are unsure of a spelling, use a different word that you know is correct rather than guessing.

Yes, personal examples are acceptable in IELTS Task 2 and can make your arguments more convincing. However, Academic Task 2 typically requires a more formal, objective tone, so balance personal examples with more general evidence where possible. The key is that your examples effectively support your argument, regardless of whether they are personal or general.

Band 6 writers address the task adequately with generally organised responses and reasonably accurate grammar and vocabulary, though with some errors. Band 7 writers present clear, well-developed arguments with good organisation, varied vocabulary, and a mix of complex and simple sentences with occasional errors. Band 8 writers produce fully developed, well-organised responses with sophisticated vocabulary, wide grammatical range, and very few errors. Each band represents a significant step up in precision, range, and sophistication.

Additional IELTS Writing Tips

Beyond the FAQ above, keep these key principles in mind for IELTS Writing success. Always plan your essay before writing — a 3-5 minute plan saves time and produces better-organised responses. Focus on one main idea per paragraph and develop it fully with explanations and examples. Use a variety of sentence structures and vocabulary to demonstrate your language range. Leave 3-5 minutes at the end to check for grammar errors and spelling mistakes. Practise under timed conditions regularly to build speed and confidence.
