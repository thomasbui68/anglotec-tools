Understanding IELTS Writing Task 2

IELTS Writing Task 2 carries two-thirds of your total writing score. You have 40 minutes to write at least 250 words responding to a point of view, argument, or problem. Task 2 is weighted more heavily than Task 1, so performing well here has a significant impact on your overall band score.

Examiners evaluate your essay using four equally weighted criteria: Task Response (does your answer the question fully?), Coherence and Cohesion (is your essay well-organised and connected?), Lexical Resource (is your vocabulary varied and accurate?), and Grammatical Range and Accuracy (do you use a range of structures with few errors?).

Key Facts

  • Time: 40 minutes (recommended)
  • Word count: Minimum 250 words (260-300 is ideal)
  • Score weight: 66% of writing module
  • Question types: 5 distinct essay types

The 5 IELTS Essay Types

Every IELTS Task 2 question falls into one of five categories. Identifying the type correctly is essential because each requires a different structure and approach. Misidentifying the essay type is one of the most common reasons candidates score below Band 6.

1. Opinion Essay (Agree/Disagree)

You are asked whether you agree or disagree with a statement. You must give a clear opinion throughout the essay.

Example: "Some people think that the best way to reduce crime is to give criminals longer prison sentences. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"

2. Discussion Essay (Discuss Both Views)

You must discuss both sides of an issue and then give your own opinion.

Example: "Some people believe that university education should be free for everyone. Others think that students should pay for their own education. Discuss both views and give your opinion."

3. Problem-Solution Essay

You must identify problems and propose solutions. Sometimes the question asks for causes and solutions.

Example: "In many countries, traditional foods are being replaced by international fast food. This is having a negative effect on families and communities. What are the causes and what solutions can you suggest?"

4. Advantage-Disadvantage Essay

You discuss the positive and negative aspects of a trend or development. The question may ask if advantages outweigh disadvantages.

Example: "In the future, robots may do most of our work. Do the advantages of this development outweigh the disadvantages?"

5. Direct Question Essay (Two-Part)

The question asks two or more direct questions that you must answer.

Example: "As countries develop, more people buy and own cars. Why do you think this is happening? What problems does this cause for society?"

Structures for Each Essay Type

Using the correct essay structure is critical for Coherence and Cohesion. Here are proven structures for each essay type:

Opinion Essay Structure

Introduction: Paraphrase topic + state your opinion clearly

Body 1: Main reason for your opinion + explanation + example

Body 2: Second reason + explanation + example

Conclusion: Restate opinion + summarise main points

Discussion Essay Structure

Introduction: Paraphrase both views + thesis statement

Body 1: First view + supporting arguments

Body 2: Second view + supporting arguments

Body 3 (optional): Your opinion with reasons

Conclusion: Balanced summary + your opinion

Problem-Solution Essay Structure

Introduction: Paraphrase the problem

Body 1: Problem 1 + explanation + extent

Body 2: Problem 2 + explanation

Body 3: Solution(s) with supporting details

Conclusion: Summarise problems + reiterate solutions

Advantage-Disadvantage Essay Structure

Introduction: Paraphrase topic + outline that both sides will be discussed

Body 1: Advantage(s) + examples

Body 2: Disadvantage(s) + examples

Conclusion: State which side is stronger (if asked) + summary

High-Scoring Vocabulary

A Band 7+ essay uses less common lexical items with awareness of style and collocation. Avoid overused words like "good," "bad," "many," and "some."

Giving Opinions

I firmly believe that... / It seems evident that... / From my perspective... / I am convinced that... / There is no doubt that...

Adding Points

Furthermore... / Moreover... / In addition... / What is more... / Another key point is that...

Contrasting

Nevertheless... / Conversely... / On the contrary... / Despite the fact that... / While it is true that...

Giving Examples

For instance... / A clear example of this is... / To illustrate... / This can be seen when...

Concluding

In conclusion... / To sum up... / Taking all points into consideration... / Ultimately...

Cause & Effect

As a result... / Consequently... / This leads to... / The primary cause is... / This gives rise to...

Grammar for Band 7+

Band 7+ grammar requires frequent error-free sentences with a mix of simple and complex structures. Master these patterns:

1. Conditional Sentences (Type 2 & 3)

"If governments invested more in public transport, traffic congestion would decrease significantly." (Type 2 - hypothetical present)

"If stricter laws had been introduced earlier, the environmental damage would have been reduced." (Type 3 - hypothetical past)

2. Relative Clauses

"Online education, which has grown rapidly since 2020, offers flexibility for students who live in remote areas."

3. Passive Voice

"It is widely believed that technology has transformed the workplace. Stricter regulations should be introduced to protect workers' rights."

4. Nominalisation

"The implementation of new policies led to a significant improvement in air quality." (instead of "When they implemented new policies, air quality improved significantly.")

5. Cleft Sentences for Emphasis

"It is education, not punishment, that offers the most effective solution to reducing youth crime."

Brainstorming & Planning

Spending 5 minutes planning will improve your score significantly. A well-planned essay is more coherent and covers all parts of the task.

The 4-Step Planning Method

  1. 1. Analyse the question (1 min): Identify the essay type, topic, and what you must do.
  2. 2. Brainstorm ideas (2 min): Write down 4-6 ideas related to the topic. Select the 2 strongest.
  3. 3. Plan your structure (1 min): Decide how many body paragraphs and what each will cover.
  4. 4. Note vocabulary (1 min): Write 3-4 topic-specific words or phrases to use.

Writing the Introduction

Your introduction should be 40-60 words and contain two elements: (1) a paraphrase of the question, and (2) a thesis or outline statement.

Formula: General statement + Specific focus

"It is true that advances in technology have transformed the way people communicate. While there are some drawbacks to this trend, I believe that the benefits outweigh the disadvantages."

Avoid memorised phrases like "In this essay I will discuss..." or "This is a controversial topic..." Examiners penalise memorised language.

Writing Body Paragraphs

Each body paragraph should follow the PEEL structure: Point, Explanation, Example, Link.

Point: Topic sentence stating your main idea

Explanation: 2-3 sentences developing the idea

Example: A specific, relevant example (real or hypothetical)

Link: Connect back to the question or forward to the next paragraph

A strong body paragraph is 80-100 words. Use discourse markers to connect ideas but avoid overusing "firstly, secondly, finally" at Band 7+.

Writing the Conclusion

Your conclusion should be 30-50 words. Never introduce new ideas here. Restate your position and summarise your main points using different vocabulary from the introduction.

Golden Rules for Conclusions

  • - No new arguments or examples
  • - No memorised phrases ("In a nutshell," "After weighing both sides")
  • - Use synonyms to avoid repetition
  • - For opinion essays, state your position clearly

Band 7+ Sample Essays

Opinion Essay Sample

Band 8.0 | Topic: Technology & Employment

Question: Some people believe that technology has made manual labour unnecessary and this is a positive development. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

It is argued that technological advancements have rendered physical work obsolete and that this trend is beneficial. I strongly disagree with this viewpoint, as the elimination of manual labour leads to significant unemployment and threatens the preservation of traditional skills.

The primary consequence of replacing human workers with machines is widespread job loss. In manufacturing sectors across the developed world, automated assembly lines have displaced millions of factory workers who previously relied on these positions to support their families. While companies may benefit from increased efficiency and lower costs, the social impact of mass unemployment cannot be overlooked. Communities that were once thriving industrial centres have experienced economic decline and rising poverty as a direct result of automation.

Furthermore, the decline of manual labour threatens the survival of valuable traditional craftsmanship. Skills such as carpentry, tailoring, and pottery-making, which have been passed down through generations, are rapidly disappearing as younger people turn away from these trades. This represents not only an economic loss but also a cultural one, as these crafts form an integral part of human heritage. Handmade goods, for instance, often possess a quality and uniqueness that mass-produced items cannot replicate, and their disappearance diminishes the richness of society.

In conclusion, although technology undoubtedly brings certain efficiencies, the replacement of manual labour is not a positive development. The resulting unemployment and erosion of traditional skills far outweigh any economic benefits, and governments should therefore implement policies to protect both workers and craftspeople.

Discussion Essay Sample

Band 7.5 | Topic: Space Exploration

Question: Some people think that space exploration is a waste of resources. Others believe it is essential for humanity's future. Discuss both views and give your opinion.

The question of whether investment in space exploration represents a worthwhile use of public funds has generated considerable debate. While some argue that these resources would be better directed toward pressing issues on Earth, others maintain that exploring space is crucial for the long-term survival of humanity. This essay will examine both perspectives.

Those who criticise space funding point to the numerous problems that remain unsolved on our own planet. Billions of people still lack access to clean drinking water, adequate healthcare, and basic education. Critics argue that the billions of dollars spent on missions to Mars or the Moon could be redirected to alleviate poverty, combat climate change, or fund medical research. From this perspective, space exploration appears to be an expensive luxury that offers little immediate benefit to ordinary citizens.

On the other hand, proponents of space exploration highlight its potential to secure humanity's future. Earth has finite resources, and population growth continues to place unsustainable pressure on the environment. Space research has already yielded practical benefits, including satellite technology for weather forecasting and global communications. Moreover, the search for habitable planets or accessible resources in space may one day prove essential if Earth becomes uninhabitable. The technological innovations driven by space programmes also create economic opportunities and inspire younger generations to pursue scientific careers.

In my view, while the arguments for addressing terrestrial problems are compelling, completely abandoning space exploration would be short-sighted. A balanced approach that maintains space funding while increasing investment in earthly challenges represents the most sensible strategy.

Problem-Solution Essay Sample

Band 7.5 | Topic: Obesity

Question: In many countries, obesity rates among children have risen dramatically. What are the causes of this trend, and what measures can be taken to address it?

Childhood obesity has become a pressing public health concern in numerous nations worldwide. This essay will explore the primary causes of this alarming trend and propose effective measures to combat it.

One of the main factors contributing to rising obesity rates among children is the widespread availability and aggressive marketing of unhealthy foods. Fast food outlets, sugary snacks, and carbonated drinks are not only affordable and convenient but also heavily promoted through advertising campaigns targeting young people. Additionally, changes in family structure and lifestyle mean that many parents lack the time to prepare nutritious meals, resorting instead to processed alternatives. Another significant cause is the sedentary nature of modern childhood. With the proliferation of smartphones, video games, and streaming services, many children spend hours engaged in screen-based activities rather than participating in physical exercise.

To address this issue, a multi-faceted approach is required. Governments should implement stricter regulations on the advertising of junk food to children and consider taxing sugary products to discourage consumption. Schools also have a vital role to play by ensuring that meals provided on campus meet nutritional standards and by incorporating daily physical activity into the curriculum. Parents, too, must take responsibility by modelling healthy eating habits and limiting their children's recreational screen time.

In conclusion, childhood obesity stems primarily from dietary changes and reduced physical activity. Through coordinated action by governments, educational institutions, and families, this trend can be reversed and the health of future generations safeguarded.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many words should I write for Task 2?

You must write at least 250 words. There is no penalty for writing more, but 260-300 words is the sweet spot. Longer essays increase the risk of errors and repetition.

Can I use personal examples?

Yes, personal examples are acceptable and can make your essay more authentic. However, ensure they are relevant and support your argument. Academic or general examples work equally well.

Should I memorise model essays?

No. Examiners are trained to detect memorised language and will penalise it. Instead, study model essays to understand structure and vocabulary, then practise writing your own responses on various topics.

Can I write a balanced opinion for agree/disagree questions?

It is possible but difficult. If you choose a balanced approach, you must still have a clear position and devote more space to one side. A strong, clear opinion is easier to argue and score higher.

Is it okay to use idioms?

Use idioms sparingly and only if you are certain of their meaning and context. Academic essays should be formal. Informal idioms ("a piece of cake," "cost an arm and a leg") are inappropriate.

How can I improve my coherence and cohesion score?

Use a range of linking words appropriately. Ensure each paragraph has a clear topic sentence. Use referencing words ("this," "these," "it," "they") to connect ideas. Avoid overusing mechanical linking ("firstly, secondly, thirdly") at higher bands.
