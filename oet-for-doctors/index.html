What is OET for Doctors?

The Occupational English Test (OET) is an international English language test specifically designed for healthcare professionals. The OET Medicine version is tailored for doctors seeking to register and practice in English-speaking healthcare environments, particularly the UK's National Health Service (NHS).

Why Doctors Choose OET Over IELTS

OET uses real healthcare scenarios that doctors encounter in their daily practice. Unlike IELTS, which tests general and academic English, OET assesses your ability to communicate effectively in medical contexts.

  • Medical vocabulary you already know and use
  • Healthcare-specific scenarios (patient consultations, referrals)
  • Relevant to your daily work as a doctor
  • Boosts confidence for workplace communication
  • Recognized by GMC, NMC, and all UK healthcare regulators
  • Also accepted in Australia, New Zealand, Ireland, Singapore, UAE, and more
  • Many doctors find it more practical and relevant than IELTS

OET vs IELTS for GMC Registration

The GMC accepts both OET and IELTS for English language evidence. OET requires Grade B (350+) in all four sub-tests, while IELTS requires 7.5 overall with minimum 7.0 in all bands (note: this is higher than the IELTS 7.0 required for nurses). Many doctors find OET more achievable because it uses medical terminology and clinical scenarios they're already familiar with, rather than testing general academic English.

OET Test Format for Doctors (Medicine)

The OET Medicine test consists of four sub-tests, each assessing a different language skill in a healthcare context. The test takes approximately 3 hours to complete.

Listening (Approx. 45 minutes)

Three parts with a total of 42 questions. You hear healthcare consultations and workplace conversations.

  • Part A: Two patient consultations (24 questions) — note-taking exercise
  • Part B: Six short workplace extracts (6 questions) — multiple choice
  • Part C: Two longer presentations or interviews (12 questions) — multiple choice
  • Topics: Medical histories, examination discussions, treatment explanations
  • Accents: Australian, British, American — mixed throughout
  • You hear each recording twice in Part A, once in Parts B and C

Reading (60 minutes)

Three parts with a total of 42 questions. Tests your ability to understand and analyze medical texts.

  • Part A: Four short medical texts (20 questions) — rapid reading, matching, short answer
  • Part B: Six short workplace texts (6 questions) — multiple choice
  • Part C: Two longer medical articles (16 questions) — multiple choice
  • Texts include: Medical journal articles, hospital policies, clinical guidelines
  • Part A is the most time-pressured — practice speed reading

Writing (45 minutes)

One task: Write a professional healthcare letter based on case notes provided.

  • Task: Write a referral, discharge, or transfer letter
  • Word count: 180-200 words
  • Case notes provided: Patient history, examination findings, test results
  • Audience: Another healthcare professional (GP, specialist, colleague)
  • Letter types: Referral, discharge, transfer, letter to patient
  • Assessed on: Purpose, content, conciseness and clarity, genre and style, organization and layout, language

Speaking (Approx. 20 minutes)

Two role-play scenarios with a trained interlocutor. Each role-play lasts about 5 minutes.

  • Two role-plays: Different medical scenarios in each
  • Preparation time: 3 minutes to read the scenario card before each role-play
  • Your role: You play the doctor; the interlocutor plays the patient/relative
  • Scenarios may include: Breaking bad news, explaining treatment, taking history, counseling
  • Assessed on: Relationship-building, understanding and incorporating the patient's perspective, providing information, gathering information
  • Assessed on linguistic criteria: Intelligibility, fluency, appropriateness of language, resources of grammar and expression

GMC Score Combinations Accepted

The GMC allows score combining from two OET sittings taken within 6 months, provided: all four sub-tests were taken in both sittings, you achieve at least Grade C+ (300+) in every sub-test across both sittings, and you achieve Grade B (350+) in all four sub-tests when combining the best scores from both attempts. This means if you get B in Listening, Reading, Writing but C+ in Speaking on your first attempt, and then get B in Speaking on your second attempt (with at least C+ in the other three), the GMC will accept the combined results.

OET Preparation Strategy for Doctors

A structured preparation plan is essential for achieving Grade B in all four OET sub-tests. Here's a recommended approach.

1

Assess Your Level

Take a diagnostic practice test to identify your weak areas. Focus preparation time where you need most improvement.

2

Build Medical Vocabulary

Study common medical conditions, treatments, and terminology in English. Focus on terms used in patient communication.

3

Practice Each Sub-test

Use official OET practice materials. Spend extra time on your weakest section. Practice under timed conditions.

4

Master Writing Letters

Practice all letter types (referral, discharge, transfer). Learn to select relevant information from case notes. Keep to 180-200 words.

5

Role-play Speaking

Practice with colleagues or a tutor. Record yourself and review. Focus on empathy, clarity, and appropriate tone.

6

Full Mock Tests

Complete full timed mock tests in exam conditions. Review all errors systematically. Build exam stamina and time management.

Listening Preparation

  • Listen to BBC Health podcasts and medical documentaries
  • Practice note-taking while listening — don't try to write everything
  • Use abbreviations and symbols you're comfortable with
  • Focus on key information: symptoms, diagnosis, treatment plan
  • Get used to different English accents (British, Australian, American)
  • Practice with sample recordings from the OET website
  • Learn to predict what information is coming next

Reading Preparation

  • Read medical journals and clinical guidelines regularly
  • Practice speed reading for Part A — you have limited time
  • Learn to scan for specific information quickly
  • Build your medical vocabulary systematically
  • Practice with authentic medical texts
  • Work on understanding implied meaning (not just literal)
  • Time yourself strictly during practice

Writing Preparation

  • Practice ALL letter types: referral, discharge, transfer
  • Learn the standard format for each letter type
  • Practice selecting only relevant information from case notes
  • Keep your writing concise — 180-200 words
  • Use appropriate medical terminology and abbreviations
  • Study sample letters with high scores on OET website
  • Get feedback on your writing from an experienced tutor
  • Practice transforming informal case notes into formal letter language

Speaking Preparation

  • Practice role-plays with colleagues or in study groups
  • Record yourself and review for clarity, pace, and tone
  • Use the 3-minute preparation time effectively — plan your approach
  • Show empathy and patient-centered communication
  • Use open-ended questions to gather information
  • Check patient understanding throughout the consultation
  • Don't use complex medical jargon with patients
  • Practice explaining conditions in simple, clear English
  • Be prepared for emotional scenarios — angry relatives, distressed patients

OET Registration, Fees & Test Dates

Practical information about booking and taking your OET Medicine test.

Registration & Fees

  • Booking: Online at oet.com — create an account and select Medicine
  • Test fee: Approximately AUD 587 (varies by venue, roughly £320-£380)
  • Payment: Credit/debit card online
  • ID required: Valid passport (must match registration details exactly)
  • Booking window: Tests available throughout the year
  • Booking deadline: Usually 2-3 weeks before test date
  • Rescheduling: Possible up to the booking deadline for a fee

Test Format Options

  • OET on Paper: Traditional paper-based test at a test venue
  • OET on Computer: Take Reading and Listening on computer at a venue
  • OET@Home: Take all sub-tests from home (online proctored) — check availability in your country
  • Speaking is always done via video call with a live interlocutor, regardless of format
  • Results released approximately 12 business days after the test
  • Digital certificate available through your OET account
  • Results valid for 2 years

Frequently Asked Questions

What OET score do doctors need for GMC registration?

Doctors need Grade B (score of 350 or higher) in all four OET sub-tests — Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking. The GMC also accepts combined scores from two sittings within 6 months, provided all sub-tests are at least Grade C+ (300+) and the combined results show Grade B in all four areas.

Is OET easier than IELTS for doctors?

Many doctors find OET easier because it uses medical terminology and clinical scenarios they're already familiar with from their daily practice. IELTS tests general academic English which may include topics far removed from medicine. However, OET requires specific skills (like writing referral letters) that need targeted practice. The best choice depends on your individual strengths.

How long is OET valid for GMC registration?

OET results are valid for 2 years from the test date. Your OET certificate must be valid when you apply for GMC registration. If your results expire before completing the registration process, you will need to retake the test.

Can I combine OET scores from two different test dates?

Yes, the GMC allows OET score combining from two sittings within a 6-month period. Conditions: you must take all four sub-tests in both sittings, achieve at least Grade C+ (300+) in every sub-test in both sittings, and the combined best scores must achieve at least Grade B (350+) in all four sub-tests.

How much does OET for doctors cost?

The OET test fee is approximately AUD 587 (around £320-£380 or $400 USD), though prices vary slightly by test venue. Additional costs may include preparation materials, courses, and travel to the test centre. The OET@Home option has the same fee but saves travel costs.

How long does it take to prepare for OET?

Most doctors need 4-8 weeks of dedicated preparation, studying 1-2 hours daily. Doctors with strong English skills may need only 2-3 weeks of familiarization with the test format. Those needing significant improvement in writing or speaking may need 10-12 weeks. The writing sub-test typically requires the most preparation time.

Can I take OET Medicine if I'm not currently practicing?

Yes, you can take OET Medicine regardless of your current employment status. You don't need to be actively practicing to register for or take the test. However, clinical knowledge helps in understanding the scenarios, so recent graduates or those who have taken a break may need extra preparation time.
