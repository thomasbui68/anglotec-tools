Understanding the NHS CV

An NHS CV is different from a corporate CV. NHS recruiters and hiring managers look for specific information presented in a particular way. Understanding these expectations is the first step to getting shortlisted.

The NHS uses the Agenda for Change framework for most roles, and the Person Specification document accompanying every job advert defines the essential and desirable criteria. Your CV must demonstrate that you meet these criteria — ideally with specific evidence for each point.

NHS CVs are typically 2 pages maximum for Band 5-6 roles, and 2-3 pages for Band 7+ or medical roles. Quality matters more than quantity — every sentence should earn its place.

Key Principle: The NHS CV is evidence-based. Every claim you make should be supported by a specific example, achievement, or qualification. "I am a good communicator" is weak. "I delivered handover to multidisciplinary teams of 15+ staff daily using SBAR" is strong.

The 8 Essential Sections of an NHS CV

Your NHS CV should include these sections in this order:

Section 1: Personal Details

Keep this simple and professional. Include:

  • Full name: As it appears on your professional registration
  • Address: Town/city and postcode (full address optional)
  • Phone number: Mobile, ensure voicemail is professional
  • Email address: Professional format (firstname.lastname@email.com)
  • Professional registration number: NMC PIN or GMC number (if applicable)
  • Right to work: Brief note if relevant ("Eligible to work in the UK")
Do NOT Include: Date of birth, age, marital status, nationality (unless relevant), photograph, or religion. These are protected characteristics and including them can lead to unconscious bias or raise GDPR concerns.

Section 2: Professional Profile

This is your 3-4 line elevator pitch. It should summarise your experience, specialism, key strengths, and what you're looking for. Tailor it to every application.

Example Professional Profiles

Band 5 Staff Nurse (Medical): "A compassionate and dedicated Registered Nurse with 3 years' experience in acute medical settings. Proven ability to manage a diverse caseload, prioritise complex care needs, and work effectively within multidisciplinary teams. Committed to evidence-based practice and delivering high-quality patient-centred care. Seeking a Band 5 position on an acute medical ward to further develop clinical skills."

Band 6 Charge Nurse (Elderly Care): "An experienced Registered Nurse with 6 years in elderly care and 2 years in a Band 6 leadership role. Skilled in managing clinical teams, driving quality improvement initiatives, and implementing falls prevention strategies. Strong mentor with experience supporting NQNs through preceptorship. Seeking a Band 6 Charge Nurse role to lead a dedicated team in delivering exceptional dementia care."

Profile Formula: [Role] with [X years] experience in [specialism]. Proven [2-3 key strengths]. [Evidence of impact/achievement]. Seeking [target role] to [career goal].

Section 4: Employment History

This is the most important section. List roles in reverse chronological order (most recent first). For each role, include:

Structure for Each Role

Job title | Employer | Ward/Department | Dates (MM/YYYY)

Then 4-6 bullet points covering:

  • Core responsibilities: Caseload size, shift patterns, types of patients
  • Clinical skills: Specific procedures, assessments, competencies
  • Teamwork: MDT working, delegation, supervision
  • Quality/Education: Audits, teaching, QI projects
  • Achievements: Quantifiable results where possible

Example Employment Entry

Band 5 Staff Nurse | Manchester Royal Infirmary | Acute Medical Ward | 09/2022 – Present
  • Manage a caseload of 6-8 acute medical patients per shift, prioritising care using NEWS2 and early warning scores
  • Administer medications to 20+ patients per shift, including IV therapy, maintaining 100% accuracy in documentation
  • Supervise and delegate to 2-3 HCAs per shift, ensuring safe staffing ratios and task allocation
  • Participate in weekly MDT meetings, presenting updates on 8-10 patients and contributing to discharge planning
  • Completed a quality improvement project reducing medication administration errors by 15% over 6 months
  • Mentored 2 student nurses and 1 NQN through their preceptorship, both achieving successful outcomes
Achievement-Focused Language: Use action verbs and quantify where possible. "Reduced" not "helped reduce." "Managed 8 patients" not "responsible for patients." "Trained 5 staff" not "involved in training."

Section 5: Education & Qualifications

List your qualifications in reverse chronological order. Include:

  • Degree/Diploma name
  • Institution
  • Year of completion
  • Grade/Classification (if favourable — e.g., First Class, Distinction)

Example

BSc (Hons) Nursing (Adult) | University of Manchester | 2019 | 2:1

A-Levels: Biology (B), Chemistry (C), Psychology (B) | Manchester College | 2016

Section 6: Professional Registration

This is a critical section for healthcare roles. Include:

  • NMC/GMC PIN number (or equivalent for your profession)
  • Registration date
  • Revalidation date (to show you're current)
  • Any additional registrations (e.g., HCPC, GPhC)
  • Indemnity/insurance arrangements

Example: Registered Nurse (Adult) — NMC PIN: 12A3456B | Registration: March 2019 | Revalidation: Due March 2026

Section 7-8: Additional Training & Information

Additional Training

List relevant courses and study days with dates:

  • ALS/ILS Certification (2024)
  • Mental Health First Aid (2023)
  • Wound Care Management Study Day (2023)
  • Venepuncture and Cannulation (2022)
  • Leadership for Band 6 Preparation (2024)

Additional Information

Include only if relevant:

  • Languages: Include proficiency level (fluent, conversational, basic)
  • Voluntary work: Especially if healthcare-related
  • Interests: Brief, to show you're well-rounded (optional)
References: "References available on request" is sufficient. Do not include referee names and contact details on your CV — provide these only when requested. This protects your referees' data and ensures you can notify them in advance.

Expert NHS CV Writing Tips

Tailor Every Application

Never send a generic CV. Read the person specification and match your CV to the essential and desirable criteria. If the job asks for "experience in wound care," ensure your CV mentions wound care. Use similar language to the job description — many NHS trusts use automated screening software that looks for keywords.

Use the STAR Method for Key Achievements

For your most important achievements, use the STAR structure (Situation, Task, Action, Result). This provides evidence and context that makes your CV stand out.

Quantify Everything

Numbers make your CV credible. "Managed 8 patients" is stronger than "managed patients." "Reduced errors by 15%" is stronger than "reduced errors." "Mentored 4 students" is stronger than "mentored students."

Keep It Current

Update your CV after every significant training, project, or role change. Don't wait until you need to apply for a job — you'll forget important details.

Use Professional Formatting

  • Font: Calibri, Arial, or Garamond in 10-11pt
  • Margins: 2cm minimum
  • No photographs, graphics, or coloured backgrounds
  • Clear section headings (bold, slightly larger)
  • Consistent bullet points throughout
  • Save as PDF unless the application specifies otherwise

✓

Frequently Asked Questions About NHS CVs

How long should an NHS CV be?

For Band 5-6 nursing roles, 2 pages is the maximum and the expectation. For Band 7+ or medical roles, 2-3 pages may be acceptable if the content is relevant. Recruiters spend an average of 30 seconds on initial CV screening — make every line count.

Should I include a cover letter with my NHS CV?

Yes, always include a cover letter unless the application specifically states not to. A good cover letter should be one page, addressed to the hiring manager, and explain why you're applying, how you meet the essential criteria, and what you can bring to the role.

How do I explain gaps in my employment?

Be honest and brief. "Career break for family responsibilities" or "Travel and personal development" is sufficient. If you undertook any relevant activity during the gap (volunteering, study, caring), mention it.

Should newly qualified nurses include all placements?

List all placements but group minor ones together: "Student Nurse Placements — Medical Ward, Surgical Ward, Community, Mental Health (Various NHS Trusts, 2019-2021)." Expand on the most relevant 2-3 placements with bullet points.

Do I need different CVs for different NHS trusts?

Yes, tailor your CV for each application. Different roles have different person specifications. The time spent tailoring is far less than the time wasted on rejected applications.

How often should I update my NHS CV?

Update your CV every 3-6 months, or immediately after any significant change: new role, new qualification, completed project, or new skill. Regular updates ensure you don't forget important details.
