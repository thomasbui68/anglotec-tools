Understanding IELTS Writing Band Scores

The IELTS Writing test consists of two tasks and is assessed across four criteria, each scored from Band 1 to Band 9:

  • Task Response (Task 2) / Task Achievement (Task 1): How well you address all parts of the question
  • Coherence and Cohesion: How logically organised your writing is and how well ideas connect
  • Lexical Resource: Your vocabulary range, accuracy, and appropriateness
  • Grammatical Range and Accuracy: The variety and accuracy of your sentence structures

Your overall Writing band is the average of these four criteria. To achieve Band 7, you generally need at least Band 7 in Task Response and no criterion below Band 6.5. Understanding exactly what examiners look for at each band is the first step to improvement.

IELTS Task 2 Band 7: Step-by-Step Approach

Task 2 carries twice the weight of Task 1 in your overall Writing score. A structured approach ensures you meet all Band 7 requirements systematically.

Step 1: Analyse the Question (2 minutes)

Identify the topic, task type (opinion, discussion, problem-solution, advantage-disadvantage), and scope. Underline key instruction words: "discuss both views," "to what extent do you agree," "what are the causes and solutions." Misunderstanding the task type is the most common reason candidates score below Band 6.

Step 2: Plan Your Response (5 minutes)

Brainstorm 2-3 main ideas. Decide your position (for opinion essays). Plan your paragraph structure: Introduction (paraphrase + position/thesis), Body Paragraph 1 (main idea + explanation + example), Body Paragraph 2 (main idea + explanation + example), Conclusion (summarise + final thought). Never skip planning. Five minutes of planning saves ten minutes of rewriting.

Step 3: Write the Introduction (5 minutes)

Paraphrase the question in 1-2 sentences. State your position clearly (for opinion essays) or outline what the essay will discuss. A strong introduction sets the examiner's expectations and demonstrates your ability to rephrase effectively.

Step 4: Develop Body Paragraphs (20 minutes)

Each body paragraph should follow the PEEL structure: Point (topic sentence), Explanation (develop the idea), Example (specific illustration), Link (connect to question or next paragraph). Include specific examples — general statements without support will not reach Band 7.

Step 5: Write the Conclusion (3 minutes)

Summarise your main points. Restate your position clearly. Add a final thought, recommendation, or prediction. Never introduce new ideas in the conclusion. Keep it concise: 2-3 sentences maximum.

Step 6: Review (5 minutes)

Check for grammatical errors, spelling mistakes, and unclear sentences. Ensure word count is at least 250 words. Verify that you answered all parts of the question. This final check can lift your score by 0.5 bands.

Instead, governments should implement stricter safety regulations and mandatory protective equipment to minimise risk while preserving individual freedom of choice."

BAND 5

BAND 7

Topic: Causes of obesity and solutions

"The rising prevalence of obesity can be attributed primarily to two interconnected factors: the widespread availability and aggressive marketing of processed, calorie-dense foods, coupled with increasingly sedentary lifestyles driven by desk-based employment and technological convenience. To address this, governments should impose levies on sugary products while simultaneously investing in public recreational facilities to encourage physical activity."

BAND 5

Topic: Technology and education

"Technology is very important in schools now. Students use computers and tablets to learn. This is good because they can find information easily. But some teachers don't like it because students play games instead of studying."

"The integration of digital technology into educational settings has fundamentally transformed modern pedagogy, offering students unprecedented access to information and interactive learning tools. 10 Common Mistakes Keeping You at Band 5

Mistake 1: Not Answering the Question Fully

Many candidates write a generic essay without addressing the specific task requirements. A discussion essay that does not discuss both views equally cannot score above Band 6 for Task Response.

Fix: Underline every instruction in the question. Create a checklist: "Have I discussed both views?" "Have I given my opinion?" "Have I suggested solutions?" Address every component explicitly.

Mistake 2: Memorised Introductions and Conclusions

Examiners can spot memorised phrases instantly. "In this essay I will discuss both views and give my opinion" is overused and adds no value.

Fix: Paraphrase the question naturally. Vary your introductions: start with a general statement, a rhetorical question, or a factual observation related to the topic.

Mistake 3: Using Only Simple Sentences

Band 5 writing consists mainly of short, simple sentences: "Technology is important. Students use computers. This helps them learn." This demonstrates limited grammatical range.

Fix: Combine ideas using subordinate clauses, relative clauses, and participial phrases. "Technology, which has become integral to education, enables students to access vast resources."

Mistake 4: Repetition of Words and Ideas

Repeating the same vocabulary or restating the same idea in different words wastes words and demonstrates limited lexical resource.

Fix: Use synonyms and pronouns to avoid repetition. Plan your essay to ensure each sentence adds new information or develops an existing point further.

Mistake 5: No Clear Position

In opinion essays, sitting on the fence or being unclear about your position damages your Task Response score. Examiners need to know where you stand.

Fix: State your position clearly in the introduction and reiterate it in the conclusion. Even in discussion essays, a brief indication of your view shows critical engagement.

Mistake 6: Overusing Linking Words

Some candidates use "furthermore," "moreover," and "in addition" in every paragraph, often incorrectly. This creates a mechanical, unnatural feel.

Fix: Use a variety of cohesive devices: referencing ("this approach," "such measures"), conjunctions, and lexical cohesion (repeating key terms with collocations). Use linking words only when they genuinely add meaning.

Mistake 7: Weak or Missing Examples

Band 7 essays support main ideas with specific, relevant examples. Vague statements like "for example, in many countries" do not demonstrate depth of development.

Fix: Include concrete examples: "In Finland, for instance, schools have eliminated homework entirely, resulting in consistently high rankings in international education assessments." Specificity strengthens your argument.

Mistake 8: Spelling and Punctuation Errors

Consistent spelling mistakes and incorrect punctuation (especially comma splices and missing apostrophes) reduce your score for both Lexical Resource and Grammar.

Fix: Proofread systematically in your final 5 minutes. Check commonly misspelled words: government, environment, benefit, necessary, accommodation, occurrence, privilege, separate, definitely.

Mistake 9: Informal Language

Using contractions ("don't," "can't"), slang ("kids," "a lot of"), or overly casual expressions is inappropriate for academic writing.

Fix: Write formally: use "children" instead of "kids," "do not" instead of "don't," "many" or "numerous" instead of "lots of," and "significant" instead of "big."

Mistake 10: Insufficient Word Count

Writing under 250 words for Task 2 or under 150 words for Task 1 results in an automatic penalty. Many Band 5 candidates run out of ideas.

Fix: Always plan your essay to ensure sufficient content. Aim for 260-280 words for Task 2. If you struggle to reach the count, add more explanation or a more detailed example to each body paragraph.

Essential Band 7 Sentence Structures

Grammatical range is 25% of your score. Master these structures to demonstrate sophisticated control of English grammar.

1. Subordinate Clauses (Complex Sentences)

Although many people argue that technology isolates individuals, I believe it can actually strengthen social connections when used purposefully.

Use: although, while, whereas, because, since, as, if, unless, even though

2. Relative Clauses

Students who participate in extracurricular activities often demonstrate superior time management skills compared to those who focus solely on academics.

Use: who, which, that, whose, where, when, whom — both defining and non-defining

3. Passive Voice

It is widely believed that environmental regulations should be strengthened to protect endangered ecosystems.

Use for: general opinions, processes, formal statements. Not for every sentence, but 2-3 times per essay.

4. Cleft Sentences

It is not the quantity of education that matters, but the quality of instruction and student engagement.

Use: It is/was... that/who... — adds emphasis and demonstrates sophisticated grammar

5. Participial Phrases

Having considered both perspectives, I am convinced that the benefits of international travel far outweigh the drawbacks.

Use: Having..., Being..., Considering..., Resulting from... — excellent for introductions and conclusions

6. Conditionals (All Types)

If governments were to invest more heavily in public transport, traffic congestion in urban centres would decrease significantly. (Type 2)

Had the authorities acted sooner, the crisis could have been averted. (Inverted Type 3)

Use a mix: Type 1 for likely situations, Type 2 for hypothetical, Type 3 for past counterfactuals

7. Nominalisation

The rapid expansion of cities has led to increased pollution and a decline in quality of life for residents. (instead of "Cities have expanded rapidly, which has led to...")

Turn verbs into nouns for a more academic tone: expand → expansion, pollute → pollution, develop → development

8. Inversion for Emphasis

Not only does regular exercise improve physical health, but it also enhances cognitive function and mental wellbeing.

Use: Not only... but also..., Rarely..., Never..., Seldom..., Under no circumstances...

IELTS Task 1: Key Tips for Band 7

Task 1 requires you to describe visual information (graph, chart, table, map, or process) in at least 150 words. It contributes one-third of your Writing score, so a strong Task 1 can compensate for a weaker Task 2.

1. Identify Key Trends

Focus on the most significant changes: highest/lowest points, steepest increases/decreases, periods of stability, and any notable comparisons. Do not describe every data point.

2. Use Data to Support

Always include specific figures to support your statements. "Sales rose significantly" is weak. "Sales rose from approximately 25,000 units in 2010 to just over 60,000 in 2020" is strong.

3. Appropriate Tenses

Use past tense for completed time periods, present tense for current/universal truths, and future tense for predictions. Mixed time frames require careful tense management.

4. Vary Your Language

Avoid repeating "increased" and "decreased." Use: rose, grew, climbed, surged, soared, plummeted, declined, dropped, fell, levelled off, remained stable, fluctuated.

5. No Personal Opinion

Unlike Task 2, Task 1 is purely descriptive. Never give your opinion, speculate on reasons, or suggest solutions. Simply report what the data shows.

6. Structure: Overview First

Write an overview after the introduction that summarises 2-3 main trends. Examiners prioritise this paragraph. Without a clear overview, you cannot score Band 6+ for Task Achievement.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to improve from Band 5 to Band 7 in IELTS Writing?
With consistent daily practice (45-60 minutes), most candidates see improvement within 4-8 weeks. The key is targeted practice: identifying your specific weaknesses and addressing them systematically rather than simply writing more essays.
Is it possible to get Band 7 with occasional grammar mistakes?
Yes. Band 7 allows for "occasional errors" in grammar and vocabulary. The key is that errors should not obscure meaning and should be interspersed with generally accurate and varied language use. A mix of complex and simple sentences with good control is the target.
What is the most important criterion for Band 7?
Task Response (for Task 2) is the most heavily weighted criterion. You must answer all parts of the question, present a clear position, and support your ideas with relevant, extended arguments. No amount of good grammar can compensate for an off-topic essay.
Should I use complex vocabulary to get Band 7?
Not at the expense of accuracy. Band 7 requires "a sufficient range of vocabulary" including some less common items, but these must be used accurately and appropriately. Using sophisticated words incorrectly will lower your score. Precision matters more than complexity.
Can I use personal examples in Task 2?
Yes, personal examples are acceptable, especially if they effectively illustrate your point. However, more general or academic examples (referencing studies, countries, or well-known facts) often carry more weight and appear more sophisticated.
How many paragraphs should a Band 7 essay have?
A standard four-paragraph structure works well: Introduction, Body Paragraph 1, Body Paragraph 2, Conclusion. Some candidates use five paragraphs (splitting one body paragraph into two) for more complex essays. What matters most is logical organisation and clear progression.
Do IELTS examiners prefer British or American English?
Both are accepted equally. The key is consistency: do not mix British and American spelling (e.g., "colour" and "center" in the same essay). Choose one style and stick to it throughout.
