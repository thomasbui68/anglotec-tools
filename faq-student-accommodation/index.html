Finding suitable accommodation is one of the most important — and often most stressful — aspects of preparing to study in the UK. For international students arriving from abroad, navigating an unfamiliar housing market, understanding rental contracts, and budgeting for living costs can be challenging. These 20 frequently asked questions provide comprehensive guidance on every aspect of UK student accommodation, helping you make informed decisions and avoid common pitfalls.

UK Student Accommodation Quick Guide 2026

Halls: £120-£400/week | Private shared: £90-£280/week | PBSAs: £140-£450/week | Deposit: up to 5 weeks' rent | Bills: £50-100/month extra in private | Apply: March-August for Sept entry | Guarantor often required for private | 20hr/week work allowed on student visa

UK students can choose from several accommodation types: University halls of residence (catered or self-catered, typically on or near campus), private purpose-built student accommodation (PBSA) (modern buildings with en-suite rooms and shared kitchens), private rented houses/flats (shared with other students, arranged through landlords or letting agents), and homestay (living with a host family, less common for university students but available through some institutions). Each type offers different benefits in terms of cost, independence, social life, and convenience.

University halls are owned and managed by the university, offering guaranteed places for first-year students, all-inclusive bills, on-site support staff, and easy socialising with other students. They are typically located on or very near campus. Private accommodation includes purpose-built student flats and private rentals, offering more choice in location and room type, often with better facilities, but requiring more self-management. Private accommodation may be cheaper or more expensive than halls depending on location and quality. Private rentals require dealing directly with landlords or agents.

Student accommodation costs vary significantly by location and type. In 2026, typical weekly rents are: University halls (shared bathroom): £120-£180 outside London, £200-£300 in London. University halls (en-suite): £150-£220 outside London, £250-£400 in London. Private purpose-built: £140-£250 outside London, £280-£450 in London. Private shared houses: £90-£150 outside London, £180-£280 in London. Budget at least £600-£1,200 per month for accommodation depending on your city and preferences.

Most UK universities guarantee accommodation for international students in their first year, provided you apply by the specified deadline (usually between June and August for September entry). This guarantee is a significant advantage, removing the stress of finding accommodation from abroad. After the first year, you typically need to find private accommodation as university halls priority goes to new students. Always check your specific university's accommodation guarantee policy and deadline.

Apply for accommodation as soon as you accept your university offer. For September entry, applications typically open in March-May. Popular options fill quickly, so early application increases your chances of getting your preferred accommodation. If you have a conditional offer, you can usually apply for accommodation before your place is confirmed, though the offer may be conditional on you achieving your grades. Do not wait until you receive your visa — apply for accommodation in parallel with your visa application.

University accommodation rent typically includes: the room (furnished with bed, desk, wardrobe, and sometimes shelving), utilities (gas, electricity, water), Wi-Fi internet, contents insurance for your room, and access to communal facilities (kitchen, laundry, common rooms). Some halls include a meal plan (catered halls), while others provide kitchen facilities only (self-catered). Check exactly what is included before signing, as some universities charge separately for certain services.

Yes, most accommodation requires a deposit (also called a damage deposit or security deposit) before you move in. For university halls, this is typically £200-£500. For private rentals, deposits are capped at 5 weeks' rent under UK law (for annual rents under £50,000). Your deposit must be protected in a government-approved deposit protection scheme if you are renting privately. University halls deposits are held by the university under their own policies. Deposits are returned at the end of your tenancy minus any deductions for damages beyond fair wear and tear.

A tenancy agreement is your legal contract with the accommodation provider. Before signing, check: the contract length and start/end dates, the rent amount and payment schedule, what is included in the rent, the deposit amount and protection scheme, rules about guests and visitors, cancellation policy, and your responsibilities versus the landlord's. For private rentals, check who manages repairs, whether you can give notice early, and any restrictions on decorating or changes. Never sign a contract you do not understand — universities offer contract-checking services for students.

Most university accommodation contracts include a visa refusal clause that allows you to cancel without penalty if your student visa is refused. You typically need to provide evidence of the refusal (the refusal letter from UKVI) within a specified timeframe. Private accommodation cancellation policies vary — some purpose-built student providers offer similar protection, but private landlord contracts may not. Always check the cancellation policy before signing and consider whether you need accommodation before your visa is confirmed.

A guarantor is someone (usually a parent or family member) who agrees to pay your rent if you cannot. Most private landlords and some purpose-built student providers require UK-based guarantors. International students often struggle to provide UK guarantors. Solutions include: paying rent upfront for the full contract period (if the provider allows), using a guarantor service (which charges a fee), asking your university to act as guarantor (some offer this service), or finding accommodation providers that do not require guarantors for international students. University halls typically do not require guarantors.

For first-year international students, university halls are generally recommended. Benefits include: guaranteed place, no need to find accommodation from abroad, instant community of other students, on-site support if you have problems, all-inclusive bills so you can budget easily, and proximity to campus so you can learn your way around. Private accommodation suits more independent students or those who want specific amenities. If you choose private accommodation for first year, ensure you can view it virtually and verify the landlord's legitimacy before paying any money.

To find private accommodation: start with your university's accommodation office which maintains lists of approved private providers. Use reputable student accommodation websites such as Rightmove, Zoopla, SpareRoom, and student-specific platforms like StudentPad or UniHomes. Join your university's official Facebook groups where students advertise rooms. Visit properties in person before signing if possible, or request a virtual tour. Never send money before viewing or verifying the property. Check that landlords of shared properties have the correct HMO licence ( Houses in Multiple Occupation) if required in your area.

Pros: Shared houses are usually the cheapest option, offer more independence and space than halls, allow you to choose your housemates, and provide a more 'adult' living experience. Cons: You must manage bills (or arrange inclusive rent), deal directly with landlords or agents, handle maintenance issues yourself, and navigate shared responsibilities for cleaning and buying essentials. Disputes between housemates are common. For international students, shared houses in second or third year are popular after gaining UK living experience in halls during first year.

University halls rooms are typically furnished with a bed, mattress, desk, chair, wardrobe, and bookshelves. You should bring: bedding (duvet, pillows, sheets — check bed size, usually single or small double), towels, kitchen equipment (if self-catered: plates, cutlery, pots, pans, though some flatmates may coordinate shared purchases), toiletries, clothes for all seasons (UK weather varies), laptop and study materials, adapter plugs (UK uses 3-pin type G plugs at 230V), and any personal items that make you feel at home. Many items can be purchased affordably in the UK after arrival — do not overpack.

In university halls, bills are usually included in your rent. In private accommodation, you need to arrange and pay for utilities separately unless your rent is 'bills included'. Utilities include: electricity, gas (if applicable), water, broadband internet, and a TV licence if you watch live television. As of 2026, expect to pay £50-£100 per person per month for utilities in a shared house, though energy costs have been volatile. Setting up a joint bank account or using a bill-splitting app with housemates ensures fair payment.

Student tenants in the UK have significant legal rights including: the right to a safe, habitable property that meets health and safety standards, protection from unfair eviction (landlords must follow legal procedures), the right to have your deposit protected in a government scheme, the right to quiet enjoyment of the property, the right to have repairs done promptly, and protection from unfair rent increases during the contract period. If your landlord violates these rights, contact your university accommodation service, Citizens Advice, or Shelter for free guidance.

A joint tenancy means all tenants sign the same contract and are jointly responsible for the rent and property condition. If one tenant does not pay, the others must cover their share. An individual tenancy means each tenant has a separate contract with the landlord, typically for a room in a shared house, with responsibility only for your own rent and room. Individual tenancies offer more protection but may be more expensive. Joint tenancies are common for groups of friends renting whole houses together.

Yes, student visa holders can work up to 20 hours per week during term time and full-time during official vacation periods. Many students use part-time earnings to supplement their living costs, including accommodation. Typical student jobs (retail, hospitality, tutoring) pay £10-£12 per hour. Working 15 hours per week at £11/hour earns approximately £165 weekly or £660 monthly, which can cover a significant portion of accommodation costs. Ensure your work does not interfere with your studies, as maintaining your student visa requires satisfactory academic progress.

If you encounter accommodation problems: contact your university accommodation office first — they provide support for both hall residents and students in private accommodation. For disputes with private landlords, contact Citizens Advice (free, confidential advice), Shelter (housing charity with expert advice), or your students' union which may have housing advisers. For serious issues such as harassment or unsafe conditions, contact the local council's housing team or, in emergencies, the police. Your university has a duty of care to ensure you have safe, suitable accommodation.

Choosing the Right Accommodation

The best accommodation choice depends on your priorities, budget, and personality. If you value convenience, community, and support, university halls are ideal for your first year. If you want independence and lower costs, private shared housing suits you better. If you prioritise modern facilities and en-suite bathrooms, purpose-built student accommodation offers hotel-style living at a premium. Whatever you choose, apply early, read contracts carefully, and never send money before verifying the property and landlord. Your university accommodation office is your best resource — use their expertise throughout your search.
