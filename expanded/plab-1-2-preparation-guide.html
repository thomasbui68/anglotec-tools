What is the PLAB Exam?

The Professional and Linguistic Assessments Board (PLAB) exam is the assessment that international medical graduates (IMGs) must pass to demonstrate they have the necessary knowledge and skills to practise medicine safely in the United Kingdom. Administered by the General Medical Council (GMC), the PLAB exam ensures that doctors trained outside the UK meet the same standards as UK medical graduates.

The PLAB exam consists of two parts:

  • PLAB 1 - A written multiple-choice exam testing your clinical knowledge
  • PLAB 2 - A practical Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE) testing your clinical and communication skills

Passing both parts of the PLAB exam is essential for any international doctor who wants to apply for full registration with the GMC and work as a doctor in the UK. The exam is designed to test what a UK doctor who has completed their Foundation Year 1 training would know and be able to do.

Important: Before taking PLAB, you must have your primary medical qualification verified by the GMC and demonstrate English language proficiency through IELTS (minimum 7.0 overall, 7.0 in each band) or OET (minimum Grade B in all sections).

PLAB 1: Overview & Format

PLAB 1 is a computer-based, multiple-choice exam consisting of 180 single best answer (SBA) questions. You have 3 hours to complete the exam. Each question presents a clinical scenario followed by five possible answers, and you must select the single best answer.

The exam is held four times a year at various centres around the world, including multiple locations in the UK, India, Pakistan, Nigeria, Egypt, Bangladesh, and Sri Lanka. This international availability makes it accessible to doctors from most regions without requiring travel to the UK.

The questions in PLAB 1 cover the entire breadth of medical practice you would encounter during FY1 training. They test not only your factual medical knowledge but also your clinical reasoning, decision-making, and ability to apply knowledge in practical scenarios.

PLAB 1 Syllabus & Topics

The PLAB 1 syllabus is broad and reflects the competencies expected of a Foundation Year 1 doctor in the NHS. The exam covers the following major areas:

Core Clinical Topics

  • Cardiology - Chest pain, heart failure, arrhythmias, hypertension, acute coronary syndromes, ECG interpretation
  • Respiratory Medicine - Asthma, COPD, pneumonia, pleural effusion, pulmonary embolism, lung cancer
  • Gastroenterology - Abdominal pain, liver disease, inflammatory bowel disease, GI bleeding, acute pancreatitis
  • Neurology - Headache, stroke, seizures, dizziness, weakness, altered consciousness
  • Endocrinology - Diabetes management, thyroid disorders, adrenal disease, electrolyte imbalances
  • Renal Medicine - Acute kidney injury, chronic kidney disease, electrolyte disturbances, acid-base disorders
  • Infectious Diseases - Sepsis, meningitis, HIV, TB, tropical infections, antimicrobial stewardship

Other Important Areas

  • Emergency Medicine - Trauma, burns, poisoning, anaphylaxis, acute abdomen
  • Surgery - Pre and post-operative care, surgical emergencies, wound management
  • Obstetrics & Gynaecology - Antenatal care, emergencies in pregnancy, contraception, menstrual disorders
  • Paediatrics - Common childhood illnesses, developmental milestones, neonatal jaundice, childhood vaccinations
  • Psychiatry - Depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, suicide risk assessment, capacity assessment
  • Ophthalmology - Red eye, acute vision loss, diabetes eye screening
  • Dermatology - Common rashes, skin infections, eczema, psoriasis, skin cancer
  • ENT - Ear infections, hearing loss, epistaxis, sore throat, sinusitis
  • Orthopaedics - Fractures, back pain, joint pain, osteoporosis
  • Haematology & Oncology - Anaemia, coagulation disorders, common cancers, transfusion
  • Clinical Pharmacology - Drug interactions, prescribing safely, adverse drug reactions
  • Medical Ethics & Law - Consent, confidentiality, mental capacity, end-of-life care, GMC guidance

The exam places significant emphasis on conditions you would commonly encounter as an FY1 doctor working in acute medical or surgical settings. Common and important conditions are tested more frequently than rare diseases.

PLAB 1 Study Plan (12 Weeks)

A structured 12-week study plan is ideal for most candidates preparing for PLAB 1. This allows sufficient time to cover all topics while also building revision and practice into your schedule.

Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

  • Cover all major specialties systematically
  • Use a comprehensive question bank daily (50-100 questions)
  • Review explanations thoroughly, even for correct answers
  • Focus on: Medicine, Surgery, Emergency Medicine
  • Create condensed notes for rapid revision later

Phase 2: Consolidation (Weeks 5-8)

  • Complete remaining specialties: Obs/Gynae, Paeds, Psychiatry
  • Increase question bank practice to 100-150 questions daily
  • Identify weak areas from question bank analytics
  • Study GMC guidance and ethical frameworks
  • Begin timed practice sessions

Phase 3: Intensive Revision (Weeks 9-10)

  • Focus exclusively on weak areas identified earlier
  • Complete full mock exams under timed conditions
  • Revise condensed notes daily
  • Review all previously incorrect questions
  • Memorise key guidelines and protocols

Phase 4: Final Preparation (Weeks 11-12)

  • Take at least 3 full-length timed mock exams
  • Score consistently above 65% in mocks
  • Light revision only - avoid new topics
  • Focus on exam technique and time management
  • Ensure good sleep and rest before exam day

Most successful candidates study for 4-6 hours per day during the preparation period. Consistency is more important than intensity - regular daily study yields better results than sporadic cramming sessions.

PLAB 2: Overview & Format

PLAB 2 is an Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE) that tests your clinical and communication skills in a practical setting. It is a 16-station circuit held at the GMC's assessment centre in Manchester, UK. Each station lasts 8 minutes, with 2 minutes of reading time before entering the station.

Of the 16 stations, 2 are rest stations where no assessment takes place, leaving 14 scored stations. To pass PLAB 2, you must achieve a satisfactory score across the stations, demonstrating competency in both clinical knowledge and practical skills.

PLAB 2 is widely considered the more challenging part of the PLAB exam for many international doctors, not because the clinical content is difficult, but because the OSCE format requires specific skills that may be unfamiliar to doctors trained in different healthcare systems.

PLAB 2 OSCE Station Types

PLAB 2 stations are designed to simulate the real situations you would encounter as a Foundation Year 1 doctor in the NHS. Understanding the different station types is essential for targeted preparation.

1. History Taking Stations

These stations assess your ability to take a focused, structured history from a patient or their relative. You must demonstrate effective communication skills, ask relevant questions, and show empathy. Common scenarios include chest pain, abdominal pain, headache, shortness of breath, and trauma history.

2. Examination Stations

You will be asked to perform a physical examination on a simulated patient (actor) or manikin. Examinations commonly tested include cardiovascular, respiratory, abdominal, neurological (upper and lower limb cranial nerves), thyroid, and joint examinations.

3. Communication & Counselling Stations

These stations test your ability to explain medical information clearly, break bad news, discuss treatment options, obtain consent, and handle difficult conversations. Scenarios may include explaining a diagnosis of cancer, discussing end-of-life care, or addressing a medication error.

4. Practical Procedure Stations

You must demonstrate competency in common practical procedures such as venepuncture, cannulation, blood cultures, urinalysis, peak flow measurement, basic life support (BLS), and suturing.

5. Prescribing Stations

These stations test your prescribing skills, including writing drug charts, identifying prescribing errors, completing TTO (to take out) prescriptions, and demonstrating safe prescribing practices.

6. Ethics & Professionalism Stations

Scenarios involving ethical dilemmas, confidentiality issues, capacity assessment, and professional conduct. You must demonstrate understanding of GMC guidance and apply it to realistic situations.

7. Emergency Management Stations

Simulated emergency scenarios requiring rapid assessment and management, including anaphylaxis, acute asthma, sepsis, diabetic emergencies, and basic life support.

8. Data Interpretation Stations

You will be presented with clinical data such as blood results, ECGs, chest X-rays, or CT scans and asked to interpret the findings and formulate an appropriate management plan.

Key Skills for PLAB 2

Success in PLAB 2 depends on mastering several key skill areas:

Communication Skills

Communication is the single most important factor in PLAB 2. The examiners are assessing how you interact with patients, not just what medical knowledge you possess. Key communication skills include:

  • Active listening - Paying full attention to the patient, acknowledging their concerns
  • Empathy - Demonstrating understanding of the patient's feelings and situation
  • Clear explanations - Using plain English, avoiding jargon, checking understanding
  • ICE framework - Routinely exploring Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations
  • Structure - Opening, gathering information, explaining, involving the patient, closing

Clinical Examination Technique

For examination stations, you must demonstrate a logical, systematic approach while maintaining patient comfort and dignity:

  • Introduce yourself and explain what you are doing
  • Ensure adequate exposure while preserving dignity
  • Follow a systematic routine for each examination
  • Look before you touch - inspection is key
  • Comment on positive and relevant negative findings
  • Summarise findings and suggest a management plan

Time Management

With only 8 minutes per station, managing your time effectively is crucial. Practice completing history takings and examinations within the time limit. In communication stations, ensure you address all key points while still allowing the patient to speak.

OSCE Practice Strategies

Effective OSCE practice requires a structured approach. Here are proven strategies used by successful candidates:

1. Practise with Study Partners

Find one or more study partners preparing for the same exam. Take turns playing the doctor and the patient/examiner. Give each other honest, constructive feedback. Practise regularly - ideally daily in the weeks leading up to the exam.

2. Use Simulated Patients

If possible, practise with trained simulated patients who can provide professional feedback on your communication skills. Some PLAB 2 courses offer this as part of their programme.

3. Record Yourself

Recording your practice sessions allows you to review your performance critically. Pay attention to body language, eye contact, clarity of explanations, and use of filler words like "um" and "ah".

4. Practise Under Timed Conditions

Always practise with a timer. Use 2 minutes of reading time followed by 8 minutes for the station. This helps build your internal clock and ensures you can complete all required tasks within the time limit.

5. Use AI-Powered OSCE Tools

Modern AI platforms can provide realistic OSCE practice with instant feedback. These tools are especially valuable when study partners are not available.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Learning from the mistakes of previous candidates can significantly improve your chances of passing:

  • Poor time management - Running out of time before completing all tasks. Always keep an eye on the clock.
  • Missing the ICE framework - Failing to explore the patient's Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations will cost you marks in every communication station.
  • Using medical jargon - Explaining things in technical language that patients cannot understand. Always use plain English.
  • Inadequate hand washing - Forgetting to wash hands before and after examination stations. This is a critical safety issue.
  • Not introducing yourself - Failing to state your name and role at the beginning of each station.
  • Neglecting confidentiality - Discussing patient information inappropriately or failing to maintain privacy.
  • Not gaining consent - Beginning examinations or procedures without explaining and obtaining consent.
  • Poor closing - Ending stations abruptly without summarising, checking understanding, or outlining next steps.
  • Over-preparing knowledge, under-preparing skills - PLAB 2 tests skills, not knowledge. Communication practice is more important than reading textbooks.

Exam Day Tips

Being well-prepared for exam day can make a significant difference to your performance:

  • Arrive early - Allow plenty of time to find the venue and settle in. Manchester can be busy and traffic unpredictable.
  • Dress professionally - Wear smart professional attire as you would in a clinical setting. Ensure your clothes are comfortable.
  • Bring required documents - Check the GMC's requirements for identification and admission documents.
  • Use the rest stations - The two rest stations are your opportunity to breathe, reset, and mentally prepare for the next stations.
  • Read the instructions carefully - Use the 2-minute reading time to fully understand what the station requires.
  • Don't dwell on mistakes - If a station goes badly, move on mentally. Performance in one station does not affect others.
  • Treat every station as a fresh start - Enter each station with confidence and professionalism.
  • Be nice to everyone - Be courteous to all staff, simulated patients, and other candidates throughout the day.

After Passing PLAB: Next Steps

Congratulations! Once you have passed both parts of the PLAB exam, you can apply for full registration with the GMC. The next steps are:

  1. Apply for GMC Registration - Submit your application through the GMC online portal with your PLAB certificates and supporting documents.
  2. Complete ID Check - Attend an identity check at the GMC offices or arrange a remote verification.
  3. Apply for Jobs - Start applying for non-training FY1 or FY2 posts through NHS Jobs or individual trusts.
  4. Get Your NHS Number - You will need an NHS employer to issue your performers list number.
  5. Consider Core Training - After gaining FY experience, you can apply for Core Training programmes in your chosen specialty.

Many international doctors also choose to sit the MRCP or other postgraduate exams alongside or after completing PLAB to accelerate their career progression in the UK.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to prepare for PLAB 1?

Most candidates need 3-4 months of dedicated study to prepare for PLAB 1, though this varies depending on your clinical experience and how recently you completed medical school. A structured 12-week study plan works well for most doctors.

Can I take PLAB 2 before PLAB 1?

No, you must pass PLAB 1 before you can book PLAB 2. Your PLAB 1 result is valid for 3 years, giving you ample time to prepare for and pass PLAB 2.

How many times can I attempt PLAB?

You can attempt PLAB 1 a maximum of 4 times. For PLAB 2, you also have a maximum of 4 attempts. If you exhaust your attempts, you must wait 12 months before reapplying.

Is PLAB 2 harder than PLAB 1?

PLAB 2 is generally considered more challenging because it tests practical and communication skills rather than just knowledge. Many international doctors are less familiar with the OSCE format, which requires a different type of preparation.

Do I need to attend a PLAB 2 course?

While not mandatory, many candidates find PLAB 2 courses beneficial for structured practice and feedback. Online AI-powered OSCE tools can provide supplementary practice at a fraction of the cost.

Can I work in the UK without PLAB?

Some doctors may be exempt from PLAB through postgraduate qualifications like MRCP(UK), MRCS, or GMC-approved sponsorship schemes. However, most international medical graduates must pass PLAB to gain GMC registration.

What is the pass rate for PLAB?

PLAB 1 pass rates vary by sitting but are typically around 60-70%. PLAB 2 pass rates are generally higher at 70-75%. Thorough preparation significantly improves your chances of passing both exams.

How much does the PLAB exam cost in total?

As of 2025, PLAB 1 costs £247 and PLAB 2 costs £913, giving a total of £1,160. Additional costs include travel to Manchester for PLAB 2, study materials, and potentially preparation courses.
