Quick Overview

If you need to prove your English proficiency for university admission, visa applications, or professional registration, you will most likely choose between TOEFL (Test of English as a Foreign Language) and IELTS (International English Language Testing System). These are the two most widely accepted English language tests in the world, and understanding their differences is the first step to making the right choice.

Both tests assess your ability in four core language skills: reading, writing, listening, and speaking. Both are accepted by thousands of universities, immigration authorities, and professional bodies worldwide. However, they differ significantly in format, scoring, availability, and the contexts in which each is preferred.

TOEFL iBT

  • Administered by ETS (Educational Testing Service)
  • Primarily American English
  • Fully computer-based (iBT version)
  • Approximately 3 hours long
  • Scored 0-120 points
  • Most popular in USA, Canada
  • Speaking recorded via microphone
  • Multiple-choice focused

IELTS Academic

  • Jointly managed by British Council, IDP, and Cambridge
  • British, Australian, and American English
  • Paper-based or computer-based
  • Approximately 2 hours 45 minutes
  • Scored 0-9 bands
  • Most popular in UK, Australia, Canada
  • Speaking: face-to-face interview
  • Varied question types

Section-by-Section Breakdown

Reading

TOEFL iBT Reading: You will read 3-4 academic passages of approximately 700 words each and answer 30-40 multiple-choice questions. The passages are taken from university-level textbooks. You have 54-72 minutes to complete this section. All questions are multiple-choice, and the vocabulary questions are often considered straightforward.

IELTS Academic Reading: You will read 3 passages of increasing difficulty and answer 40 questions of various types including multiple-choice, True/False/Not Given, matching headings, sentence completion, and short answer questions. You have 60 minutes. The variety of question types and the infamous "Not Given" option make this section challenging for many test-takers.

Listening

TOEFL iBT Listening: You will listen to 3-4 lectures and 2-3 conversations, all in academic settings. Questions are multiple-choice and you have 41-57 minutes. You can take notes and refer to them when answering. The content is entirely academic.

IELTS Listening: You will listen to 4 recordings: a social conversation, a monologue in a social context, a conversation in an educational context, and an academic monologue. You have 30 minutes plus 10 minutes to transfer answers to the answer sheet. Question types include multiple-choice, matching, map/plan/diagram labelling, form completion, and sentence completion.

Writing

TOEFL iBT Writing: Two tasks. Task 1 (Integrated) involves reading a passage, listening to a lecture, and writing a response that summarises the relationship between them (20 minutes, 150-225 words). Task 2 (Independent) is an essay on a given topic expressing your opinion (30 minutes, 300+ words).

IELTS Academic Writing: Two tasks. Task 1 (20 minutes, 150 words) requires describing a graph, chart, table, diagram, or process. Task 2 (40 minutes, 250 words) is an essay responding to a point of view, argument, or problem. Task 2 carries twice the marks of Task 1.

Speaking

TOEFL iBT Speaking: Four tasks completed by speaking into a microphone. Tasks 1 is independent (express an opinion). Tasks 2-4 are integrated (read, listen, then speak). The entire section takes 17 minutes. Your responses are recorded and scored by human raters and AI scoring.

IELTS Speaking: A face-to-face interview with a certified examiner lasting 11-14 minutes. Part 1 (4-5 min) is general questions about yourself. Part 2 (3-4 min) is a long turn where you speak for 2 minutes on a given topic. Part 3 (4-5 min) is a two-way discussion on abstract issues related to Part 2. Many candidates find the human interaction less stressful than speaking to a computer.

For Visa Applications

When it comes to visa applications, the choice between TOEFL and IELTS becomes more clearly defined:

UK Visa

The UK Home Office only accepts IELTS for UKVI (or other approved SELT tests like PTE Academic UKVI, LanguageCert, or Trinity). Standard TOEFL is not accepted for any UK visa application. If you need a UK visa, you must take a SELT test, and IELTS UKVI is the most widely available option.

US Visa

The US does not have a specific English language requirement for most non-immigrant visas. However, if you are applying for a student visa (F-1), you will need to show acceptance at a university, and that university will have English requirements. Both TOEFL and IELTS are accepted by virtually all US universities.

Canadian Immigration

Canada accepts both IELTS (General Training) and TOEFL for Express Entry and other immigration programmes. However, IELTS is the more commonly used test for Canadian immigration purposes, and the scoring system aligns more directly with Canadian Language Benchmarks (CLB).

Australian Visa

Australia accepts IELTS, TOEFL iBT, PTE Academic, and Cambridge tests for most visa categories. IELTS is the most commonly used and widely recognised test for Australian immigration.

Key Takeaway for Visa Applicants

If you need a UK visa, TOEFL is not an option. You must take IELTS for UKVI or another approved SELT. For other countries, both tests are generally acceptable, but IELTS offers broader acceptance across multiple immigration systems simultaneously.

For Professional Registration

Healthcare professionals and other regulated workers often need English certification for professional body registration:

  • General Medical Council (UK doctors) - Accepts IELTS (7.5 overall) and OET. Does NOT accept TOEFL.
  • Nursing and Midwifery Council (UK nurses) - Accepts IELTS (7.0 overall) and OET. Does NOT accept TOEFL.
  • General Dental Council - Accepts IELTS and OET. Does NOT accept TOEFL.
  • Engineering Council - Varies; many engineering bodies accept both TOEFL and IELTS.
  • Teaching Council - Varies by country; IELTS generally preferred in UK, Australia, and Canada.

If you are a healthcare professional seeking to work in the UK, Australia, or Ireland, IELTS or OET are your only viable options. TOEFL is not accepted by any UK healthcare regulator.

Which Test is Harder?

There is no definitive answer to which test is harder, as difficulty depends on your individual strengths and preferences:

You might find TOEFL harder if:

  • You are not comfortable with computer-based testing for extended periods
  • Speaking into a microphone feels unnatural or stressful
  • You struggle with North American accents
  • You prefer varied question types over multiple-choice
  • You are uncomfortable with integrated tasks (combining reading, listening, and writing/speaking)

You might find IELTS harder if:

  • Face-to-face interviews make you nervous
  • You find True/False/Not Given questions confusing
  • Writing Task 1 (graph description) is unfamiliar
  • You prefer American English spelling and vocabulary
  • You are not used to managing your own time across sections

In general, candidates who are more comfortable with academic American English and computer-based testing tend to perform better on TOEFL. Candidates who prefer varied question types, human interaction, and exposure to multiple English accents often prefer IELTS.

Our Recommendation

Our Verdict

For most international applicants in 2025, we recommend IELTS.

IELTS offers broader global acceptance, is accepted for UK visas (which TOEFL is not), is preferred by healthcare regulators, and the face-to-face speaking test is often less stressful than speaking to a computer.

Choose IELTS if you:

• Need a UK, Australian, or Canadian visa
• Are a healthcare professional
• Prefer human interaction
• Want the option of paper-based testing
• Are applying to universities in multiple countries

Choose TOEFL if you:

• Are applying exclusively to US universities
• Prefer computer-based testing
• Are comfortable speaking into a microphone
• Prefer multiple-choice questions
• Want results in under 10 days

Frequently Asked Questions

Can universities see how many times I have taken TOEFL or IELTS?

No. You choose which score reports to send to institutions. They will only see the scores you send them. There is no penalty for taking the test multiple times.

Can I combine scores from multiple test sittings?

Generally, no. Most institutions require all scores from a single test date. Some UK healthcare bodies (NMC) allow IELTS score combining across two tests under specific conditions.

How long are TOEFL and IELTS scores valid?

Both TOEFL and IELTS scores are valid for 2 years from the test date. After this period, you will need to retake the test if required.

Can I switch from TOEFL to IELTS preparation easily?

Yes. The underlying English skills are the same. You will need to familiarise yourself with the different test format, question types, and timing, but your English proficiency carries over directly.

Which test has more test dates available?

TOEFL iBT typically offers more test dates per year (60+) compared to IELTS (48 paper-based dates). However, computer-delivered IELTS is increasingly available with multiple dates per week.

Is TOEFL or IELTS better for a non-native speaker of English?

Neither test is inherently easier. It depends on your personal strengths, learning style, and the context in which you learned English. Try practice questions from both tests to see which format feels more comfortable.
