History of the 6 C's

The 6 C's of Nursing were introduced in December 2012 by England's Chief Nursing Officer, Jane Cummings, as part of the Compassion in Practice strategy. This national strategy was developed in response to the Francis Inquiry into failings at Mid Staffordshire NHS Foundation Trust, which revealed catastrophic lapses in the quality of nursing care.

The 6 C's were designed to provide a universal framework that all nursing staff could understand, remember, and apply in their daily practice. They represent the core values and behaviours that patients and the public have the right to expect from nursing professionals.

Since their introduction, the 6 C's have been adopted across the NHS and have influenced nursing education, recruitment, appraisal, and regulatory standards. They are now embedded in the NMC Code and form the basis of many nursing interviews and performance reviews.

Why the 6 C's Were Created

  • - Response to failures in compassionate care at Mid Staffs
  • - To give nursing a clear, memorable values framework
  • - To align NHS nursing practice across all settings
  • - To restore public confidence in the nursing profession
  • - To guide nursing education and professional development

All 6 C's Defined

Here is a quick-reference summary of all six values. Each is explored in depth in its dedicated section below.

1. Care

Delivering care that is safe, effective, and puts patients at the centre of everything. Treating patients with dignity and respecting their individual needs.

2. Compassion

Showing kindness, empathy, and understanding. Recognising patients as human beings with feelings, families, and lives beyond their illness.

3. Competence

Having the knowledge, skills, and expertise to deliver high-quality care. Understanding one's own limitations and seeking development.

4. Communication

Listening carefully, sharing information clearly, and building therapeutic relationships through effective verbal and non-verbal communication.

5. Courage

Doing the right thing even when it is difficult. Speaking up about poor practice and advocating for patients despite personal risk.

6. Commitment

Dedication to patients, the profession, and continuous improvement. Going above and beyond to ensure the best possible outcomes.

Care: The Foundation of Nursing

Care is the first and most fundamental of the 6 C's. It means delivering care that is safe, effective, and of the highest possible quality. Care is about treating every patient with dignity, respecting their individuality, and putting them at the centre of all decision-making.

In practice, delivering care means ensuring that basic needs are met: patients are clean, comfortable, hydrated, nourished, and pain-free. It means following evidence-based practice, maintaining safety standards, and continuously evaluating the quality of care provided.

Examples of Care in Practice

Example 1: A nurse notices that a patient with dementia hasn't eaten their lunch. Instead of simply recording "meal refused," the nurse sits with the patient, offers different foods, provides gentle encouragement, and ensures adequate nutrition.
Example 2: A nurse performing a pressure area risk assessment identifies a patient at high risk and immediately implements a turning schedule, special mattress, and regular skin inspections, preventing a pressure ulcer from developing.
Example 3: A nurse ensures a patient nearing end of life receives adequate pain relief, regular mouth care, and peaceful surroundings, involving the family in care decisions.

Key Behaviours: Care

  • ✓ Prioritising patient comfort and safety
  • ✓ Meeting fundamental needs (nutrition, hydration, hygiene)
  • ✓ Following evidence-based practice
  • ✓ Risk assessment and prevention
  • ✓ Individualised care planning
  • ✓ Respecting patient choices and preferences

Compassion: The Human Connection

Compassion is about how care is delivered. It involves recognising patients as whole human beings with emotions, relationships, and lives beyond their illness. Compassionate care is intelligent kindness that is founded on empathy, respect, and dignity.

Research has shown that compassionate care improves patient outcomes, increases patient satisfaction, reduces anxiety, and even accelerates physical recovery. Conversely, the absence of compassion - as seen at Mid Staffs - causes profound harm.

Examples of Compassion in Practice

Example 1: A nurse holds the hand of a frightened patient before surgery, acknowledges their fear, and stays with them until the anaesthetic takes effect, providing comfort through physical presence.
Example 2: A nurse working night shift takes time to sit with a lonely elderly patient who cannot sleep, listening to their stories about their late spouse, providing emotional comfort.
Example 3: After a stillbirth, a nurse creates memory items for the parents - handprints, photographs, a lock of hair - treating them with extraordinary gentleness and acknowledging their profound loss.

Key Behaviours: Compassion

  • ✓ Showing empathy and understanding
  • ✓ Being present and attentive
  • ✓ Recognising emotional needs
  • ✓ Treating patients with dignity and respect
  • ✓ Responding to distress with warmth
  • ✓ Going beyond the clinical task

Competence: Clinical Excellence

Competence means having the knowledge, skills, judgement, and expertise to deliver high-quality care. It involves understanding one's own limitations, maintaining professional development, and applying evidence-based practice to clinical decisions.

Competence is not just about technical skills. It includes clinical reasoning, decision-making, leadership, and the ability to work effectively within multidisciplinary teams. A competent nurse knows when to seek help and how to escalate concerns appropriately.

Examples of Competence in Practice

Example 1: A nurse recognises the early signs of sepsis in a post-operative patient, performs a full set of observations, calculates a NEWS2 score of 7, and escalates to the medical team using SBAR within minutes, potentially saving the patient's life.
Example 2: A nurse completing their revalidation identifies a gap in their knowledge around catheter care, undertakes additional training, updates the ward protocol, and teaches colleagues the improved technique.
Example 3: When a patient's condition deteriorates beyond their training, a student nurse appropriately seeks supervision rather than attempting interventions independently, demonstrating awareness of their scope of practice.

Key Behaviours: Competence

  • ✓ Maintaining up-to-date knowledge and skills
  • ✓ Applying evidence-based practice
  • ✓ Recognising own limitations
  • ✓ Appropriate escalation of concerns
  • ✓ Continuing professional development
  • ✓ Clinical reasoning and decision-making

Communication: The Bridge to Trust

Communication in nursing encompasses all interactions: with patients, families, colleagues, and other healthcare professionals. It involves listening actively, sharing information clearly and honestly, and building therapeutic relationships based on trust.

Poor communication is one of the most common causes of patient harm and complaints. Effective communication prevents errors, improves adherence to treatment, reduces anxiety, and ensures coordinated care across the multidisciplinary team.

Examples of Communication in Practice

Example 1: A nurse uses the "teach-back" method after explaining discharge medication: "Mr. Ahmed, so that I'm sure I've explained this clearly, can you tell me when you'll take your tablets?" This confirms understanding and prevents medication errors.
Example 2: During handover, a nurse uses SBAR to communicate a patient's deteriorating condition clearly and concisely, ensuring the incoming team has all essential information to provide safe care.
Example 3: A nurse recognises that a patient with learning disabilities processes information slowly. They use simple language, visual aids, and involve the patient's advocate to ensure informed consent is truly informed.

Key Behaviours: Communication

  • ✓ Active listening without interruption
  • ✓ Clear, jargon-free explanations
  • ✓ Checking understanding
  • ✓ Effective handover and documentation
  • ✓ Adapting style to patient needs
  • ✓ Honest, timely information sharing

Courage: Doing the Right Thing

Courage in nursing means doing the right thing even when it is difficult, uncomfortable, or carries personal risk. It involves speaking up about poor practice, advocating for patients who cannot advocate for themselves, and challenging decisions that may not be in patients' best interests.

The Francis Report highlighted that many staff at Mid Staffs knew care was inadequate but failed to speak up. Courage is the C that prevents such failures. It requires moral strength, professional confidence, and support from organisational cultures that welcome raising concerns.

Examples of Courage in Practice

Example 1: A newly qualified nurse notices that an experienced colleague is consistently rough when handling patients. Despite feeling intimidated, the nurse reports their observations to the ward manager through the appropriate channels.
Example 2: A nurse challenges a doctor's discharge plan when they believe the patient is not medically fit to go home, providing evidence from observations and patient assessment to support their concern.
Example 3: A nurse advocates for a patient with limited English to have a professional interpreter rather than relying on family members, ensuring accurate communication about a serious diagnosis.

Key Behaviours: Courage

  • ✓ Speaking up about poor practice
  • ✓ Advocating for patients
  • ✓ Challenging unsafe decisions
  • ✓ Whistleblowing when necessary
  • ✓ Admitting and learning from mistakes
  • ✓ Supporting colleagues who raise concerns

Commitment: Dedication to Excellence

Commitment is the dedication to patients, the nursing profession, and continuous improvement. It means going above and beyond basic requirements to ensure the best possible outcomes for those in your care. Committed nurses take ownership of their practice and drive positive change.

Commitment also involves contributing to the wider nursing community: mentoring students, participating in quality improvement projects, and helping to shape the future of the profession. It is the sustained dedication that turns values into consistent practice.

Examples of Commitment in Practice

Example 1: A nurse notices that patients on their ward consistently miss medication doses because of timing issues. They conduct an audit, present findings to management, and implement a new medication round schedule that improves compliance by 40%.
Example 2: A nurse commits to staying 30 minutes after their shift to support a newly qualified colleague through their first patient death, providing emotional support and debriefing.
Example 3: A nurse volunteers to become a dementia champion, undertaking specialist training and then sharing their knowledge with the whole team, transforming the ward's approach to dementia care.

Key Behaviours: Commitment

  • ✓ Taking ownership of care quality
  • ✓ Contributing to quality improvement
  • ✓ Mentoring and supporting others
  • ✓ Keeping knowledge and skills current
  • ✓ Advocating for the profession
  • ✓ Sustained dedication despite challenges

6 C's in Practice: Scenario Comparison

The following table shows how all 6 C's apply to a single clinical scenario:

Scenario: A nurse caring for a patient with advanced dementia who has refused to eat for two days.

CHow It's Demonstrated
CarePerforms a full assessment, checks for swallowing difficulties, oral problems, and pain. Ensures basic needs are met.
CompassionSits with the patient, offers favourite foods gently, shows patience when food is refused, maintains dignity.
CompetenceRecognises signs of aspiration risk, involves speech and language therapy, follows evidenced dysphagia management.
CommunicationSpeaks gently and slowly, uses non-verbal cues, involves family in understanding preferences, documents accurately.
CourageChallenges the dietitian's generic plan when it's unsuitable, speaks up to the doctor about potential safeguarding concerns.
CommitmentStays beyond shift to feed the patient, researches alternative strategies, shares learning with the team.

Using the 6 C's in Interviews

NHS nursing interviews frequently ask about the 6 C's. Here are common questions and how to answer them using the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result):

Q: "Tell us about a time you demonstrated compassion in your nursing practice."

"In my previous role, I was caring for a patient who had received a terminal cancer diagnosis. She was withdrawn and refused to engage with the team. I made a point of sitting with her during every medication round, not rushing, and simply being present. Over several days, she began to open up about her fears for her children. I arranged for our Macmillan nurse to visit and facilitated a family meeting where she could express her wishes. By the end of the week, she had completed an advance care plan and appeared more at peace."

Q: "Describe a situation where you showed courage as a nurse."

"I noticed that a senior colleague was consistently signing off medication rounds they hadn't actually witnessed. I was concerned about patient safety and the legal implications. I documented specific instances and raised my concerns with the ward manager through our local raising concerns policy. An investigation was conducted, additional training was provided, and the ward implemented a new double-checking system. It was uncomfortable, but patient safety had to come first."

Q: "How do you ensure you maintain competence in your practice?"

"I take responsibility for my own professional development. I complete all mandatory training before deadlines, attend relevant study days, and read nursing journals regularly. I recently identified that my knowledge of diabetic ketoacidosis management needed refreshing, so I completed an online module and arranged a practical session with our diabetes specialist nurse. I also keep a reflective portfolio and use feedback from appraisals to set development goals."

Why the 6 C's Matter to Patients

Research and patient feedback consistently show that the 6 C's directly impact patient experience and outcomes:

Patient surveys consistently rank compassion and communication as the most important aspects of care, often above clinical outcomes.
Compassionate care has been linked to reduced patient anxiety, lower pain scores, shorter hospital stays, and improved adherence to treatment.
Effective communication prevents medical errors. The WHO estimates that communication failures contribute to approximately 70% of sentinel events.
Courageous cultures where staff feel safe to speak up reduce mortality rates. Hospitals with strong safety cultures have significantly lower rates of preventable harm.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are the 6 C's still relevant today?

Absolutely. They remain a core framework in the NHS Long Term Plan, NMC Code, and most NHS trust values. They are regularly cited in CQC inspection reports and nursing education.

Do the 6 C's apply to all nursing roles?

Yes. Whether you work in hospitals, community settings, mental health, research, or management, all 6 C's are relevant. They are values, not clinical tasks.

How do I demonstrate the 6 C's as a newly qualified nurse?

Focus on the fundamentals: compassionate care (C), clear communication (C), commitment to learning (C), and courage to ask questions (C). Competence (C) builds over time through experience and supervision.

Are there any additional C's?

Some organisations have added additional values, but the original 6 C's remain the national framework. The NMC Code incorporates all six within its four themes.

How should I prepare to discuss the 6 C's in an interview?

Prepare 2-3 STAR examples for each C from your own experience. Be specific about what YOU did and what the outcome was. Avoid vague statements like "I always show compassion."

What's the difference between Care and Compassion?

Care is WHAT you do (the clinical actions, meeting needs). Compassion is HOW you do it (the emotional quality, empathy, warmth). You can deliver care without compassion, but the best nursing combines both.
