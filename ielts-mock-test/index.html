Passage 1 — The History of Tea

Tea is the most widely consumed beverage in the world after water. Its origins can be traced back to ancient China, where legend has it that Emperor Shen Nung discovered tea in 2737 BC when leaves from a wild tree blew into his pot of boiling water. For centuries, tea was consumed primarily for its medicinal properties rather than as a daily refreshment.

The spread of tea beyond China began during the Tang Dynasty (618-907 AD), when tea became China's national drink and Buddhist monks introduced it to Japan. By the 16th century, Portuguese priests and merchants had encountered tea during their travels to the East and brought samples back to Europe. However, it was the Dutch who first shipped commercial quantities of tea to Europe in 1610, establishing it as a luxury item among the aristocracy.

The British East India Company began importing tea to England in the late 17th century. Initially, it was so expensive that only the wealthy could afford it. As import volumes increased and prices fell, tea became accessible to the middle classes. By the 18th century, tea had become Britain's national beverage, a status it maintains today. The British passion for tea led to the establishment of tea plantations in India and Ceylon (now Sri Lanka) in the 19th century, breaking China's monopoly on tea production.

Today, tea is grown in over 60 countries worldwide. The major tea-producing nations include China, India, Kenya, and Sri Lanka. There are six main varieties of tea — white, yellow, green, oolong, black, and post-fermented — all derived from the Camellia sinensis plant. The differences between them arise from the level of oxidation the leaves undergo during processing.

Passage 2 — The Science of Sleep

Sleep is a fundamental biological process that remains one of the most intriguing mysteries of human physiology. Despite spending approximately one-third of our lives sleeping, scientists are still uncovering the precise mechanisms and purposes of this seemingly passive state. What is clear, however, is that sleep is essential for physical health, cognitive function, and emotional wellbeing.

The sleep-wake cycle is regulated by two main systems: the circadian rhythm and sleep homeostasis. The circadian rhythm is an internal 24-hour clock that responds primarily to light and darkness, controlling when we feel alert and when we feel sleepy. Sleep homeostasis, often referred to as "sleep drive," builds up during waking hours and dissipates during sleep. These two systems work together to determine the timing, duration, and quality of sleep.

During sleep, the brain cycles through several distinct stages. Non-REM sleep consists of three stages, ranging from light sleep to deep slow-wave sleep. This is followed by REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep, during which most dreaming occurs. A complete cycle lasts approximately 90 minutes, and a healthy adult typically experiences four to six cycles per night. Deep sleep is particularly important for physical restoration and memory consolidation, while REM sleep plays a crucial role in emotional regulation and creative problem-solving.

Chronic sleep deprivation has been linked to numerous health problems, including obesity, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and weakened immune function. Cognitive effects include impaired attention, reduced decision-making capacity, and slower reaction times. Alarmingly, studies have shown that being awake for 17-19 hours produces cognitive impairment equivalent to a blood alcohol concentration of 0.05%. Despite these risks, modern lifestyles often prioritise productivity over rest, with surveys indicating that up to 35% of adults in developed countries fail to get the recommended 7-9 hours of sleep per night.

Passage 3 — Renewable Energy: Challenges and Opportunities

The global transition to renewable energy is one of the defining challenges of the 21st century. As the world grapples with the escalating climate crisis, the shift away from fossil fuels toward sustainable energy sources has become an urgent priority. However, this transition is far from straightforward, involving complex technical, economic, and political considerations.

Solar and wind energy have emerged as the frontrunners in the renewable revolution. The cost of solar photovoltaic panels has dropped by over 90% since 2010, making it the cheapest source of new electricity generation in most parts of the world. Wind energy has seen similar cost reductions, with both onshore and offshore wind farms becoming increasingly efficient. In 2023, renewable sources accounted for nearly 30% of global electricity generation, up from just 20% in 2015.

Despite these advances, significant challenges remain. Energy storage is perhaps the most critical bottleneck. Solar and wind power are intermittent — the sun does not always shine, and the wind does not always blow. Current battery technology, while improving rapidly, cannot yet store electricity at the scale and duration needed to ensure grid stability. Lithium-ion batteries, the current standard, face limitations in capacity, lifespan, and the environmental impact of lithium mining.

Grid infrastructure presents another major hurdle. Existing electricity grids were designed for centralised power plants running on fossil fuels. Integrating distributed renewable sources requires substantial upgrades to transmission and distribution systems. Smart grid technologies, which use digital communication to optimise electricity flow, offer promising solutions but require massive capital investment.

The political landscape adds further complexity. While most nations have committed to net-zero emissions targets, the pace of implementation varies enormously. Developing countries, which are often most vulnerable to climate change, frequently lack the financial resources to invest in renewable infrastructure. International climate finance mechanisms have fallen short of their promised targets, leaving a significant funding gap.

What Is the IELTS Reading Test?

The International English Language Testing System (IELTS) is the world's most popular English language proficiency test, accepted by over 12,000 organisations worldwide. The Reading section is one of four components, alongside Listening, Writing, and Speaking.

The Academic Reading test consists of three long passages taken from books, journals, magazines, and newspapers. These texts range from descriptive and factual to discursive and analytical. The passages are authentic and intended for a non-specialist audience, though they may contain technical vocabulary appropriate to an academic context.

The 40 questions test a wide range of reading skills, including:

  • Reading for gist, main ideas, and detail
  • Understanding inferences and implied meaning
  • Recognising a writer's opinions, attitudes, and purpose
  • Following the development of an argument
  • Identifying specific information quickly (skimming and scanning)

Top Tips for IELTS Reading Success

1. Skim Before You Scan

Spend 1-2 minutes skimming each passage for the main idea before looking at the questions. This gives you a mental map of where information is located.

2. Read the Questions First

For most question types, reading the questions before the passage helps you know what to look for, saving valuable time.

3. Watch the Time

Spend approximately 15 minutes on Passage 1, 20 on Passage 2, and 25 on Passage 3. Do not spend too long on one question.

4. Transfer Answers Carefully

You get no marks for spelling errors. Check your spelling, grammar, and word limits (e.g., "NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS").

5. Do Not Leave Blanks

There is no negative marking in IELTS. If you are unsure, make your best guess — you might be right!

6. Practise Regularly

Consistency is key. Take a free IELTS practice test online at least once a week to build speed and accuracy.
