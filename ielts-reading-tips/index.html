Understanding the IELTS Reading Format

The IELTS Reading test consists of 40 questions based on 3 passages, and you have exactly 60 minutes to complete them. There is no extra time for transferring answers — in the paper-based test, you must write all answers on the answer sheet before time expires.

For IELTS Academic, the three passages are taken from books, journals, magazines, and newspapers. They progress in difficulty — Passage 1 is the easiest, Passage 3 the most challenging. Topics are of general academic interest and accessible to candidates entering undergraduate or postgraduate courses.

For IELTS General Training, Section 1 contains two or three short factual texts, Section 2 contains two short work-related texts, and Section 3 contains one longer text on a topic of general interest. The General Training test is generally considered easier, but the scoring is adjusted accordingly.

Master Skimming and Scanning: The Foundation of Speed

The biggest difference between Band 6 and Band 8 readers isn't vocabulary — it's reading speed combined with accuracy. Skimming and scanning are the two core techniques that enable this.

Skimming: Getting the Gist in 2 Minutes

Skimming means reading quickly to get the general idea of a passage. Before you look at any questions, spend 2 minutes skimming the entire passage. Read the title, subtitle, first paragraph, topic sentence of each paragraph, and the conclusion.

While skimming, ask yourself: What is this passage about? What is the writer's main argument? How is the passage organised? This mental map helps you locate answers faster when you start the questions.

Scanning: Finding Specific Information

Scanning means looking for specific words, numbers, or phrases without reading everything. Use scanning for questions that ask you to find a specific detail — matching names to statements, finding dates, or locating particular information.

When scanning, use your finger or cursor to guide your eyes down the page in a zigzag pattern. Look for keywords from the question or their synonyms. Names, dates, and numbers are easiest to spot — use them as anchors.

The Combined Approach

Band 8+ scorers use both techniques in combination: skim first for the overall structure, then scan to find specific answers. They never read the entire passage word-for-word before starting questions. This approach alone can save you 10-15 minutes.

Don't Do This: Reading the entire passage carefully before looking at questions is the single biggest time-waster. You won't remember the details when you need them, and you'll end up re-reading multiple times.

True/False/Not Given: The Hardest Question Type Mastered

This is consistently the question type where candidates lose the most marks. The difference between False and Not Given is subtle but learnable.

The Decision Framework

TRUE/YES: The statement agrees with the information in the passage. The passage confirms the statement, possibly using different words (paraphrase).

FALSE/NO: The statement contradicts the information in the passage. The passage explicitly says something different from the statement.

NOT GIVEN: The passage does not mention this information at all, or the information is insufficient to determine the answer.

How to Tell False from Not Given

This is where most candidates struggle. The key question is: Does the passage say anything about this topic?

If the passage discusses the topic and says something that contradicts the statement → FALSE.

If the passage doesn't mention the topic at all, or mentions it but doesn't address the specific claim → NOT GIVEN.

Pro Tip: If you find yourself spending more than 90 seconds on a T/F/NG question, mark your best guess and move on. These questions should average 60 seconds each. Spending too long on one question jeopardises your ability to complete the entire test.

Practice Method

Take 20 T/F/NG questions and categorise your errors. Most candidates find they confuse False with Not Given, or they overthink and create connections that don't exist. Drill these questions specifically until you achieve 85%+ accuracy.

Matching Questions: Strategies for Every Type

Matching questions test your ability to connect information across the passage. There are four sub-types, each requiring a different approach.

Matching Headings

This question type lists headings (a-f or i-x) and asks you to match them to paragraphs. The key insight: headings summarise the main idea, not details.

Strategy: Read each paragraph first, identify the main idea, then look for a heading that captures that idea. Don't match based on single words — a heading might share a word with the paragraph but mean something different. Cross off headings as you use them to avoid repetition.

Matching Information to Paragraphs

Here you're given statements and must identify which paragraph contains the relevant information. The statements are usually paraphrased — the exact words won't appear.

Strategy: Skim each statement for keywords, then scan the passage for those keywords or synonyms. NB: You can use any paragraph letter more than once (the instructions will tell you if you can use a letter more than once).

Matching Features

You match features (e.g., researchers' names, dates, theories) to statements. Find every mention of each feature in the passage, then match to the correct statement.

Matching Sentence Endings

Complete sentences by matching beginnings to endings. Check that the completed sentence is grammatically correct and matches the passage meaning.

Gap-Fill Question Strategies

Completion questions (sentence, summary, note, table, diagram, short answer) are often the easiest question types. They test your ability to locate and extract specific information.

The Golden Rules

1. Respect the word limit. If the instruction says "NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS," writing three words makes the answer wrong, even if the content is correct.

2. Use words from the passage. The answer is always in the text. Don't change word forms or use synonyms unless the grammar requires it.

3. Check grammar. Your answer must fit grammatically into the sentence. If it doesn't, you've probably got the wrong answer or the wrong form.

4. Hyphenated words count as one word. "Well-known" is one word. Numbers written as digits count as one word.

Quick Win: Gap-fill questions should be your highest-scoring question type. If you're losing marks here, it's usually due to not following instructions (word limits) or not checking grammar. These are easy fixes that can add 2-3 band points.

Time Management: The Band 8+ Difference

Time management separates good readers from great readers. You have 60 minutes for 40 questions across 3 passages — that's 1.5 minutes per question, plus reading time.

Time-Saving Techniques

Do easy questions first. Within each passage, start with the question types you find easiest. This builds confidence and secures marks early.

Don't get stuck. If you can't find an answer after 90 seconds, make an educated guess, mark it, and move on. Come back only if you have time at the end.

Transfer answers as you go. In the paper-based test, write answers directly on the answer sheet. There is no extra transfer time, and trying to transfer 40 answers in the final 5 minutes is a recipe for disaster.

Use a watch. You cannot rely on being able to see the clock in the exam room. Bring a simple watch (not a smartwatch) and check it after each passage.

Building the Vocabulary You Need for Band 8+

Vocabulary is tested indirectly in IELTS Reading. You need to understand paraphrases, synonyms, and academic language to locate and comprehend answers.

Academic Word List (AWL)

Study the Academic Word List — 570 word families that appear frequently in academic texts. Focus on the most common sublists (1-5). Learn not just the word but also its common collocations and synonyms.

Topic Vocabulary

IELTS Reading passages cover recurring topics: environmental science, psychology, education, history, technology, and medicine. Build topic-specific vocabulary by reading articles from sources like New Scientist, National Geographic, and The Economist.

Synonym Recognition

Questions rarely use the same words as the passage. Train yourself to recognise synonyms and paraphrases. When you read an answer in the passage, ask yourself: "How else could this be said?" This skill is crucial for all question types.

The Practice Routine That Takes You to Band 8+

Random practice doesn't work. You need a structured routine that targets your weaknesses and builds exam skills systematically.

Weekly Practice Structure

Monday-Tuesday: Focused question-type practice. Choose one question type you're weak on and complete 20+ questions. Analyse every error.

Wednesday-Thursday: Full passage practice. Complete one full passage (all question types) under timed conditions. Review thoroughly.

Friday: Vocabulary day. Learn new words, review old ones, and practise paraphrasing.

Saturday: Full mock test. Complete all 3 passages in 60 minutes. Simulate exam conditions strictly.

Sunday: Review and rest. Analyse Saturday's mock test. Identify patterns in your errors. Plan next week's focus.

How to Review Practice Tests

Completing practice tests without review is wasted time. For every wrong answer, ask: Why did I get this wrong? Was it a vocabulary problem (I didn't understand the text or question)? A strategy problem (I used the wrong approach)? A careless mistake (I misread the question)? A time problem (I rushed)? Track your error types — after 5 tests, patterns will emerge that show you exactly what to work on.

✓

Frequently Asked Questions About IELTS Reading

How can I improve my IELTS Reading speed?

Speed comes from technique, not just practice. Master skimming (2-minute overview) and scanning (finding specific information). Stop reading every word. Use your finger to guide your eyes. Time every practice session. Most candidates can gain 5-10 minutes through technique improvements alone.

What is the best way to practice IELTS Reading at home?

Use official Cambridge IELTS books (1-18+) for authentic practice material. Follow a structured weekly plan: focused question-type practice on weekdays, full timed tests on weekends. Always review errors thoroughly. Supplement with academic reading from New Scientist, The Economist, or BBC Science.

Is IELTS Academic Reading harder than General Training?

Academic Reading uses more complex texts with more advanced vocabulary. However, General Training has easier texts but requires more correct answers for the same band score. For example, Band 7 in Academic requires 30 correct answers; in General Training, it requires 34. The difficulty is roughly equivalent when accounting for the scoring adjustment.

How many hours should I study for IELTS Reading?

For a one-band improvement (e.g., 6 to 7), most candidates need 60-80 hours of focused study. For a two-band improvement (e.g., 6 to 8), expect 120-160 hours. Quality matters more than quantity — 10 hours of focused, error-analysis-driven study beats 30 hours of passive practice.

Can I use a highlighter in the IELTS Reading test?

In the paper-based test, you can use a pencil to underline or circle text on the question booklet. You cannot use a highlighter. In the computer-based test, you can use the highlighting tool. Mark key information as you read — it saves re-reading time.

What should I read to prepare for IELTS Reading?

Read academic articles from scientific publications (New Scientist, Scientific American), quality newspapers (The Guardian, BBC), and magazines (The Economist, National Geographic). Focus on topics that commonly appear: environment, technology, psychology, education, and health. Read for main ideas and structural organisation, not just content.

Why am I stuck at Band 6.5 in Reading?

The Band 6.5 to 7+ jump typically requires three improvements: (1) mastering T/F/NG questions, (2) faster skimming/scanning to complete all questions in time, and (3) expanded vocabulary for understanding paraphrases. Analyse your last three practice tests to identify which of these is your main barrier.
