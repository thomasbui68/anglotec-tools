The OET Writing sub-test requires you to write a healthcare-related letter based on case notes provided. You have 45 minutes to complete it. The letter type and content are specific to your healthcare profession.
The most common letter types are referral letters, transfer letters, discharge letters, and letters to advise or inform other healthcare professionals, patients, or organisations.
There is no strict word count, but a good OET letter is typically between 180 and 220 words. Quality, clarity, and relevance matter more than length. Focus on including all essential information concisely.
OET Writing is scored on six criteria: Purpose, Content, Conciseness and Clarity, Genre and Style, Organisation and Layout, and Language. Each criterion is assessed on a scale from 0 to 7.
Most regulatory bodies require a Grade B (score of 350 or above) in the Writing sub-test. This is equivalent to an IELTS band 7.0. Some bodies may accept a Grade C+ in Writing if other sub-tests are strong.
The purpose statement is a clear opening sentence that explains why you are writing the letter. It should be specific, concise, and appear in the first paragraph. It is one of the most important scoring elements.
No. You must be selective. Include only information relevant to the recipient and the purpose of the letter. Irrelevant details demonstrate poor clinical judgement and will reduce your score.
A standard OET referral letter includes: date, recipient address, subject line, salutation, purpose statement, relevant patient details, relevant medical history, current situation, request or action needed, and a professional closing.
You can use standard, widely accepted medical abbreviations that the recipient would understand. Avoid uncommon abbreviations or ones that could cause confusion. When in doubt, write the full term.
Common mistakes include: including irrelevant information, missing the purpose statement, poor organisation, using informal language, making spelling errors, exceeding time limits, and not proofreading before submission.
Spelling and grammar are important but not the only factors. Minor errors that do not impede communication are acceptable. However, frequent or serious errors that affect clarity will lower your language score.
Always use professional, formal language appropriate for healthcare communication. The tone should reflect communication between healthcare professionals. Avoid contractions, slang, or overly casual expressions.
A recommended approach: 5 minutes reading and selecting notes, 30 minutes writing the letter, and 10 minutes reviewing and editing. Good time management is crucial for a polished final letter.
A referral letter requests another professional to take over or contribute to patient care. A transfer letter accompanies a patient being moved to another facility and focuses on continuity of care.
You can learn structural frameworks for different letter types, but you must not memorise and repeat fixed templates. Examiners penalise template-heavy responses that do not address the specific task.
Nurses should: practise with realistic case notes, study sample letters, focus on selecting relevant information, master the purpose statement, improve medical vocabulary, and practise writing under timed conditions.
No, the OET Writing task is profession-specific. Nurses write nursing-related letters, doctors write medical letters, dentists write dental letters, and so on. The case notes reflect real scenarios from each profession.
The subject line should clearly state the patient’s name, age, and the purpose of the letter. For example: “Re: Mr John Smith, 45 years old. Referral for ongoing wound management.”

If time is running short, prioritise completing the letter with a purpose statement, key patient details, and a clear closing. A complete but brief letter scores better than an unfinished detailed one. Practise timing to avoid this situation.
